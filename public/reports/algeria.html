Algeria: Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation

Daniel Edwan

 

Abstract: Analysis of the Algerian civil war (1991-2006), known in Algeria as the “national tragedy,” for the purpose of gaining insights into reintegration models is limited at the national policy level. On September 2005, the Algerian people overwhelmingly approved President Bouteflika’s proposed Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation, which granted amnesty for all individuals, whether Islamists, civilian militia, or national security forces, from prosecution for crimes committed during the war with a few exceptions. Despite its critics, the Charter provided a legal framework that began a national reintegration process that depended on current institutions to implement. Following the Charter, the Algerian government applied revised and new de-radicalization policies and strategies in order to suppress further Islamist radicals.[119] The net effects of these approaches over time successfully ended the civil war and significantly reduced violence and threats of terrorism. The two primary elements of the Algerian reintegration model are: first, comprehensive national amnesty followed by the regional distribution of the reintegration process to existing institutions; second, the implementation of de-radicalization policies across the country’s religious infrastructure.

 

Insurgency Description

            From 1992-2006, Algeria was locked in a protracted brutal civil war in which armed Islamic radical groups, pro-regime militias, and government security forces fought for power. The Islamist insurgency consisted of four distinct groups: the Armed Islamic Movement (MIA); the Islamic Salvation Army, AIS, (the self_declared armed wing of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS) political apparatus); the Armed Islamic Group (GIA); and, later, the Salafi Group for Preaching and Combat (GSPC). Although all groups launched violent campaigns against government forces, they appeared at different times and varied in their ideology and motives.[120] The MIA and AIS were moderate and targeted government forces for limited objectives to restore the FIS and validate the elections. The GIA and GSPC (jihadist Salafist organizations), with true intentions of establishing a caliphate, attacked government forces and civilians not loyal to the Islamists.[121] The period between 1995 and 1998 was characterized by a series of massacres directed against civilians in rural and isolated communities. The massacres were intended to terrorize the population that was hostile to the Islamists, or those who formerly supported the Islamist cause but who had withdrawn their support.

            The members of Islamist radical groups were generally classified as “those ill-treated by the security forces who were looking for revenge; and the ideologically driven devout activists or self_proclaimed ‘defenders of Islam.’”[122] Many young men were the product of poverty, boredom and repression, and engaging in the ‘jihad’ was a way of gaining wealth and prestige in their community. The war was a period of tremendous brutality, but for many it also offered chances to progress in society and improve their livelihoods.

 

Causal Elements

            Algeria’s brutal civil war (1991-2006), in which an estimated 200,000 were killed, was the result of three things: an inadequate post-French colonial power structures, a secular authoritarian regime, and the rise of Islamic radicalism. Characterized by a history of political violence, Algerian conflicts “evolved from resistance against the French occupation during the independence struggle (1954-1962), to small_scale violence against state personnel during the 1980s, to a full scale war against the Algerian state and civilians during the 1990s.”[123] Since gaining independence from France in 1962, the National Liberation Front (FLN), formed around a core of military elites, has remained in power as an authoritarian regime, in which pluralism and opposition were not tolerated. The Algerian national identity is both Arab and Muslim, in which latent ideological tensions between Arab secularism and Islamic Fundamentalism would slowly surface. Islam was reduced to ‘official Islam.’ Meanwhile, Islam in the public sphere was virtually abolished, encouraging the development of extremist religious narratives.[124]

            Violent resistance emerged for the first time in the 1980s, the decade during which “militant Islam in Algeria was promoted by a new post_independence generation, who rebelled against the FLN’s monopoly of power.”[125] To this generation of Muslims under the age of twenty-five, “political Islam was an attempt to restore the values that they advocated: social justice; the redistribution of political power; and the ‘threatened’ Islamic identity.” [126]  Contributing to these factors was social inequality and poor economic growth in which 72 percent under the age of twenty-five were unemployed.[127] The deteriorating socio-economic circumstances culminated in widespread protests and a series of violent attacks on government offices that were violently suppressed by security forces. In 1988, as an effort control further Islamic radicalization and stabilize the country, President Benjedid supported political reforms to the Constitution “based on three principles: a) the separation of power between the state and the FLN, which would function as an independent political party; b) free participation in municipal and legislative elections; and c) more room for associations and social movements to express their ideas.”[128] The FLN’s attempt to appease the Islamic movement underestimated the size, scale and mobilization of its political power that would backfire with the rise of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS).

            The FIS founded shortly after the constitutional amendment in 1989 called for a return to the values of equal opportunity, justice and accountability. Mobilizing its political message of “Neither National Charter nor Constitution: Islam is the Solution,” through an urban network of mosques and social services, the FIS won “188 of the 231 seats in the first round of the general elections held in December 1991, compared with only eighteen seats won by the governing party, the FLN.”[129] In January 1992, the FLN military’s Higher State Council seized power, declared a state of emergency, compelled President Benjedid to resign and cancelled the elections. The FIS was dissolved and the military detained over 18,000 former FIS members and suspected Islamic sympathizers without trial in concentration camps.[130] The FLN justified it actions to supporters by stating the FIS would not uphold democratic principles because “Islam and democracy are incompatible.”[131] The FLN’s hostile actions and egregious hypocrisy of justifying their actions to uphold democracy while suppressing the Constitutional mandate for free and public elections was the tipping point of the Algerian civil war.

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

The Algerian military armed civilians and encouraged the organization of self-defense militias in order to expand government control.[132] The Algerian government mobilized all civilian agencies in the execution of the civil war.

 

International Agency Involvement

The IMF, US, France and UN Funds provided outside material support to the government during the conflict.

 

NGO Involvement

            NGOs were marginalized during the conflict and their support was not government sponsored.

Criteria for Reintegration

            Reintegration after the civil war was based on a foundational principle of national amnesty and included a provision of exemption from prosecution for crimes committed during the war -- including Islamists, militia, or security forces.

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

            In 2005, the Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation was proposed as a public referendum. The prevailing idea was for the country to look forward without declaring winners or losers. Of note, the regime would remain blameless for the conflict.[133] “According to government figures the charter was supported by 97 percent of the voters in a popular referendum with a turnout of 82 percent.”[134]

Key measures of the Charter included:

_      Measures ending state prosecution against individuals implicated in instances of terrorism;

_      Reduced or commuted sentences for individuals condemned and not eligible to release;

_      Care for the families of individuals in the battle against terrorism;

_      Exclusion of individual who committed rapes, set off explosives in public places, or carried out assassinations from the benefit of those measures.[135]

            Besides the exemption of individuals, whether Islamists, militia, or security forces, from prosecution for crimes committed during the war, it also provided harsh punitive measures against those who attempt to discredit the regime or security force involvement.[136] Specifically anyone that attempts to “undermine the good reputation of its agents,” or “tarnish the reputation of Algeria internationally, shall be punished.”[137] These measures remain in effect to the present day. Unsurprisingly, these legal parameters significantly affect government transparency and limit international agency efforts to investigate human rights abuses and provide support. Specifically, the Algerian government announced that the names of Islamist terrorists or the reports of rape or massacres would not be made public.[138]

 

Community Engagement

            Another significant aspect of the Charter was the issue of the “disappeared,” or the victims of terrorism. This amplified growing skepticism about the regime’s desire and capacity to facilitate a transparent reconciliation and reintegration. Families of the disappeared formed associations “demanding information on the fate of their loved ones and insisting that truth must precede reconciliation.”[139] An Algerian National commission was formed, uncommon due to the government's use of existing ministerial structure, to adjudicate the claims of the disappeared. After four years of deliberation, the vast majority of the 13,541 applications were denied due the regime’s claims of a national state of self-defense and the admission of “no records, no witnesses, the court cases would never lead to convictions,” and it would be “impossible to settle all the unresolved cases in court.”[140] These comments present the stark reality that the Algerian judicial system was incapable of conducting a fair trial for the thousands involved. Algerian amnesty, therefore, appears to be the result of a limited bureaucratic capacity as opposed to an expansive desire for forgiveness.

 

Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

The Charter provided the legal framework for the government to pursue an underlying strategy to “isolate the mainstream Islamists from the violent ideologists, and to reintegrate them in society.”[141] Additionally it provided a means for an acceptable amnesty for the regime and its security forces. The FLN elite were faced with the dilemma of vindicating their adverse actions of 1992 against the FIS. In addition, the FLN elite were creating a publically accepted revised and more pervasive set of laws and policies to suppress dissent over their conduct of the civil war. The regime was not only successful at achieving this goal, but it found a means in the counter-terrorism doctrine to remain a viable international partner, despite significant human rights skepticism from many aid organizations.[142]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

            Available research materials indicate that the Algerian government did not constitute additional reintegration infrastructure such as special designed camps to provide ex-combatants employment, education, and income support. The Charter only provided the means for ex-combatants to regain their civil liberties. Otherwise in pursuit of subverting Islamist radicalism, national measures in the form of de-radicalization strategies were implemented. 

Government de-radicalization strategies were designed to support a management of religious affairs through the propagation of a moderate, less politicized form of Islamic teachings. The Algerian government employed five noteworthy techniques: (1) The government widely publicized a former radical leader’s testimony to convince to jihadists to reintegrate; (2) The government sponsored hundreds of external Muslim preachers to Algeria to convey a moderate non-violent version of Islam;[143] (3) The government heavily monitored sermons, religious school curricula, and appointed imams as civil servants through the Ministry of Religious Affairs; (4) The government consulted FLN elite and scholars for strategies that promote a peaceful and tolerant Islamic teachings; and (5) The government sponsored a non-political variant of Islamic Salafism as a means to undermine radical Salafism.[144]

 

Cost Estimate

            The secrecy of Algerian government financial distributions and their outright claim that the conflict was a civil war make the study of Algerian community reintegration models unfeasible. Cost estimates reference Algeria’s reintegration program are negligible since the government used a national reconciliation process using existing governmental institutions.

 

Financing

Armed groups collected taxes in areas under their control. In other areas, armed group extorted businesses and established roadside checkpoints. There were significant links across borders to transnational and organized criminal organizations. The government was on the verge of bankruptcy in 1993 and utilized debt rescheduling and the World Bank’s Structural Adjustment Program (SAP) to reverse its financial situation. This made the necessary funds available to defeat armed groups.[145]

 

Challenges

The most significant challenge was on the Algerian regime itself. The idea of national reconciliation negotiated by many of the same military leaders that started the conflict and the attempts at resolving human rights atrocities perpetuated an adverse international image of the country. There are similar strategic challenges for the Nigerian government, in that, the Nigerian military with numerous documented human rights abuses is the government entity responsible for Operation Safe Corridor. This controversial arrangement could result in Nigerian adverse international image.   

The Charter provided the regime renewed authority to suppress Islamic radical political parties from assembly and participation. Specifically, Article 26 of the Charter “excludes from political life anyone who committed acts of terrorism or manipulated Islam for political purposes.”[146] The suppression of political rights is contrary to democratic processes and will likely serve as a future grievance.

The use of local militias in the Algerian civil war, declared as ‘Patriots’ by executive decree created a set of problems and human rights abuses. In some cases, militias were involved in assassinations, extortion and the use of excessive force.[147]

Although national reconciliation was declared, the lack of implementing guidance down to the local level (especially regarding employment) created significant issues among former militants who were denied jobs and passports. The idea that a blanket policy of reconciliation would permeate to the local level after over a decade of conflict proved illusory.[148]

 

Successes

            The Charter proved to be an effective end of the civil war, in which the Algerian government claims that an estimate of six thousand former combatants, not including security forces, utilized the amnesty program.[149] The Algerian Ministry of Foreign Affairs claimed the following: “Thanks to this policy, thousands of repented agreed to surrender of their own free will, thereby allowing the Nation to spare hundreds or even thousands of lives, as well as to enable the return and reintegration into society of a large number of repented.” [150] Despite significant international criticism, sustained violence in Algeria has diminished since 2006 indicating that the country was able to move forward. Significant residual grievances have not yet threatened stability.

 

Similarities to Boko Haram

There are numerous contextual similarities between the Nigerian Boko Haram and the Algerian civil war. The most fundamental similarity is that both conflicts contain an Islamist radicalization element in competition with a secular regime. Due to this, Algeria’s de-radicalization strategies could provide implementation value to Nigeria’s reintegration process. In particular, Nigeria could publicize former Boko Haram leader’s testimony. This de-radicalization strategy could result in increased community support and convince to jihadists to reintegrate. Additionally, Nigeria may benefit sponsoring external Muslim imams to preach a moderate non-violent version of Islam impacting a larger aspect of the radicalization environment.

In both conflicts, there are extensive alleged governmental security force human right violations. Additionally, both conflicts appear to be characterized by the lack of judicial infrastructure and/or prosecutable evidence. Due to these similarities, Nigeria may ultimately require a similarly comprehensive national reconciliation effort that is supported both internally and externally. Internal support enables resolution of grievances among all parties, while external support translates into government legitimacy and international funding streams. Additionally, both Nigerian and Algerian governments empowered armed civilian militias. Lessons from Algeria indicate that a government-sponsored civilian force left unaccountable may act in accordance with their own interests rather than the government's. This paper proposes a comprehensive set of transitional justice methods to achieve reconciliation for the Boko Haram conflict. Even though these methods differ from Algeria’s blanket amnesty approach, the idea of creating an internally and externally supported transitional justice system is founded on lessons learned from the Algerian civil war.

 

Differences from Boko Haram

             The Algerian regime, although secular, was still steeped in the singularly-dominant Muslim culture. Meanwhile, Nigeria is starkly divided along Muslim and Christian lines (roughly 50-50). The cultural tension between the southern Christians and ruling Nigerian government versus the Islamist areas in northern Nigeria may be a barrier to reintegration. On a positive note, relative to Algeria, the Nigerian government has established limited local reintegration agencies to support policy implementation at the local level. This is important to de-radicalize and integrate the former militant into society, and is preferable to a heavy reliance on current institutions in which reintegration expertise is not centralized or coordinated.
