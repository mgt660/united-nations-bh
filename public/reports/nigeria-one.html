Operation Safe Corridor: Nigeria’s Attempts to Reintegrate Boko Haram

 Justin W. Crocker

 

Abstract: Nigeria’s conflict with Boko Haram, ongoing since 2002 and reaching its peak of violence in 2014-2015, has caused more than 20,000 civilian deaths and the displacement of nearly 2.7 million refugees.[10]  As recent successes by the Nigerian military and Multinational Joint Task Force (MNJTF)[11] continue to reduce much of the area once dominated by Boko Haram, the Nigerian government in Abuja now struggles with ways to manage surrendered or captured insurgents. In September 2015, the Federal Government of Nigeria (FGN) established Operation Safe Corridor (OSC), a program designed to rehabilitate surrendered Boko Haram insurgents and eventually reintegrate them back into Nigerian society.  Specifically, OSC has been designed to counter Boko Haram’s radical interpretation of Islam, address many of the psychological factors faced by insurgents freshly off of the battlefield and provide vocational training that will assist former militants return to their communities as productive citizens.  However, while OSC shows positive steps for the FGN, the program has significant flaws that must be addressed.  The program is only designed for surrendered militants, is currently under resourced, and fails to address many of the human rights abuses perpetrated by Nigerian security forces.  This section will further explore OSC, outline the FGN’s goals for the program, and identify both challenges and potential solutions for OSC. Lastly, this section will evaluate OSC’s suitability as the continued method to rehabilitate and reintegrate members of Boko Haram back into Nigerian society.

 

Background of Boko Haram in Nigeria

            Discovery of massive deposits of commercial-quality oil in the Niger Delta in 1956 quickly propelled Nigeria to a more prominent geopolitical status. Today, while Nigeria’s economy is the largest in Africa and one of the fastest growing in the world, most Nigerians are getting poorer.[12]  According to the data from Nigeria’s National Bureau of Statistics (NBS), poverty rates rose from 42.7 percent in 1992 to 65.6 percent in 1996 to a staggering 69 percent in 2010.[13]  NBS statistics also show that northern Nigeria, the heartland of Boko Haram and rising Islamic fundamentalism, is disproportionately affected by poverty and unemployment.[14]  It was this socio-economic backdrop that prompted Islamic cleric Mohammad Yusuf to establish the Ibn Taimiyyah Masijid (Mosque) in Borno state’s capital of Maiduguri in 2004.[15]  Yusuf, a charismatic and energetic Salafist Muslim cleric, drew a large following by preaching against the oppressive Nigerian government. He blamed western education for corrupting Nigeria’s leaders and advocated for an Islamic society based on Sharia law. Yusuf’s messages resonated profoundly with many northern Muslims. Young, illiterate and unemployed men proved to be particularly susceptible. By 2007, Yusuf had attracted a significant following that included people from neighboring Niger, Chad, and Cameroon.[16] Yusuf and his followers remained relatively quiet until July 2009, when clashes with police over a period of four days resulted in over eight hundred people killed. Among the dead was Yusuf; however, his extrajudicial murder by police (which went viral because it was captured on videotape) became a catalyst for future violence.

            Boko Haram withdrew into the shadows but reemerged in mid-2010 with a much more violent identity under a new leader: Abubakar Shekau. In September 2010, the group conducted a well-planned attack on a prison in Bauchi in northern Nigeria, freeing more than seven hundred inmates, many of whom were Boko Haram members. Seven separate bombings on Christmas Eve in 2010 in Jos—a major city in Nigeria’s central region—killed at least thirty-two churchgoers. The violence continued to escalate and spread throughout Nigeria, and included one of Boko Haram’s first suicide attacks on the UN compound in Abuja in August 2011. The attack claimed twenty-three lives and resulted in another eighty-seven wounded. The group’s kidnapping of 276 girls from a school in Chibok, a remote town approximately seventy miles south of Maiduguri, in April 2014 propelled Boko Haram into international notoriety. The abduction prompted the EU and the UN to place the group on their respective terrorist organization lists. By early 2015, Boko Haram controlled most of Borno state and many areas in neighboring Yobe and Adamawa states in northeast Nigeria.

 

What is Operation Safe Corridor?[17]

            While the program was approved in concept under the Goodluck Jonathan administration in as early as 2013, significant progress establishing OSC -- location, cadre, structure of the deradicalization and trade training -- did not occur until mid to late-2015. Through research in Nigeria and a series of interviews with a member of the OSC staff, we have learned that specific goals of OSC include:

1.     Provide members of Boko Haram with an alternative to fighting.

2.     Assist the FGN in implementing a formalized disarmament, demobilization and reintegration (DDR) program in Northeast Nigeria and the greater Lake Chad Basin.

3.     Provide surrendered Boko Haram insurgents with deradicalization counseling and vocational training, thereby providing Nigeria with a trained and productive labor force.

4.     Assist in ending hostilities in northeast Nigeria.

 

Operation Safe Corridor is physically located in a Nigerian military camp in Borno state’s capital of Maiduguri. Currently, approximately one thousand former Boko Haram militants are said to be in the program, of which approximately eighty percent are male and twenty percent are female. The average age of the participants is said to be approximately thirty years old. OSC consists of a twelve-week de-radicalization and vocational training program involving thirty-seven Nigerian agencies. These agencies include, but are not limited to: Department for Counter Violent Extremist at the Office of the National Security Adviser National Emergency Management Agency (NEMA), National Orientation Agency, National Youth Service Corps, Nigeria Identity Card Management Commission, Federal Ministry of Health, Ministry of Interior, National Directorate of Employment, and Nigeria Prisons Services. While predominantly civilian agencies run the program’s de-radicalization and trade training programs, the Nigerian military controls OSC’s leadership and security mechanisms. Additionally, participation in OSC is reserved for those who surrender to Nigerian authorities. Captured Boko Haram militants--defined here as those who do not physically surrender--are placed in other detention facilities and are currently not eligible for OSC enrollment. We are unaware of exactly how Nigerian security forces distinguish between surrendered militants from those who are captured. Such information was not disclosed during our research in Nigeria nor by our interviewee.

The de-radicalization portion of OSC consists of psychological counseling and religious training. Newly surrendered militants are given initial psychological assessments to determine the extent of treatment required, particularly for younger militants and those who were forcibly conscripted into Boko Haram. Specific psychological counseling methods could not be determined by our research. Interestingly, OSC authorities claim to have incorporated religious leaders and scholars from the international community, notably Pakistan, into religious de-radicalization efforts. Moderate Salafist clerics and leaders perform de-radicalization training by speaking directly with former militants and focus on countering many of the distorted versions of Salafist Islam.[18]  The OSC authority with whom we spoke indicated that the primary component of OSC is religious de-radicalization. However, we did not identify any specific metrics used to initially and then subsequently evaluate participants at the end of the program.

The vocational portion of OSC involves training in predominantly low-skilled trade (e.g., agriculture, wood- and steel-working, as well textile manufacturing). This training is intended to provide former militants with skills necessary to become self-reliant once they are deemed de-radicalized and prepared for reintegration back into Nigerian society. Additionally, vocational training in a useful trade can ease reintegration into otherwise resistant communities. Finally, self-sufficiency theoretically addresses a few of the socio-economic reasons for initially joining Boko Haram.

From what could be discerned, surrendered militants are psychologically evaluated at least twice during their twelve weeks in OSC; once during their initial entry into the program and once at the culmination of the program to determine whether or not they are prepared to reintegrate back into Nigerian society. We could not discern specifically how OSC officials determine whether a former militant is effectively de-radicalized. According to our authority, approximately thirty percent of the militants currently entering OSC are deemed to not be completely de-radicalized after the twelve weeks. Those determined unfit for reintegration are placed back into the program, but it is unclear whether they complete the twelve-week program again or are inserted into a follow-on program on a case-by-case basis. Those who are determined to be prepared for reintegration are sent to unfamiliar communities for a “fresh start” and an opportunity to reestablish themselves. Specific numbers of those who had been effectively rehabilitated and reintegrated through OSC could not be discerned. Each community's willingness to welcome former militants into their society was also unclear.

 

Challenges with Operation Safe Corridor

Although much regarding OSC is still publicly unknown, we were able to discern some of its potential challenges and pitfalls. While most Nigerian civilians understand the merits of a program like OSC, many more have displayed strong resistance to the program. Most northern Nigerians believe that the harms caused by Boko Haram should be addressed before former militants receive treatment and vocational training. Others fear the risk of recidivism to be too high to reintegrate former insurgents back into communities in the near term. How the FGN addresses these concerns will be vital in ensuring OSC or a similar program is successful; without support from those citizens and communities charged with ultimately reintegrating former militants, any such program will undoubtedly fail.

Boko Haram’s insurgency ravaged northeastern Nigeria and dramatically altered the lives of millions of Nigerians. As mentioned earlier, nearly three million Nigerians have been forced from their homes. Many of these Nigerians are forced to live in under-resourced, internally-displaced persons (IDP) camps or “host communities”—essentially shantytowns with extremely limited means of life support—and are draining already scarce resources. Additionally, as more areas of northeast Nigeria become accessible as a result of increased security force operations, a growing humanitarian crisis is emerging. The latest report from the United Nation’s Office for the Coordination of Human Affairs (UNOCHA) estimates that over seven million people are in need of emergency life-saving assistance, of which an estimated 4.4 million remain in unsecure or inaccessible areas due to ongoing violence.[19]  An estimated one million northeast Nigerians suffer from severe acute malnourishment, including 250,000 children under six years of age. Médecins Sans Frontières and UNOCHA officials warn that unless immediate steps are taken to improve security and access in northeast Nigeria, fifty thousand children will likely die.[20]  For many northern Nigerians, these problems created and perpetuated by Boko Haram must be addressed before surrendered militants benefit from a program such as OSC. To do otherwise, in the eyes of many Nigerians, would amount to rewarding former Boko Haram militants for their violence while simultaneously punishing the millions of Nigerians affected by the conflict.

During a discussion among community and government leaders from Borno state in Maiduguri in June 2016, many of the participants expressed concern about the high risk of recidivism among militants deemed de-radicalized by OSC authorities and reintegrated into their communities. Specifically, most were skeptical of OSC’s ability to determine whether a former militant continued to hold radical religious views. Would a former militant subsequently be capable of resisting rejoining Boko Haram or similar violent extremist groups such as Ansaru and Al Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM)? Most participants believed reintegration in the near term was dangerous and called for a ten-year period to allow the population to heal first before reintegration should begin. While the feasibility of a ten-year period is likely unrealistic, the stakeholders’ sentiment and strong resistance to reintegration in the near term displays a strong lack of Nigerian community acceptance of the program. Therefore, we believe that OSC must be implemented in conjunction with similar programs aimed at developing vocational skills and access to jobs for the millions of IDPs affected by Boko Haram. Psychological counseling and rehabilitation must also be afforded to those who have witnessed Boko Haram’s violence and violent security force operations. Understandably, many Nigerians are strongly opposed to Boko Haram and, left unaddressed, can significantly inhibit public acceptance of OSC’s legitimacy. Establishing a rehabilitation and reintegration program in which both former militants and civilians can participate is critical to achieving public support for OSC.

Another challenge of OSC is that, while thirty-seven different civilian agencies are involved, the program is perceived to be a military-run initiative. OSC is commanded by a Nigerian Army Brigadier General, has a predominantly military officer staff, and it is physically located on a military camp guarded by Nigerian soldiers. Nigeria’s military has been widely criticized for its brutality and disregard for human rights, particularly during its fight with Boko Haram. In a highly critical report on Nigerian security force war crimes and human rights violations, Amnesty International documented twenty-seven extrajudicial killings committed by security force members in 2013 and 2014 alone. The report claimed that at least twelve hundred people, mostly men and young boys, had been killed and another seven thousand had died in under-resourced and overcrowded detention facilities.[21] The most notorious of these incidents occurred in March 2014 after Boko Haram attacked a detention facility at Giwa Barracks in Maiduguri and released an estimated seven hundred prisoners. In the days that followed, Nigerian soldiers and members of the Civilian Joint Task Force (CJTF) began house-to-house searches of local residences in an effort to recover the released prisoners. Amnesty International’s investigation presented overwhelming evidence that at least 640 men and boys, most of whom were released during the Giwa Barracks attack, were extra-judicially killed by soldiers and CJTF volunteers.[22]  

Given this history of abuse, it is highly unlikely that many Boko Haram militants will willingly surrender to Nigerian security forces. The benefits of surrender and participation in OSC must outweigh the potential cost of continuing to fight under Boko Haram’s banner. But as long as the Nigerian military is involved in OSC, it will be difficult for militants to willingly surrender. It is important for OSC’s future that military involvement be limited, with the ultimate goal of being completely removed in the near future. As a first step, physical security for OSC’s camp should be transferred to Nigeria’s National Police Force, Civil Defense Corps, or another security agency run and operated by Nigerian civilians. Additionally, the OSC camp should be immediately removed from a military base and transferred to an area deemed acceptable by the new agency responsible for physical security. These changes will immediately and tangibly display Nigeria’s sincere commitment to rehabilitating and ultimately reintegrating surrendered militants back into Nigerian communities. The militants will no longer perceive the OSC as a trap to overcrowded and under-resourced prisons, but as providing a real and sustainable path forward.

In addition to limiting military involvement, OSC officials must also address the legal status of surrendered Boko Haram members and strike the difficult balance between retribution and rehabilitation. Public support for OSC is limited due in large part to northern Nigerians’ demands for retribution for the harms perpetrated by Boko Haram. Amnesty for surrendered militants, regardless of whether their involvement with Boko Haram was forced, is nearly unanimously rejected by Nigerians. Prosecuting thousands of militants individually and fairly, however, will prove difficult due to the lack of clear evidence on individual fighters and the FGN’s limited legal resources. But blanket punishment, regardless of the extent of each militant’s involvement in the insurgency, is also an unsuitable answer as it will undoubtedly decrease the number of those willing to surrender. Many low-level fighters and supporters of Boko Haram, particularly those forced into the group or coerced into directly supporting the group’s activities, may be unwilling to surrender if their punishment is equal to that of mid-level leaders or those who took part in rape, murder, and other war crimes. The FGN must develop a robust method for delivering prosecutorial justice to Boko Haram militants while simultaneously avoiding a homogenous approach that disproportionately alienates low-level fighters and supporters. Without striking this careful balance, both public support and militant willingness to surrender and participate in OSC will likely prove inadequate.

 

Conclusion

Operation Safe Corridor has many egregious flaws that can and must be addressed if the program is to be successful. OSC represents a positive step forward for the FGN, which just two years ago seemed completely incapable of defeating Boko Haram militarily. The inauguration of President Muhammadu Buhari in May 2015 ushered in a reinvigorated effort against Boko Haram and reversed six years of the group’s gains. However, Boko Haram will not be defeated with military force alone. Similar to other successful counterinsurgencies, Nigeria will have to incorporate a way for Boko Haram militants to lay down their arms and eventually return to Nigerian society. We believe OSC represents the beginning of such a program and should be built on using lessons learned from conflicts throughout the world. With assistance from international governments and other non-state actors, OSC could prove to be the program most responsible for bringing Boko Haram’s seven-year insurgency to an end.
