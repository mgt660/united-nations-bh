KEYS TO REINTEGRATION SUCCESS

Throughout the course of this research, we have observed myriad trends that have led to the success or failure of previous reintegration programs. This section seeks to outline the most pertinent elements and their applicability to the Nigerian context. The following is not meant to be understood as direct policy recommendations, but rather as strategic frameworks from which to draw when considering the feasibility of any action taken towards Boko Haram. It is our hope that international actors, as well as Nigerian state officials, will consider these trends when contemplating future actions.

 

All reintegration models discussed in this report have involved experts in diplomacy, demobilization, de-radicalization, reintegration, criminal justice, human rights and cultural inclusion -- working proactively with governments on reintegration processes. We believe that a similar approach would benefit Operation Safe Corridor. International expertise in Nigeria could ensure that Operation Safe Corridor has credibility, and will facilitate international buy-in and financial support for Nigerian efforts to reintegrate former Boko Haram combatants. We believe forming an international monitoring mission will have positive collateral consequences and will facilitate long-term sustainability of any reintegration process. In conjunction with and in support of the monitoring mission, we outline the following trends which may positively influence success in Nigeria:

 

Communicate National Priorities

_      Invoke Strategic Leadership: Given the large-scale security risks and impediments to economic development in the northeastern Nigeria caused by the Boko Haram insurgency, the success of any reintegration efforts requires the leadership and full political commitment of the Nigerian government. The reintegration effort must go hand-in-hand with a comprehensive program for humanitarian relief.

_      Foster Nigerian-Led Solutions: Collaboration can spell success or failure in any reintegration effort. Success in Nigeria will be enhanced by the Nigerian government establishing and empowering an interministerial task force, led by the Ministry of Justice, to coordinate the reintegration program and effectively address the humanitarian situation in northeastern Nigeria. Yet this task force should avoid being military-dominated, as it must include representatives from throughout Nigeria and across the political and religious spectrum.

_      Seek International Assistance: Because of the widespread accusations of human rights abuses by the Nigerian security forces and the popular perception of disenfranchisement in northeast Nigeria, international assistance is vital to resolve the conflict and to restore credibility in the Nigerian government. We turn to the example of the Aceh Monitoring Mission in Indonesia as an example of what to do during a reintegration process. Similarly, a monitoring mission in Nigeria would function as a neutral third-party to: 1) promote compliance with international law; 2) strengthen existing Nigerian institutions; 3) improve the appeal of the reintegration process for ex-combatants; and 4) build confidence in foreign investors to facilitate economic reconstruction; 5) promote peaceful transition of ex-combatants into civil society. International assistance would also amplify concurrent efforts to combat the devastating famine.

_      Forge Political Consensus: No reintegration attempt can be successful without political transparency and popular support. Although reintegration is highly contentious, any reintegration program would need to be debated from the National Assembly to the local community level to ensure legitimacy, national support, and the appropriation of sufficient government funds. Villages and communities must understand the nature of the transitional justice and invest in reintegration. In order to achieve lasting success, communities must be willing and prepared to receive former Boko Haram victims and combatants, with appropriate incentives as necessary.

_      Mobilize Popular Support: Reintegration efforts are doomed if governments make no effort to gauge community support for reintegration efforts through a public information campaign. Given the controversial nature of the reintegration process, the highest levels of government must promote the reintegration effort to the Nigerian public as the foremost national security priority. Success would involve communities that commit to support of reintegration being first in line to receive the benefits of foreign investment.

 

Establish Transitional Justice Mechanisms

_      Establish a Truth Commission: Building on the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) model, we recommend establishing a truth-seeking body. The body should be overseen by the international monitoring mission and engage all parties to the ongoing conflict in dialogue – former Boko Haram combatants, Nigerian security forces, community leaders, and victims alike. By acknowledging the collective atrocities of the past, this body can ensure accountability, serve justice, and facilitate reconciliation.

_      Prosecute Worst Offenders: We suggest that all former combatants be profiled by a team of psychology, human rights, and criminal law experts, in order to categorize combatants according to the depth and length of their involvement with Boko Haram - thereby categorizing leaders versus low-level fighters in the insurgency. Following the Sri Lankan model, ex-combatants identified by the interdisciplinary team as highly involved in the insurgency should undergo a judicial process whereby they are sentenced to prison time, if sufficient evidence of their involvement is found. Such prosecutions shall be carried out by a special terrorism court, established within the existing framework of the Nigerian judicial system.

_      Allow Conditional Amnesty for Truth Telling: Again, building on the South African model, we argue that former Boko Haram combatants who have been deemed less highly involved should have the opportunity for conditional amnesty through whole-truth telling, in order to bolster intelligence gathering to combat the ongoing insurgency.

_      Practice Leniency for Child Soldiers: In recognition of the Paris Principles, the Nigerian government should consider child soldiers victims rather than perpetrators. Criminal prosecution of these child soldiers should be pursued only for war crimes or crimes against humanity.

_      Hold Security Forces Accountable: Finally, Nigerian security forces must be held accountable for their heavy-handed tactics against Boko Haram, including the extrajudicial killing of Mohammed Yusuf. They must also be held accountable for the human rights abuses committed against civilians of Nigeria’s northeastern states. While the Nigerian government may wish to avoid criminal prosecution of its soldiers, it is our recommendation that punitive measures be taken against those who performed the most egregious violations of human rights. Additionally, voluntary involvement in the TRC body and commitment to whole-truth telling will be a vital step toward reestablishing community trust and a precondition for amnesty. Statements made by Nigerian security forces should be available to the public and be corroborated by the international monitoring mission. Security forces who choose not to participate in the TRC will be at risk of future criminal prosecution for their actions.

 

Implement Rehabilitation and Reintegration Programs for Affected Civilians

_      Incorporate Similar Programs for Civilians: Just as Boko Haram fighters must ultimately be rehabilitated and reintegrated into Nigerian society, the physical and psychological damage of those affected by Boko Haram’s violence and Nigerian security forces’ reprisals must also be addressed. A rehabilitation and reintegration program for civilians should occur simultaneously with one designed for former insurgents.

_      Provide Community-Based Reparations: In order to both gauge community support for the reintegration of former combatants and to build a more positive economic climate in northeastern Nigeria, affected communities should be offered reparations in the form of public investments into infrastructure and the provision of public goods and subsidies to training programs. The Government may consider instituting a public service component for reintegration, so that Boko Haram ex-combatants can help alleviate the hardships that Boko Haram helped create.

_      Address the IDP Crisis: Currently, only approximately ten percent of the estimated 2.5 million internally displaced persons (IDPs) reside in IDP camps; the rest are forced to live in “host communities” -- essentially shantytowns with limited resources. More resources, primarily from international organizations (IOs), non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and other non-state actors must be brought into northeast Nigeria to establish additional IDP camps.

_      Address Food Scarcity: Food scarcity in northeast Nigeria is also a growing concern. Nearly one million Nigerians are severely acutely malnourished; an estimated 50,000 to 75,000 children under five years of age are expected to die of malnutrition in the near future if nothing is done quickly. Therefore, as with the IDP crisis, Nigeria’s food crisis must be addressed through IOs, NGOs and other non-state actors.

_      Offer Vocational Training and Civil Schooling: Many IDPs left homes and jobs behind when they fled Boko Haram. Vocational training and educational opportunities should be provided in IDP camps and throughout the various “host communities” in northeast Nigeria. This will provide IDPs with a vocation or set of skills that can be transferred to jobs when they return to their communities.

 

Engage Traditional Leaders and Institutions

_      Rebuild Traditional Institutions: Traditional leaders and institutions -- religious leaders, community leaders, and councils -- have been marginalized and supplanted by elected politicians, particularly in northeastern Nigeria. This has resulted in less community resilience and cohesion and allowed for the infiltration of Boko Haram into northeastern communities. To make matters worse, in many areas, Boko Haram has systematically decimated traditional leaders and institutions. Reestablishing these leaders and institutions is essential to a successful program and to guard against future violent extremist groups. While some areas require traditional leader empowerment, others require total reconstruction from the grassroots level.

_      Incorporate into Decision-Making: Traditional leaders and institutions should be part of a formal decision making process to determine the placement of former combatants into specific communities. This will assist with community buy-in and guard against possible retribution from community members.

_      Incorporate Xeer Model from Somalia: For reconciliation at the family and community level, we recommend the adoption of the Xeer method (discussed in the Somalia model). This provides a way for family members, traditional leaders, and traditional institutions to become actively involved in the rehabilitation and reintegration process.

_      Allow Traditional Religious Leaders to Determine De-radicalization: Religious leaders should inform the creation of the determinants of de-radicalization prior to an ex-combatant’s release from a de-radicalization program. Many northeastern community members do not trust that Operation Safe Corridor provides adequate de-radicalization counseling and are unwilling to welcome former militants back into their communities. Incorporating religious leaders in the de-radicalization process and allowing them to determine de-radicalization criteria will prove crucial for community acceptance.

_      Encourage Communications Link between Traditional Leaders and Government: Traditional leaders and institutions should become the primary means of communication between their respective communities and other forms of local, state, and federal government. This will ensure a link between communities that accept Boko Haram militants with government mechanisms. Additionally, this link will help solidify traditional leaders and institutions and their roles in moving Nigeria beyond Boko Haram and violent extremism.

 

Determine the Physical Process

_      Hybrid Approach to Reintegration: As elaborated previously, it is crucial that any reintegration efforts not only focus on ex-combatants, but also approach reintegration from the community level. Thus, a hybrid approach to reintegration, in which ex-combatants remain in a secured facility for a certain amount of time for the purpose of counselling and de-radicalization and pursue vocational training outside the facility, can build bridges between combatants and the affected communities.

_      Facility Management: Each reintegration facility should be led by a team of specialists from the monitoring mission, interministerial task force, representatives of civil society organizations, psychologists, independent justice officials, medical professionals, and an expert on women’s affairs.

_      Length of Reintegration Program: Given the level of indoctrination of Boko Haram combatants, we do not believe that twelve weeks is sufficient for ex-combatants to remain in the facility. A longer period is recommended and should be specified by the panel responsible for profiling combatants on an individual basis.

_      Differentiation between Beneficiaries: There should be adequate facilities to cater to different groups. Specifically, we recommend differentiation by level of threat, gender, and age.

_      De-radicalization through Collaboration: De-radicalization efforts should be led by psychologists and be supported by local traditional and religious leaders. By particularly including local leaders and experts into the process, de-radicalization will be more effective, as beneficiaries can identify better with the personnel because language and cultural barriers are reduced.

_      Assessment before Reinsertion: Before beneficiaries are reinserted into communities, an assessment by a panel responsible for their profiling and counselling should be made, giving a clear recommendation and evaluation on each beneficiary’s status.

 

Address Human Rights

_      Institute Public Service for Food Security: In recognition of the relevance of agriculture in northern Nigeria and the severe food insecurity in the region, beneficiaries should be obligated to participate in public service to remedy any consequences created by the insurgents. Such service could include working on farms and assisting the transportation of farm goods to markets.

_      Apply a Climate Change Lens to Economic Reintegration Efforts: Failing to consider the impact of climate change on livelihoods when demobilizing Boko Haram fighters and providing them with vocational training -- such as through the current Operation Safe Corridor model -– may eventually drive them back to insurgency. Developing climate resilient livelihoods for not only ex-Boko Haram fighters but all people in the northeast, which is deeply impacted by climate change and desertification, is vital to insure a stable, sustainable peace.

_      Support Climate Change Adaptation Efforts through Regional Institutions: We recommend that the Nigerian government continue to work with the Lake Chad Basin Commission to implement its Development and Climate Action Plan, to support conservation and sustainable livelihoods on the basin/watershed level through the provision of technical assistance on best farming, fishing, and grazing practices. By supporting these efforts, the Nigerian government will help to establish long-term sustainability in the region and build community resilience.

 

Focus on Long-Term Sustainability

_      Address Security Sector Reform: We recommend that the Nigerian government, in coordination with the international monitor, implement Security Sector Reform (SSR) for the Nigerian security forces to improve compliance with international standards and human rights laws, as well as to build trust between the military and Nigerian community. The monitoring mission inherently supports further international assistance and assists the Nigerian government in modifying the methods, processes, and outcomes of the Nigerian military forces.

_      Combat the existing “Boko Haram mindset” to reduce recruitment: There is no reason that groups like Boko Haram cannot continue to emerge, grow, and flourish unless the underlying components of Nigerian society that make these groups appealing to young people are addressed. We recommend that in madrasas, additional non-controversial topics, such as math, are introduced into the curriculum. We also recommend that the monitoring mission work with the almajiri, and begin a discussion on whether or not secular education can, or should be implemented in the region. We also recommend that the monitoring mission seek to advocate for further women’s education - this is the first step in combatting poverty, maternal death, and overpopulation of unemployed youth, all of which act as motivators to join Boko Haram. Building an economic base for these communities will allow reintegration efforts to be more effectively monitored, and will also increase the likelihood of community forgiveness.

_      Promote foreign investment -- financially and politically: We recommend that the monitoring mission work to ensure that Nigerian Foreign Direct Investment is increased, and allocated appropriately to reintegration efforts. In showing international support and solidarity for the long-term viability of ex-combatant reintegration, the monitoring mission will inherently align with the Nigerian government to encourage Boko Haram combatants to lay down their arms, given the investment in de-radicalization and reintegration. The effects of such partnership will be powerful in reintegration processes, both in the immediate sense, and as Nigeria seeks to build a future without insurgency.
