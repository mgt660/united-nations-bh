Aceh, Indonesia: Reintegration of the GAM

Marisa Lowe

 

Abstract: The Free Aceh Movement and Boko Haram share similar underlying grievances, but the broader circumstances for these two post-conflict reintegrations diverge significantly. In 2005, the Free Aceh Movement, a separatist group based in the northern region of the island Sumatra, signed a peace agreement with the Indonesian government. The Free Aceh Movement had fought for 29 years advocating for self-determination and rule by Sharia law, and against economic marginalization and military abuses in the region. While its grievances are similar to Boko Haram, the Acehnese case is unique due to the 2004 tsunami, corresponding influx of international aid, peace resolution, and granting of regional autonomy. These differences aside, Aceh’s programmatic challenges and successes should inform a reintegration program for Boko Haram in Nigeria. Official reintegration efforts focused on political access and economic assistance over social, cultural, psychological, institutional, and other elements. In Aceh, target populations, for both combatants and victims, were difficult to define. Community-based projects were a viable form of economic assistance, but proved futile for social reconciliation and inadequate for extensive impact on welfare and poverty. The Aceh Monitoring Mission, an international body meant to ensure the implementation of the peace agreement, provided support and confidence to the transition process. The Aceh Monitoring Mission and combined efforts of the Government of Indonesia, international organizations, foreign governments, and Acehnese people are credited with Aceh’s transition to lasting peace.

 

Insurgency Description

The Free Aceh Movement (GAM) was formed in Aceh, Indonesia in 1976 with objectives of self-determination and implementation of Sharia law for the Aceh region. From 1976 to 2005, GAM-related violence and corresponding retribution by the Indonesian military came in waves. Military campaigns launched in response to GAM activity killed thousands and resulted in gross human rights violations. Following the 2004 tsunami that killed 167,000 in Aceh, the GAM and Government of Indonesia (GoI) reached a peace agreement: the Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding (MoU), signed in 2005.[286] Reintegration efforts began with this peace agreement and continue to the present day.

 

Causal Elements

GAM is only the most organized, recent iteration of Acehnese insurgency. GAM’s expressed grievances included economic marginalization (particularly involving Aceh’s vast oil and gas resources), lack of religious and political autonomy (seeking a legal system based on a more conservative interpretation of Islamic scripture), and human rights abuses by the military.[287] Surveys of the Acehnese people revealed consistent understandings of the conflict’s causes among communities.[288]

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

In accordance with the MoU, the Aceh Reintegration Agency (BRA) was formed under the Governor of Aceh.[289] Since 2005, BRA itself has seen variations in structure, form and activities.[290]

 

International Agency Involvement

International support flooded Aceh following the destructive 2004 tsunami.[291] The international agencies involved in reintegration included the International Organization of Migration (IOM), European Union (EU), World Bank (WB), and United Nations Development Programme (UNDP). The participation of these organizations in reintegration efforts varied from funding to monitoring to technical assistance to establishing and running programs. IOM was invited by the GoI to oversee the release of political prisoners and help provide for the reintegration of former combatants. IOM also facilitated critical public information sharing by printing and distributing copies of the Helsinki MoU. IOM also managed the Village Prosperity Through Peace Program (Makmu Gampong Kareuna Dame) for quick-impact projects (QIP) and traditional reconciliation activities for communities.[292]

The World Bank began the Kecamatan Development Project in October 2006, which was transformed into the Kecamatan-Based Reconstruction and Reintegration Program (KDP) and used by BRA to provide community-based projects for victim reintegration efforts.[293]

The UNDP’s Strengthening Sustainable Peace and Development in Aceh (SSDPA) Program provided technical assistance to BRA, as well as programs for livelihood improvement of ex-combatants and victims of conflict.[294] The UNDP, in conjunction with the EU, also created the Strengthening Access to Justice in Indonesia (SAJI) project, which aimed to clarify legal framework for customary law, support transitional justice, and increase awareness and access to legal counsel.[295]

The Aceh Monitoring Mission (AMM) was deployed to Aceh from 2005 to 2006 to oversee the implementation of the MoU, including reintegration. The AMM was mandated by the MoU and was supported by the EU, Norway, Switzerland, and some ASEAN countries. The AMM served as an unarmed supervisor, deploying two hundred monitors across eleven field offices, facilitating discussion between the GoI and GAM, and responding to violations. The AMM was deemed a tremendous success in dealing with the many security questions of Aceh’s transition from conflict to peace.[296]

 

NGO Involvement

            Civil society representatives were given initial seats at the BRA table, although their roles and continued representation remain unclear.[297] International aid organizations often funded civil society organizations to provide services, such as the UNDP’s Gender-Responsive Approach for Reintegration and Peace Stabilization, which provided funds to support the reintegration of female ex-combatants and women left out of the official DDR process.[298]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

            The three populations for reintegration identified in the MoU were former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians.[299] Through political negotiations, BRA’s Economic Empowerment program also came to include GAM non-combatants and the Pembela Tanah Air (PETA) pro-Indonesian militants.[300] Beyond individual programs, community-based projects provided broader support across the Acehnese population.

            It was expected that GAM would provide a list of three thousand combatants to be served by BRA’s ex-combatant reintegration efforts. The creation of such a list did not occur for three reasons. First, the number of GAM combatants was far greater than three thousand. Second, GAM feared the peace process would collapse and former combatants would be punished. Third, GAM sought to control the reintegration process, as opposed to receiving directives from the government. Remarkably, instead of receiving the list of GAM combatants, BRA provided allowances to GAM that were spread more widely among its network.[301]

GAM, BRA, and Acehnese civil society agreed on a ten-point criteria for “affected civilians.” The criteria included loss of life, physical damage, loss of wealth, displacement, injury, illness, and loss of employment.[302] Unfortunately, these criteria had little effect on the actual distribution of assistance.[303]

BRA solicited victim applications and was inundated with over 50,000 applications covering 600,000 individuals.[304] Unable to deal with such volume, BRA turned to the WB-funded KDP project to facilitate victim assistance. KDP provided community-based assistance to villages, using facilitators to help identify conflict victims and their needs. Villages were required to hold four community meetings in order to identify, analyze, and address their needs with a project. These meetings were meant to emphasize community ownership and the last meeting was devoted to report accountability after project completion. Allocation size was based on the following criteria: a village’s level of conflict, history of successful KDP grants, exposure to violence, village size, and conflict victims’ needs. Following the first round of village funding, BRA-KDP reverted their program to individual allocations.[305]

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

In accordance with the MoU, GAM combatants were given amnesty. Political prisoners were released with the exception of “disputed cases.” Article 2.2 and 2.3 of the MoU mandated the creation of a human rights court (HRC) and a truth and reconciliation commission (TRC). Article 1.4.5 held that military personnel who had committed civilian crimes would be tried in Aceh civilian court.[306]

The implementation of the human rights court and truth and reconciliation commission outlined in the MoU stalled. For ten years, victims and activists sought the creation of HRC and TRC institutions for justice, in addition to the public release of official government reports on human rights abuses by security forces.[307] In November 2015, the Aceh provincial parliament announced the selection of a team for the TRC to operate between 2016 and 2021.[308] There have been no reports of the commission’s commencement.

Similar to Somalia’s Xeer model, UNDP SAJI engaged the informal legal system and worked closely with the Aceh Customary Council (MAA) to strengthen the quality of Aceh’s adat system.[309] Adat courts were officially abolished in 1951, but adat law is still applied informally in a village setting. The adat process involves a formal complaint, hearing sessions, and a signed letter of agreement.[310] Thirteen of twenty-three districts in Aceh now provide funding support for adat justice mechanisms, and 91 percent of justice users have reported satisfaction with quality.[311]

 

Community Engagement

            Ex-GAM combatants returned to their original communities. The level of community acceptance for former combatants was high, greater than 90 percent.[312] Through KDP, communities were engaged in discussions to determine funding for community-based projects.

 

 Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

Official BRA funds focused on economic development and did not include allocations for social reintegration.[313] International aid organizations, like IOM, however, facilitated traditional reconciliation ceremonies.[314] IOM, through their Information, Counseling & Referral Services (ICRS), gave one million rupiah (US $110) to villages in conjunction with KDP for traditional peusijeuk ceremonies.[315] The peusijeuk involves pouring sacred water over participants, symbolizing the “cooling down” or lasting peace after conflict.[316] Aside from the peusijeuk, many reconciliation ceremonies involved Islam, including sermons from imams and readings from the Qur'an. In total, 74 percent of former GAM members reported attending a reconciliation ceremony in their village.[317] Evaluations of KDP-treated communities did not reveal a significant difference in social cohesion.

            The MoU provided Aceh with special autonomy and allowed the formation of local political parties. Since then, two GAM-affiliated Aceh governors have been elected.[318]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

            BRA provided economic assistance and capital to former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians without much training for the use of such capital. Given this criticism, in 2009, BRA began providing limited vocational training in certain districts. Aside from BRA, IOM has provided vocational assistance to former prisoners and combatants.[319]

Incentives

            The allocation of cash to former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians provided incentives for participation, overburdening BRA far beyond its capacity. Reintegration of ex-combatants in Niger Delta experienced similar problems with cash incentives, although the Acehnese case included victims and converted to community-based projects as a solution.

 

Cost Estimate

            Cost numbers vary widely, based on program, agency, and timeframe inclusion. BRA’s pool for allocation totaled to $150 million.[320] Another report from the Asia Foundation estimated that $365 million was spent between 2005 and 2009 by GoI and international donors on peacebuilding.[321] Financing was provided by the Government of Indonesia, foreign governments, and international organizations.

 

Results & Metrics

            There is copious data on Aceh, including household perception surveys, case studies, aid and donor reviews, economic surveys, violence incident reports, and more.[322]

The Aceh Reintegration Livelihood Surveys (ARLS) was a large-scale household and village head survey measuring the impact of the World Bank’s KDP program. The survey was meant to capture community and household welfare, individual behavior and attitudes, and community cohesion. Attitudes towards the KDP program were positive; 94 percent of villagers treated said the program was helpful. Attitudes towards welfare were also positive; individuals in treated communities believed their welfare had increased with KDP. There was no direct, measurable evidence, however, of program impact on welfare. The survey displayed no resulting increase in social cohesion and trust in government.[323]

Economic metrics reveal that post-conflict efforts have had little positive effect. Aceh’s Gross Regional Domestic Product (GRDP) growth has been negative since 2004, save for 2006, which was affected by the influx of post-tsunami recovery funds. Aceh remains poorer than the rest of Indonesia, and its Human Development Index (HDI) has not improved at the same rate as other Indonesian provinces. Foreign Direct Investment remains incredibly low. The difference in poverty between high intensity and low intensity conflict areas within Aceh, however, is significant; high intensity conflict districts generally have lower poverty rates than low intensity conflict districts.[324]

The National Violence Monitoring System (NVMS), a World-Bank program, uses local news reports to detect and record violent incidents across Indonesia.[325] According to NVMS, Aceh has seen a 94 percent decline in annual fatalities since conflict ended in 2005.[326] NVMS has also revealed patterns in the existing post-conflict violence in Aceh: first, violent incidents have grown more frequent but less fatal; second, violence is highly concentrated in areas of high intensity conflict during the war; third, violence is often triggered by elections, resources, and ethnic and religious tensions.[327]

 

Challenges

Defining the Target Population: The MoU was a pioneer in acknowledging victims as a target population for reintegration. Between former combatants, political prisoners, and “affected civilians,” however, the line between the target population and community for reintegration was difficult to define. Damages could be calculated as lives lost, monetary losses, unemployment, physical destruction, and psychological harm. Approximately 15,000 individuals lost their lives and damages exceeded $10.7 trillion.[328] A Harvard University medical team found that 33 percent of Acehnese suffered from depression and 48 percent from anxiety.[329] Forty-nine percent of the Acehnese population considers themselves to be a victim of the conflict.[330] The vastness of victimhood made verifying individual allocation targets next to impossible.

Individual Allocations vs. Community-Based Projects: BRA-KDP experimented with both individual allocations and community-based projects. Individual allocations were challenging because of verification and coordination between different programs. The Multi-Stakeholder Review of Post-Conflict Programming in Aceh (MSR) suggested that individual allocations did not have a significant effect on economic welfare.[331]

Community-based projects, on the other hand, had more widespread and measurable impacts on perceptions of welfare. Communities that received KDP saw an 11-point decrease in “poor” populations perceived by village heads. The community-based programs were more likely to include marginalized and minority groups, although there was no evidence of stronger social cohesion or government relations as a result.[332]

Holding Government Forces Accountable for Human Rights Abuses: The Acehnese NGO Coalition for Human Rights registered over ten thousand cases of human rights abuses during the conflict.[333] Despite mandates in the MoU for a HRC and TRC, neither system has been instituted.

Successes

            The Aceh peace and reintegration process has been viewed as a success, primarily because of lasting peace since the signing of the MoU in 2005. Community-funded projects had a greater effect on economic welfare than individual allocations, although the measured effect was small.

 

Similarities to Boko Haram

Religion: While both GAM and Boko Haram espoused literalist Islamic ideals, their different approaches to religion were significant. The practice of more conservative Islam in Aceh served as a distinguishing factor between the province and the rest of Indonesia. One of the initial core demands of the GAM was the implementation of Sharia law in an autonomous Aceh.[334] Since gaining special autonomy, Sharia law has been enforced in Aceh province, punishing individuals for offenses such as alcohol, homosexuality, and gambling.[335] Boko Haram, on the other hand, arguably uses Islam as more of a tool for resonance than an objective for practical implementation.

Military Abuses: In response to both GAM and Boko Haram, the military committed abuses which in turn galvanized support for the respective insurgencies. Notably, during the Aceh conflict in 2000, the Indonesian justice system convicted and jailed twenty-four soldiers for committing a massacre at an Islamic school.[336] While the trial was seen as a test of the Indonesian government’s commitment to human rights and willingness to bring justice to abuses in Aceh, human rights abuses and gross violence continued after 2000 until 2005. The failure to publish reports of military abuses and set up a HRC or TRC has continued to agitate the Acehnese people and human rights advocates.

Oil: The sharing of wealth from oil resources in Aceh, like northern Nigeria, was a major source of conflict. One way to view the GAM insurgency is that of an uprising against “oppressive natural resource exploitation.”[337] Oil extraction led to greater government appropriation of revenue and increased military presence in Aceh. Despite its role as an impetus for conflict, wealth sharing was not a primary topic of negotiation in the MoU. While the near depletion of Aceh’s oil resources reduced its significance in the post-conflict setting, the lack of any economic plan or prospects for ex-combatants and Aceh overall remains a liability.[338]

Poverty: Both Aceh and Northern Nigeria are poor relative to the rest of their countries. For Aceh, widespread poverty and lack of economic development and foreign investment are continuing challenges for the region and its stability. Increasing economic inequality in the province is especially concerning given correlations between violence and resource tensions in NVMS data.

 

Differences from Boko Haram

            Tsunami: The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami Aceh drastically affected the circumstances for post-conflict reintegration. First, the utter devastation caused by the tsunami inflicted widespread injury to fighters and non-fighters alike. Everyone was a victim. Second, the tsunami’s destruction spurred a massive outpouring of international aid to Indonesia. Post-conflict rehabilitation thus coincided with post-tsunami rehabilitation and it is hard to distinguish between the effects of the two.

            International aid organizations provided tremendous supplemental support to the post-conflict reintegration process. While BRA focused on financial allocations and economic development for the target populations outlined in the MoU, international programs extended aid to psychological elements, traditional reconciliation ceremonies like peusijeuk, skills training, and victim populations unacknowledged by the MoU and BRA.

Community Attitudes: As mentioned above, more than 90 percent of ex-combatants were accepted into their former communities. This is due in part to existing community support for GAM, as well as IOM’s public information campaigns. At the height of the conflict, GAM controlled between 70 and 80 percent of Aceh and received considerable community support. Community support was galvanized by shared grievances of the GoI, including brutal counterinsurgency operations, corruption, ineffectiveness, and a failure to implement special autonomy for Aceh.[339] Acehnese communities supported the GAM in their insurgency and their reintegration. This stands in stark contrast to the reported lack of community support for Boko Haram in Nigeria.

Organizational Cohesion: From insurgency to negotiation to reintegration to political representation, GAM leadership maintained adequate control to communicate and navigate on behalf of the group. The splintered nature of Boko Haram, on the other hand, is a concern for any attempt to deal with the insurgency as a unit. Some disagreement within GAM has triggered violence in Aceh. Ex-combatants who split with the Aceh Transition Committee (KPA), the BRA’s post-MoU organization, due to disagreements about payouts, have been responsible for grenade incidents, electoral violence, and crime.[340]

Peace Agreement & Political Representation: The conflict between GAM and GoI underwent multiple failed peace processes before the signing of the MoU. The peace negotiation processes were intensive and compromising. Among the many assessments of Acehnese peace and reintegration, there is consensus that a predominant factor for peace was the political representation granted to Aceh in the MoU. The designation of Aceh as a special province provided the region and its people with greater political access and influence in Jakarta, and long-desired cultural, social, and political autonomy in Aceh. From Jakarta’s perspective, this autonomy has had its drawbacks, including the implementation of Sharia law in Aceh, which has corresponded with human rights violations like the caning of women and fears of increasing violent extremism in Indonesia.[341]

 

Recommendations for Boko Haram in Nigeria

            Given Aceh’s programmatic challenges and successes and its similarities to the case of Boko Haram in Nigeria, the following are specific recommendations for a reintegration program:

1.     Coherent Strategy & Objective: The MoU described a desire for sustained peace, but beyond political concessions and DDR, it is unclear how the GoI and GAM envisioned sustained peace.[342] The official reintegration agency, BRA, primarily facilitated economic assistance with little economic impact. While Aceh reintegration was successful at maintaining relative peace, its success in other areas, such as welfare, economy, and social cohesion, is less clear. The multiplicity of programs and stakeholders allowed for greater coverage, but also represented different objectives, strategies, and roles, which were confusing if not conflicting. The lack of a clear strategy for reintegration meant that effects were less limited and streamlined. Ultimately, without coherent objectives, there is no clear path for program evaluation and improvement.

 

2.     Monitoring Mission: Like Colombia, Aceh had an international monitoring mission that is credited with much of Aceh’s successful transition from conflict to peace. Beyond its role as a supervisor and facilitator of disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration, the AMM served to instill trust and confidence in the implementation process. While few violations of the MoU occurred, the presence of the AMM assured participants that any minor slip-up would not jeopardize overall progress towards peace.

 

3.     Truth & Reconciliation Commission: Mimicking the South African solution, the MoU called for the creation of a TRC, a promise which has yet to be fulfilled. Given problems in Aceh, including lacking social cohesion and trust in government, greater reconciliatory efforts are needed. A structured process of evaluation, reconciliation, and even punishment, is necessary for a government to pivot from adversary to authority, especially given the military abuses that occurred.

 

4.     Community-Based Projects: Like Sri Lanka, Aceh utilized community-based projects for reintegration, which had greater impact on perceived welfare than individual allocations. The difference between perceived and actual impact and its benefits remains a question for further research. The case of Aceh suggests small, relative success with community-based projects over individual allocations in the context of widespread conflict and victimhood.
