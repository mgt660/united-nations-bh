Colombia: Peace Agreement with the FARC

Lauren Wyman

 

Abstract: The peace process between the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC) and the government of Colombia can shed light on appropriate paths to take when considering the reintegration of Boko Haram combatants into Nigerian society. While the nature of the militancy in Colombia is different than that of Boko Haram - Marxist/Leninist versus pseudo-religious - the insurgencies are similar in their use of kidnapping, child soldiers and links with illicit activities as well as their presence in traditionally marginalized areas. The Colombian peace agreement was defeated in a national referendum, however the government and the FARC revised the agreement and it was passed through Congress on November 30, 2016. Nigeria can look to the Colombian peace process for a multi-sectoral approach to peace, including land reform, a path to political participation, victims’ reparations, truth telling commissions as well as demobilization monitored through a verification mission of government, FARC and UN officials. The Colombian peace agreement aims to build peace from both the bottom-up and top-down, fostering a culture of peace and reconciliation, which could inform the Nigerian path to reintegration of Boko Haram fighters. The Nigerian government may consider assessing public opinion prior to reintegration.

 

Time Period: 1964 - present

Since the 1960s, the civil conflict between the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC), the government, and paramilitary groups have resulted in over six million displaced and 220,000 dead throughout the country.[343] The government of Colombia has launched numerous peace talks with the FARC, starting in 1982, but each has crumbled.[344]  The fourth and most recent round of peace talks, initiated in 2012 by President Juan Manuel Santos, were ratified by the federal government and by the FARC, but were rejected by the public through a national referendum on October 2, 2016. Many Colombians opposed the peace deal for being too conciliatory toward the FARC, criticizing the lack of jail time and the guarantee of seats in Congress to the FARC political party.[345]

The opposition to the peace agreement was spearheaded by former President Alvaro Uribe, whose hardline stance on the FARC is well known in Colombia.[346] However, the government and the FARC returned to the negotiating table in Havana, Cuba, after the 2016 treaty was rejected. On November 11, 2016, a revised peace agreement was produced that incorporated the opposition’s suggestions. The agreement was ratified by Congress on November 30, 2016.

 

Insurgency Description

The FARC (in Spanish: Fuerzas Armadas Revolutionarios de Colombia, Ejercito del Pueblo) is a Marxist-Leninist militant organization formed in the 1960s to institute land reform and redistribution in the countryside.[347] It emerged as an offshoot of the Colombian Communist Party and was violently repressed by the right-wing government starting in 1964, through a military attack on the group in the Tolima department in May of that year.[348]  The FARC emerged after a particularly violent stage in Colombian history known as La Violencia - the Violence - in which conservative and liberal politics ballooned into a rural war and land grab. [349]

The FARC fueled its operations through kidnapping and demanding ransom for targets, primarily politicians and social elites. In its early years, the FARC often provided medical care, education and defense services for marginalized communities in areas where government was lacking. The organization started to incorporate narcotrafficking into its strategy in the 1980s as well as illegal mining and oil extraction to fund operations.[350] At the height of the FARC’s power in the late 1990s and early 2000s, there were nearly twenty thousand FARC soldiers. As of 2016, the number of soldiers is approximately seven thousand, including three thousand women. Despite the decrease, there is a resilient network of FARC sympathizers throughout the country.[351] Of note, the Colombian populations inhabiting the rural countryside, especially indigenous communities, have been victims of attacks of not only the FARC, but also attacks by paramilitary groups and by the Colombian military.[352] 

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

On a federal level, the National Victim’s Unit (UARIV) keeps track of IDPs and connects conflict victims to social services, like healthcare and education.[353] This organization works also with local governments to implement Law 1448 of 2011, which recognizes the rights of people – particularly IDPs - to settle in land that was taken from them by the FARC or paramilitary groups.

Other civilian agencies involved include the High Commission for Post-Conflict, Human Rights and Security, which was formed in anticipation of the peace accords being ratified by the public and is intended to facilitate the national transition to peace. Governmental agencies supporting social protection programs in conflict zones include the National Planning Department, the Department of Social Prosperity, the Institute for Family Welfare and the National Department of Health, which provide services for conflict victims, including food assistance, emergency healthcare, school feeding programs and land redistribution. Additionally, other federal agencies like the Ministry of the Environment are developing agricultural and environmental projects to promote peace and climate change adaptation.

 

Military Involvement: Plan Colombia & Aerial Spraying

During the 2000s, under the presidency of Alvaro Uribe, the Colombian military launched a massive offensive known as Plan Colombia. This was a $9 billion influx of military aid, primarily from the US, to bolster Colombian counter-FARC military capacities, as well as intelligence. Through Plan Colombia, dozens of high-level FARC leaders were killed, which significantly weakened the group. [354] In recent years, the government has targeted coca plantations and sprayed them with herbicides, destroying the crops to reduce the sources of FARC funding. However, human rights groups decried the practice for causing illnesses (including cancer) and for disproportionately impacting indigenous and Afro-Colombian communities.[355]

Within the proposed peace accords, the Colombian military are to be stationed around concentration zones, where FARC members will demobilize and hand in their weapons to the United Nations. However, it is also important to note that the military was accused of war crimes as well during the fifty-two-year long conflict.[356] Due to the tension and the history of human rights abuses on both sides, the Colombian government asked the UN to accompany the turning in of arms during the peace process and to run the concentration zones.

 

Impetus for Reintegration

Plan Colombia severely weakened FARC. By eliminating FARC leadership, this military offensive reduced the social cohesion of the group, putting them on the move frequently, and forcing the FARC to resort to defensive tactics like bombings, rather than offensive attacks to seize territory. In recent years, linked with the hardline military campaign and with national advertising campaigns, the FARC had experienced a rush of desertion, reducing their numbers to less than half of their peak and providing the federal government with intelligence.[357] 

President Juan Manuel Santos, backed by the international community, made it a priority of his administration to finalize a peace agreement with the FARC. With hardline national policy on narco-trafficking, profits from coca and cocaine production were decreasing. The FARC had also proclaimed that it hoped to transition to bring its battle to the political sphere as its own political party.

 

Criteria for Reintegration

All FARC combatants will be eligible for reintegration under the current peace agreement, as long as they hand in their weapons, help identify missing persons, and confess their crimes to a peace tribunal, through a truth and reconciliation process, based closely on South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Those who admit to their crimes are eligible to alternative sentences and restorative justice to make amends to victims - by working on community farms, demining, reforestation, substituting illicit crops, etc.[358] However if the ex-combatants lie or do not turn in their weapons, they will be subject to up to twenty years in prison. As of now, the FARC is demobilizing in its entirety; there are no known FARC dissidents who are refusing to hand in their weapons and accept peace. Certain crimes disqualify ex-combatants from amnesty, including sexual violence, kidnapping, torture, extrajudicial killings, forced disappearances and recruitment of minors.[359] The original peace agreement received a lot of backlash for its “soft” stance on the FARC - specifically that most soldiers would receive no jail time.

In terms of the reintegration of child soldiers, within fifteen days of the signing of the new agreement, the government will develop a special program for their effective reintegration. The National Reincorporation Council will be in charge of this program.[360]

 

Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

The approach that the government has taken to negotiations has been that the FARC should make reparations for the damage caused by the prolonged conflict and contribute to making the post-conflict society better. The agreement recognizes the differential needs of LGBTI and women ex-combatants and the psycho-social needs of victims of sexual and gender-based violence. The agreement emphasizes that in post-conflict society, it is important to create spaces for communities to express their suffering and reconcile with ex-combatants.[361] Additionally, in the new agreement, the FARC will turn in all of its assets to the government to compensate victims. This echoes the South African model, where reparations were planned to come from a wealth tax on industries unduly benefited from apartheid.

For the sake of this brief, I will go into two key elements of the reconciliation process outlined in the peace agreement that can inform our recommendations on Boko Haram: concentration zones and political participation.

Concentration Zones:  After the peace agreement is signed, demobilizing FARC soldiers will migrate to twenty-three concentration zones, which are located in isolated regions of the country. Similar to the Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCS) in Sri Lanka, the concentration zones will serve as a halfway zone for the FARC, where they will officially demobilize and transition to civilian life. Unarmed civilians can enter the concentration zones to provide social services like healthcare and education; the military is not allowed within one-kilometer of each zone. Within 180 days of the signing of the peace agreement, the FARC must turn in all weapons to the United Nations. Once the final peace agreement is signed, fighters will go to these zones for up to six months and then will be able to move on a restricted basis throughout the country. A monitoring and verification mission made up of UN, FARC and Colombian governmental officials has access to each zone to ensure compliance.[362] Demobilized FARC soldiers, after leaving the concentration zones, are eligible for 90 percent of minimum wage for two years as well as grants to start businesses.[363]

Political Participation:  After demobilization, FARC leaders will be able to run for office and thus participate in the Colombian political sphere.[364] Over the next two election cycles, the FARC will have non-voting seats in the national Congress, after which they will be subject to the normal electoral process. Under the new peace agreement, the FARC political party will receive less government funding than in the original agreement.[365] 

 

Community Engagement

Public opinion on the peace agreements was polled frequently and captured in a national referendum on October 2, 2016. As mentioned above, the public rejected the peace agreement 50.2 percent to 49.7 percent - primarily in zones that are linked to former President Alvaro Uribe, specifically the Antioquia department. While the polls suggested for months that the peace would win by a landslide (NB: the international community was already congratulating Colombia on achieving peace before the referendum) many people throughout the country had deeply opposed it. Remarkably, many of the communities that were most impacted by the conflict were the ones who most strongly supported the peace.[366]

 

Lessons Learned

Colombia’s peace is an ongoing process: the final agreement was just passed by Congress on November 30, 2016. However, several lessons about the process of forming a peace agreement can be gleaned from Colombia’s experience:

1.     Engage with opposition parties when developing the peace agreement so that it is amenable across the political spectrum. The campaign to refuse the peace accords, spearheaded by former President Alvaro Uribe, was a stronger voice politically than Santos’ team had originally imagined. That the government and the FARC took the opposition’s suggestions into consideration while revising the peace agreement shows the strength of the process.

 

2.     Consider how the agreement is being marketed on a national level. Through the peace negotiations, several fears surfaced in the public mind about what would happen post-conflict - specifically that Colombia would turn into a Chavista state, ruled by left-wing rebels. Many Colombians are extremely familiar with the recent economic turmoil in Venezuela, thus feared that the agreement was painted by opposition forces as turning Colombia into the next Venezuela.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

The nature of the militancy in Colombia is profoundly different than that of Boko Haram. Not only did the FARC start as a left-wing, non-religious political movement in rural areas of the country, its links with the drug trade and other militant groups throughout Colombia distinguish it from the pseudo-religious Boko Haram institution. FARC has been active since the 1960s and has suffered high rates of abandonment in recent years whereas Boko Haram only began in earnest in the last six years.

However, there are crucial parallels between the groups, including the recruitment of child soldiers, kidnapping, attacks on military and government infrastructure, heavy military repression by the national government and socio-economic drivers of participation (particularly lack of economic opportunity). Additionally, in both insurgencies, the national militaries allegedly committed extensive human rights abuses.[367]

The following are recommendations that the Nigerian government can extract when thinking about how to reintegrate Boko Haram combatants:

1.     Accompaniment by external verification missions. Any reintegration and demobilization program should be accompanied by impartial external verification missions due to the history of human rights abuses by both the Nigerian military and by Boko Haram. This would follow best practices from Indonesia, where the Aceh Monitoring Mission (AM) instilled confidence in the implementation of disarmament, demobilization and reintegration through engaging impartial third parties.

 

2.     Assessing public opinion before reintegration is vital. As with the national referendum on the peace agreement, which demonstrated that many Colombians were not ready for peace, it will be important for the FGN to survey and address public opinions on reintegration of Boko Haram combatants. An utter lack of popular support will likely condemn reintegration to failure. Considering that reintegration will occur on a community level, the FGN could poll the receiving community’s opinion on reintegration of Boko Haram combatants.

 

3.     Open up alternate routes for dissident voices to be heard. Boko Haram, like the FARC, has been heavily repressed by the national government. Opening up routes for peaceful participation in national politics is a key aspect of demobilization and reintegration. Thus, as with the reintegration of the FARC, former combatants should not be barred from holding political office on the local or national levels or from voting. Ex-combatants cannot be entirely disenfranchised. They must be empowered to express their grievances and goals in a political and peaceful manner, rather than resorting to violence.

 

4.     Incorporate a cross-sectoral approach. While the Colombian peace agreement has just been passed by Congress, its cross-sectoral approach to reintegration may prove to be highly effective in the case of Boko Haram. That the agreement emphasizes rural development as well as political participation and restorative justice through community-based initiatives means that peace will be implemented at the individual, local, regional, and national levels simultaneously. 

 

5.     Open up spaces for people to talk about the conflict and reconciliation. The Colombian government is opening up campaigns to create spaces on the local level for people to come together and talk about their experiences and seek reconciliation, following the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission model. By doing so, the government hopes to foster a national culture of forgiveness and peace, which the Nigerian government could look to as a positive example.
