EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

 

Background

Since the early 2000s, Boko Haram – also known as Jama’atu Ahlus Sunnah Lid Da’awati Wal Jihad, or People Committed to the Prophet’s Teachings for Propagation and Jihad – has been a constant source of instability in northern Nigeria. Emerging in 2002 under the leadership of Mohammed Yusuf, the group began its resistance movement against the secular Nigerian government largely through proselytization and other non-violent means, interspersed with occasional “hit-and-run attacks” on police stations and security outposts throughout Nigeria’s northeastern states.[1] Following a strong government crackdown in 2009, which resulted in Yusuf’s highly publicized extra-judicial killing, Boko Haram has increasingly shifted strategies, “adopt(ing) the tactics of global Salafi Jihadist groups, including targeted assassinations, suicide bombings, and hostage taking.”[2] Beginning in 2011, the frequency and severity of the group’s attacks continued to grow, reaching its peak in 2014-2015.[3]

Compounding factors have complicated the Nigerian government and international community’s response to the Boko Haram insurgency. Dismal socio-economic prospects in northern Nigeria have left citizens feeling neglected and resentful of what they perceive to be a corrupt southern government;[4] environmental degradation has created a humanitarian emergency for thousands in the region;[5] and perceptions of Muslim marginalization have potentially increased the number of insurgency sympathizers.[6] Meanwhile, the Nigerian military has been criticized for its heavy-handed management of the conflict - accused of numerous human rights abuses against Nigerian civilians in its attempts to weaken the insurgency.[7] Internal dissent within Boko Haram itself has further complicated the government and international community’s response; numerous splinter factions within Boko Haram have demonstrated conflicting ideologies and competed for authority in Nigeria’s northern states.[8] These complications have made any attempt at a focused response to the insurgency more problematic.

Since 2015, government forces have been steadily reclaiming land in the northeast, leading many experts to assert that Boko Haram is now on the defensive.[9] Hundreds of former combatants are said to have voluntarily defected, leaving the Nigerian government with the difficult task of upholding justice and peacefully reintegrating former Boko Haram militants into local communities, all while simultaneously putting pressure on existing combatants to lay down their arms. Despite previous experience with reintegration and amnesty programs in Nigeria - e.g. in the Niger Delta region - the government has sought guidance from international actors regarding potential transitional justice strategies to address the Boko Haram challenge.

 

Report Structure

To address these challenges, the following analysis was produced as part of the Yale University Jackson Institute for Global Affairs course “Boko Haram: Defining a Path to Reintegration” during autumn of 2016. Prior to the start of the course, Professor Casey King met with representatives from the United Nations Counter-Terrorism Committee Executive Directorate (UNCTED), to solicit input on the current barriers to ending the Boko Haram insurgency. In preparation for upcoming discussions with Nigerian authorities, aimed at devising an approach for managing former Boko Haram combatants in custody or detention, UNCTED requested that the class examine similar country models for reintegration. In contrast to more traditional survey courses, the course was structured as a consulting engagement, with UNCTED serving as the “client” and the class as an interdisciplinary team of consultants. Early in the course, the class learned that UNCTED was preparing to meet with Nigerian government officials in Abuja from December 13th-14th, 2016, to present recommendations on reintegration and to facilitate a discussion with a panel of international experts. This gave the course a clear objective: to provide insight and analysis to support UNCTED’s consultations with the Nigerian government.

Lessons Learned

Through our research, several themes emerged regarding the key challenges and impediments to reintegration. In many cases, there was no movement toward reintegration prior to a formal peace deal between the government and insurgent group. This is particularly concerning given the lack of progress toward political settlement in Nigeria. Additionally, lack of public trust in the government and military, often explained by a history of human rights abuses, created additional barriers to reintegration. Lastly, we observed that disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration (DDR) programs which emphasize punitive measures without offering a path to a productive life can restart the cycle of disenfranchisement, which often precedes violent insurgency.

However, these reintegration models also illuminate a wide range of opportunities for the current conflict in Nigeria. Reintegration allows countries to formally involve underrepresented and disenfranchised groups and repair relationships with non-governmental community leaders. Reintegration models also offer economic incentives to ex-insurgents and communities alike, by building job skills and stimulating the economy. Because of the increasing interdependency of nations, reintegration not only requires a whole-of-government approach, but the disputing parties can often leverage the expertise of neighbors and the international community to create a sustainable solution.
