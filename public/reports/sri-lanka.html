Sri Lanka: Reintegration of the LTTE

Marie Sophie Pocha

 

Abstract: After the 2009 civil war came to an end with government forces defeating the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE), Sri Lanka decided to reintegrate former combatants back into communities. The government set up 24 Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCs) across the country where over 10,000 fighters have been reintegrated since 2009. The Sri Lankan Model is considered one of the most successful approaches to reintegration, because there has been no resurgence of violence since the conflict. However, the lack of strong transitional justice measures remains a reason for criticism and is still a polarizing topic in Sri Lanka and globally. Other parts of the reintegration program, however, can be considered useful examples, namely the community-based approach, the relevance of recreational and social activities, as well as the active participation of private companies in the training efforts for ex-combatants. When it comes to the reintegration of Boko Haram combatants, Sri Lanka does offer some parallels to the conflict, as the LTTE also recruited child soldiers, kidnapped members of the local communities in the north east and committed suicide attacks. Furthermore, the Sri Lankan government faced the issue of few financial means to fund the reintegration program. Still, the Sri Lankan civil war ended with the defeat of the LTTE combatants, which constitutes a significantly different power balance between the parties as a basis for a reintegration program.

 

Model Name: National Framework Proposal for Reintegration of Ex-combatants into Civilian Life in Sri Lanka.

 

Time Period

The Sri Lankan Civil War lasted over twenty-five years and officially ended in May 2009.[368] The government of Sri Lanka began designing their national plan for the reintegration of ex-combatants in March 2009, and completed their plan by the end of July 2009. The government determined that reintegration efforts should take up to one year, preferably ten to twelve months.[369] The reintegration program officially started in October 2009 with the first ex-combatants coming to Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCs), the rehabilitation facilities set up by the government during and after the civil war.

 

Insurgency Description

The Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) fought for the independence of northeastern Sri Lanka to establish their own Tamil state. Their insurgency was a reaction to the increasing discrimination of the Tamil minority by the Sinhalan majority in the 1950s through 1970s.[370] The insurgency was comprised of approximately ten thousand fighters in the 1980s and 1990s. They participated in direct armed conflict with the sizeable Sri Lankan army and also gained notoriety for their suicide attacks. The LTTE suicide bombing force is widely acknowledged to have invented the suicide jacket and carried out several hundred suicide attacks. While Sri Lankan civil war is considered an ethnic conflict rather than a religious conflict, there is debate on the relevance of religion in the hostilities, including whether religious nationalism was a relevant driver.[371] Throughout the insurgency women and girls known as the “Birds of Freedom” played an important role and needed to be considered in the reintegration efforts as well.[372] The existence of child soldiers added further complexity to the conflict.

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

The National Framework Proposal for Reintegration of Ex-combatants into Civilian Life (the Framework) was developed by a committee comprised of participants from: the Ministry of Disaster Management and Human Rights; the Ministry of Defence Public Security, Law and Order; the Ministry of Constitutional Affairs and National Integration; and the Ministry of Foreign Employment Promotion and Welfare. The International Labour Organization (ILO) lent both technical and financial support to the framework development process. In addition, previous efforts to develop rehabilitation strategies by the ILO and the Sub-Committee on Livelihoods of the Consultative Committee on Humanitarian Assistance (CCHA) and the Ministry of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources and the ILO as well as the Ministry of Employment and Labour were also taken into account when developing the Framework in 2009.[373] The Bureau of the Commissioner General for Rehabilitation, which had already been established during the conflict in the early 2000s, was tasked with the execution of rehabilitation efforts of terrorists and remains the main agency to oversee the reintegration facilities and activities up until today.[374]

 

Military Involvement

At the outset of the creation of the Framework, the military had recently defeated the LTTE. The role of military in the reintegration process was supposed to be primarily focused on disarmament efforts, including the collection and destruction of weapons in order to increase public safety and security. In furtherance of these goals, ex-combatants could not be employed by the military.[375] 

Security forces were also supposed to contribute to reconstruction and development in the conflict-affected areas. The Lessons Learnt Commission found later, however, that security forces continued to be involved in industries such as private businesses and agriculture that were not part of their mandate, which led to additional grievances for the local population. They described a feeling of unease due to the military’s continuing presence and authority (i.e. over the police forces) in the region and the lack of a civilian administration, which they deemed was crucial to feel more comfortable in their communities again.[376]

For military personnel installed in the reintegration facilities, extensive training and coaching regarding treatment of ex-combatants was mandatory. Based on the low number of reported incidents and reports of mistreatment of detainees in the PARCs, experts have commended the military participation in the reintegration efforts, overall.[377]

While the military in Sri Lanka has also been accused of human rights abuses and war crimes, similar to the conflict between Boko Haram and the Nigerian government, it is important to emphasize that the Sri Lankan army did not have a similarly prominent role in causing the conflict as the Nigerian security forces have had. Thus, a lower level of involvement of the security forces in Nigeria is advisable, while training of security forces in the respectful treatment of arrestees should be pursued to respond to the grievances that caused the conflict and ensure lasting peace.

 

International Agency/Expert Involvement

The ILO lent its technical and financial assistance to the development of the reintegration Framework.[378] The Framework considered international best practices, including lessons learned from Saudi Arabia and Singapore. Several experts from Singapore weighed in and high-level functionaries travelled to Singapore to learn from the program. One of the fundamental parts of the model are the six modes of rehabilitation that Sri Lanka adopted and then adapted to their local circumstances. These six modes included (1) education, (2) vocational reintegration, (3) psychosocial and creative therapies, (4) social, cultural and family reintegration, (5) the spiritual and religious modes and (6) recreational measures. Sri Lanka added one additional mode to the Singaporean model, community rehabilitation, which is why the Sri Lankan model is often called the “6+1 model”.[379]

 

NGO Involvement

NGOs were initially only part of the government’s agenda on a rather limited basis. One of the reasons for this decision could be that NGOs did not enjoy the full trust of the people during the conflict and that people had only little confidence in NGOs’ abilities to promote positive change and stay in the village communities on a long-term basis. In addition, NGOs were often perceived as partisan during the conflict and considered allies of the LTTE, which led to even more distrust by the Sinhalese and Muslim population.[380]

 

Impetus for Reintegration

The reintegration program in Sri Lanka was initiated between the LTTE insurgency and the Sri Lankan government towards the end of the civil war. Sri Lanka claimed victory and the LTTE declared defeat in May 2009, after which several thousand ex-combatants were captured and “surrendered.” Over the twenty-five years of the civil war, several other paramilitary groups had developed. These groups were also considered in the reintegration and rehabilitation efforts.[381]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

The reintegration efforts in Sri Lanka targeted both combatants that were captured as well as those who surrendered. Even though the program did differentiate between the two groups and recognized that each group needs different program designs, captives and those who had surrendered were granted the same rights to participate in the reintegration program. While the program primarily targeted ex-LTTE-combatants who were the largest group of people that were to be reintegrated, the program also targeted other paramilitary groups in the region that had emerged during the civil war.[382]

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

Transitional justice and punitive aspects of the reintegration process of LTTE combatants and other paramilitary groups have been a major object of criticism over the past seven years. While the Framework itself gave the Attorney General the responsibility of designing a transitional justice process tailored to the Sri Lankan experience, the Framework already stated that “[I]t has to be highlighted that, except in cases where a failure to prosecute violates a treaty obligation, the granting of amnesties in appropriate cases to perpetrators of egregious human rights violations is most likely not precluded by international law.”[383] This already signaled that the Sri Lankan government was not likely to prosecute human rights abuses of the Sri Lankan military. Up until today no credible process has been initiated. President Rajapaksa, who was president from 1995 through 2005 did not initiate any independent investigations for alleged crimes nor did he permit any prosecution of any military officials, disappointing the expectations he had raised with his promises.[384]

Forced disappearances played a major role in the Sri Lankan civil war, especially in the last four years of the conflict. The northeastern parts of Sri Lanka as well as the Colombo area were particularly affected by LTTE-led forced disappearances. Until mid-2016 no sincere action had been taken by the government to establish a transparent domestic process to address the disappearances.[385] But in August 2016, the Sri Lankan parliament voted in favor of a bill to create an independent Office on Missing Persons (OMP) comprised of seven members with experience in human rights and humanitarian law, whose mandate is to look into the large number of disappearances in Sri Lanka, specifically throughout the Sri Lankan civil war.[386]

 

Community Engagement

Community engagement played a significant role in the reintegration program in Sri Lanka. Due to the need to save on costs, communities bore significant responsibility for reintegration efforts. A variety of private companies also participated, particularly in the economic reintegration efforts. Throughout the entire reintegration process contact between former combatants and members of the community was encouraged. Such contact could manifest itself in letters, phone calls and visits. To further the participation of the community in the reintegration process, the BCGR held public reintegration festivities once former combatants were released. Upon reinsertion into the community, the responsibility to socially engage former combatants was slowly handed over to the local population in order to allow for a smooth transition back into civilian life for the combatants and a healthy relationship between the different groups.[387] Surveys of former combatants later showed that combatants felt welcomed and well recognized in the villages they returned to.[388]

Ex-combatants were reinserted into their communities of origin or at least into familiar regions to encourage re-engagement with their familial and cultural heritage, unlike Operation Safe Corridor where former combatants are placed in new and unfamiliar environments after they have completed their de-radicalization period. A common criticism that arose within the community was that former combatants received preferential treatment compared to Tamil civilians, because they had greater access to support services and facilities set up by the government, even though the local population had suffered from the decade-long conflict. Accordingly, efforts were made to combat this perception by avoiding special treatment of former combatants when possible.[389]

 

Psychological Elements

Ex-combatants were profiled before entering and upon completion of their reintegration programs. Initially, they were evaluated and classified for psychological counseling based on their crimes and their roles in the organizations. Counselors provided the government with reports on ex-combatants’ progress within two months. Support was available throughout the year-long program and after ex-combatants had been reinserted into the community.[390] It was crucial during the process to differentiate between investigation and rehabilitation to ensure confidentiality crucial to meaningful treatment. Moreover, staff training in ethics and respectful treatment of former combatants was fundamental to engender trust and mutual respect. The fact that beneficiaries felt satisfied with their treatment by facility staff (96.4%) and security personnel (94.6%) indicates that the strict separation between investigation and rehabilitation was successful.[391]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

Upon arrival at one of the 24 PARCs that the government installed in the northern and northeastern part of Sri Lanka, the ex-combatants were profiled psychologically and socio-economically. Based on their profiles, they were put into different groups that then focused on vocational training and skill development as well as spiritual classes that would facilitate social reintegration. This system also contributed to the program’s de-radicalization efforts.[392]

In general terms, the government found that rural areas would offer better employment prospects than cities. The main employment opportunities for direct employment of ex-combatants identified by the Framework were agriculture and livestock development, rural industrial development, fisheries, tourism, construction activities, micro-businesses, foreign employment and “green jobs” – meaning agricultural and manufacturing jobs.[393] The Framework emphasized that ex-combatants should have the freedom to choose which type of training they wanted to pursue. These choices included the option to found their own businesses and receive micro-loans.[394]

PARCs offered vocational training as well as higher education to ex-combatants. They could also receive language courses to make them more attractive for the labor market. Once ready for reinsertion into society, the ex-combatants were equipped with basic-needs kits that held a variety of non-cash resources, like household items, hygiene products, clothing etc. Cash payments were also made to the ex-combatants so that they would not return home empty-handed, thereby ensuring they would have some freedom of choice.[395] While this improved their ability to resettle, it also led to the dissatisfaction of other members of the community who did not enjoy the same access to monetary support.

 

Incentives

Ex-combatants of the reintegration program received cash payments for the rehabilitation period in similar amounts as IDPs and resettled communities’ cash grants. Extra costs due to disabilities were taken into account when calculating the amount paid to ex-combatants. There is no further financial assistance given to ex-combatants after the rehabilitation program.[396] The nature of the cash payments was different from the payments made as part of the Niger Delta Amnesty Program. As elaborated above, these payments were rather counterproductive and actually gave people additional incentive to participate in the insurgency. In Sri Lanka, however, the one-time cash payments were made in order to give former combatants an opportunity to resettle in their communities. It is important to note that the Sri Lankan civil war had ended in the defeat of the LTTE combatants, so the risk of a resurgence or of combatants re-joining the insurgency was deemed low. Since the conflict between the Nigerian government and Boko Haram is still an active conflict and because amnesty programs have a negative connotation in the Nigerian society, it would be unwise to draw a simplified comparison between Sri Lanka and Nigeria with regards to cash payments.

 

Follow-up

The ex-combatants were given basic legal documentation and identification cards. In addition to providing legal status to help monitor reintegration efforts, these documents also conferred beneficial psychological effects, and gave ex-combatants a sense of belonging.[397]

Cost Estimate

According to statistics published by the Bureau of the Commissioner General of Rehabilitation (BCGR), the program accumulated costs of USD 9,136,370 between January 2009 and September 2012 to manage and run the rehabilitation centers in Sri Lanka. The annual cost fluctuated depending on the number of ex-combatants in the rehabilitation program.[398]

 

Financing

Due to the fact that the government lacked financing for such a resource-intensive rehabilitation program, collaboration with the private sector was fundamental for this process. The BCGR established over forty partnerships with private companies in Sri Lanka that helped with custodial rehabilitation and community reintegration. A variety of companies offered vocational training programs for LTTE ex-combatants, including language classes, IT training, cooking courses, training in carpentry, masonry and other labor-intensive professions. Participating companies included Holcim (construction/manufacturing sector), Vusaa (IT sector) and a range of smaller local companies.[399] The funding issues that the government was facing actually led to a stronger involvement of the community in the reintegration program and to a program with lower cost.[400]

 

Results/Metrics

The Lessons Learnt Commission’s report was released in 2011 and showed that there were still several areas of concern regarding the execution of the rehabilitation programs. One issue was the lack of teachers that were able to provide the necessary training in the PARCs. While the behavior and effort of the PARC personnel overall was deemed commendable, it lacked instructors to further the staff training efforts.[401] The education in the camps was not perfectly tailored to the background of the ex-combatants. Another issue related to the availability of permanent shelter for ex-combatants, as they reintegrated back into communities with few financial means and the government had not provided sufficient facilities for shelter.[402] The report also found that security forces still intervened with civilians’ private and public life, even during the reconstruction period after the war.

In a survey to determine the success of the reintegration efforts by the Sri Lankan government, combatants gave feedback on their perception of the success of their reintegration a few years after the conflict had ended (published 2015). Using the sample of the northern cities Jaffna and Kilinochchi and Kulatunga, the report found that 90 percent of the ex-combatants felt that they were being well recognized in the villages. In these villages, the proportion of ex-combatants was at most 25 percent.[403] Furthermore, it was found that while some faced problems finding permanent employment, for the most part males integrated well back into society.[404] The unemployment and low level of income of ex-combatants is still an area of concern. However, this may be due to the environmental economic situation. Women, on the other hand, faced bigger challenges to reintegrate into society, because they were not allowed to use the skills they had acquired during their time as combatants in the labor market. Rather than being able to use their technical knowledge and physical abilities in professions like bricklaying or computer technicians, they were expected to be domestic helpers and seamstresses. Households who had to support returning female fighters did not receive sufficient government support, putting additional constraints on the families.[405] Kulatunga’s work also showed that leisure, free time and social events were highly relevant factors to make the reintegration process successful.[406]

 

Lessons Learned

A considerable area of improvement is the transitional justice process in Sri Lanka and the prosecution of those responsible for human rights abuses during the civil war. The president faces pressure to implement a transparent process.

Overall, security has greatly increased in Sri Lanka, and there has been a high level of post-war stability with no acts of terrorism since the end of the civil war in 2009.[407]

Several ex-combatants have had problems with unemployment and with setting up their own businesses, although the government had determined specific professions and geographic areas as more promising labor markets and provided training and assistance. These sectors, however, were selected intuitively rather than based on a thorough economic analysis of the labor market. Unemployment was not a problem that only or primarily affected former combatants, but rather a common problem in eastern and northeastern Sri Lanka. Rather than a lack of training and skills, it was the surrounding economic circumstances and the mismatch between the needs of the labor market and the qualification of former combatants that caused difficulties for former combatants to find employment. Therefore, it is important to accompany reintegration programs with investments into economic growth for the broader community rather than make adaptations to the reintegration program itself. New employment opportunities in the communities will have additional benefit by further strengthening community support of reintegration efforts. Furthermore, a thorough economic analysis of the needs of the labor market is necessary to ensure that former combatants have a better chance to find employment upon reinsertion. During such analysis, it is crucial to consider the medium term needs of companies, especially if economic reintegration and training efforts are carried out over several years.

The reintegration program in Sri Lanka had substantial issues finding qualified personnel that were proficient in the languages of former combatants. Finding suitable and qualified personnel for the reintegration facilities is crucial.[408]

Women, children and disabled ex-combatants seem to have experienced problems reintegrating back into society and have faced distinct issues of difficult family dynamics, alcohol problems and abuse.[409] Differentiating between different groups of beneficiaries is important. Long-term care for former combatants, especially women and disabled ex-combatants, to deal with changing family dynamics and social problems could ameliorate the situation for female and disabled beneficiaries.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

There are several parallels between the nature of the insurgency and the potential reintegration efforts that can be drawn between Sri Lanka and Boko Haram. However, there are also some crucial differences. The Sri Lankan civil war ended with the government forces defeating the LTTE combatants, while the conflict between the Nigerian security forces and Boko Haram is still ongoing. Given the different power balance, the attitudes towards reintegration of both the affected population and the combatants might be substantially different in each case.

Despite this stark contrast, both countries share many limitations, including financial constraints. Nigeria can learn from Sri Lanka’s strategy to involve the private sector strongly in the reintegration and rehabilitation efforts to offer meaningful vocational training opportunities. One way to accomplish that could include conducting a thorough analysis of the needs of the labor market before and during the execution of the reintegration program to determine which sectors could offer employment opportunities for former combatants in the future. The Nigerian government could receive funding from private enterprises, particularly in the agriculture and manufacturing sector, for education and training programs, which would also help sensitize the population for the rehabilitation process. In northeastern Nigeria processing facilities for agricultural products offer an opportunity for growth. They would not only bring employment, but also facilitate knowledge and technology-transfer, while at the same time taking advantage of the region’s fertile agricultural environment. Some ways to attract companies could be tax incentives and tax guarantees for long-term investors. If there were to be positive spillover effects for the local population resulting from the economic reintegration, these measures would potentially be substantially more effective.

An additional parallel between the Sri Lankan conflict and the Boko Haram insurgency is the prevalence of suicide attacks and child soldiers, which complicates the conflict. When it comes to reintegrating child soldiers into the community, Sri Lanka took the approach to plan a separate program for children, that lasted ten to twelve months. Distinguishing by gender and age would be beneficial when it comes to the reintegration of former Boko Haram combatants, because it would allow the psychological and community elements of a reintegration program to be tailored to the specific groups being reintegrated. In Sri Lanka, child soldiers were taken from rural families, which contributed to an unwillingness to accept ex-combatants back in their communities. Given the trauma that the families in the communities had suffered, raising community support was crucial. As is pointed out in the discussion of Operation Safe Corridor, receiving the support of the community through the engagement of local leaders could be specifically helpful. Inviting community members to actively participate in the reintegration process as was done in Sri Lanka through public festivities and joint activities, could be an additional path to community acceptance.
