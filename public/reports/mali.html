Mali: Lessons from the Reinsertion of Ex-Combatants Project

Gertrude Ansaaku & Garret Nash

 

Abstract: Located in West Africa, Mali shares notable geopolitical similarities to Nigeria and the Boko Haram conflict. Both contexts involve economic and political marginalization of northern territories, organized responses which revolve around independent states and religious rhetoric, and violent conflict. Unlike Nigeria, Mali has successfully brokered a peace agreement between the government and rebel factions. Yet a year later, violence in Mali continues. There is continued tension between the government of Mali and the still marginalized north, although violence is not restricted to that area. Furthermore, the full weight Mali’s DDR plans are yet to be carried out, resulting in a population losing faith in the government’s ability to end violence. Many rebel groups are still in possession of weapons. In order to discuss how the Mali model could be applicable to Nigera’s Boko Haram conflict, this section first provides a brief overview of Mali and events leading up to the current conflict. Then, an overview of the 2015 Algiers Agreement DDR strategies as outlined in the 2015 peace agreement are then discussed. Finally, a comparison between Mali and Boko Haram is given. Lessons learned from Mali, which can be applied to the Boko Haram context in Nigeria, concludes this section. 

 

Model Name

The reintegration effort in Mali is called the Mali Reinsertion of Ex-Combatants Project, short for Mali Disarmament, Demobilization and Reintegration and Security Sector Reform. There is a cantonment phase, or encampment of ex-combatants, which has similarities with the Operation Safe Corridor in Nigeria for disarmament before the Reinsertion (or Reintegration). While some of the ex-combatants will return to civilian life, others will be integrated into the security forces.

 

Time Period

The armed conflict began in Mali in March 2012. Although a peace agreement was brokered in May/June 2015, no timeline for a successful DDR program was established. Currently, disarmament has yet to occur and conflict remains sporadic.[76] According to human rights watch, violence has spread from northern regions southward, including to Bamako, the capital.[77] Mali gained international attention when in February of 2016, almost ten months since the signing of the Algiers Agreement, jihadists took 170 hostages, (20 of which were killed) in the capital city.[78]

Background

Mali, a landlocked West African country, is a product of European colonialism. With present-day Senegal, it fell under French colonization in the late 1800s. Its current form, boundaries, institutions, systems and structures reflect a colonial heritage distinct from its pre-colonial nature, which was characterized by trade and nomadic movements. It gained independence in 1960 -- the same year as Nigeria. Its population is estimated at 17.5 million. About 90 percent of Malians are Muslim, with traditionalists and Christians in the minority.[79]

The Republic of Mali faced frequent rebellions, coups and twenty-three years of military dictatorship until democratic elections in 1992. Although Mali is self-sufficient in food and one of Africa’s major cotton producers, its chronic foreign trade deficit makes it heavily dependent on foreign aid and remittances from Malians abroad.[80] About 30 percent of its population is unemployed. Although agriculture is a major backbone of its economy, Mali faces recurring droughts, dust-laden harmattan haze (especially during the dry season), occasional Niger River flooding, deforestation, soil erosion, desertification, and inadequate supply of potable water.[81],[82] There are issues of physical insecurity, high population growth, corruption in government and public service, weak infrastructure and low levels of human capital, which hinder sustained economic growth.[83]

The following sections look at the nature, scope and causes of Mali’s northern insurgency and how the DDR process in Mali is progressing after the peace agreement was reached. The aim is to identify similarities and differences with Boko Haram in Nigeria in order to determine the applicability of the Mali DDR model to Nigeria. The last section offers policy recommendations for the reintegration of Boko Haram into the Nigerian society based on the challenges and lessons learnt from Mali.

 

The Northern Insurgency

Mali’s northern insurgency dates back to the Tuareg rebellions of 1963, 1990-91 and 2006. In these cases, demands were made on Mali for northern secession amidst the declaration of an independent Azawad. Peace settlements reached were not adhered to, which led to distrust, suspicion, anger and frustration on the part of the northerners. In particular, provisions on the grant of some autonomy to the north were violated several times until January 2012, when another northern rebellion erupted.[84]

The 2012 insurgency was sparked by widespread dissatisfaction and frustration over the deprivation and marginalization of the North, and unfulfilled promises made by the Government of Mali to resolve earlier rebellions.[85] Unlike the past, this insurgency has taken a more radicalized form.[86] A Tuareg rebel group called the Mouvement Nationale pour la Liberation de l’Azawad (MNLA) started attacks in the northern towns of Menaka, Tessalit, and Aguel’hoc in January 2012.[87] The rebellion also spread to Gao, Kidal, and Timbuktu, all in the north. Most of the rebels were returnees from Libya, after serving in Muammar Gaddafi’s army before the civil war that led to the collapse of the Gaddafi regime. A military coup which ensued in March 2012 after a military encounter with the rebels gave room for a take-over by a loose coalition of jihadist groups linked with the Al-Qaeda and the Tuareg rebels.[88] The major among them were the Ansar Dine and the Mouvement pour l'Unicité et le Jihad en Afrique de l’Ouest (MUJAO).[89] The insurgency differed from earlier ones in the way it was radicalized with religious (Islamic) undercurrents and involved well-armed cohorts of rebels. The rebels considered their insurgency a jihadist mission.[90]

 

International Involvement and Peace Settlement

In early January 2013, the jihadist group Ansar Dine and MUJAO captured Konna, a town strategically close to to the capital city, Bamako.[91] The transitional government of Mali asked for aide, which spurred a French-led intervention.[92] French and Chadian troops drove them away from major towns in the North and restored the territorial integrity of Mali. Subsequently, a ceasefire agreement was reached in Ouagadougou in June of 2013 with the rebel groups paving the way for the democratic elections which brought the new president, Ibrahim Boubacar Keita, and a new legislature to office.[93]

Later, an African Union (AU) mission established there was repurposed as the United Nations Multinational Integrated Stabilization Mission in Mali (MINUSMA). There was also a European Union (EU) training mission and security assistance from the United States (US) and France in the form of a counter-terrorism campaign (Barkane) which targeted cross-border jihadist movements in Mali and four other Sahel countries.[94]

Mali’s peace talks experienced several deadlocks due to disagreements between the government and key rebel groups over the creation of a Malian federation and ambiguities and rivalry among the various diplomatic initiatives led by MINUSMA, ECOWAS, AU, Algeria, Burkina Faso, and Morocco.[95] A peace agreement was finally reached in Algiers in June 2015, two years after the ceasefire agreement.[96]

The Algiers Agreement was signed between two coalitions of several Tuareg rebel groups; the Coordination of Azawad Movements (CMA) and the Platform of armed groups (the Platform). However, foreign-origin jihadist groups (al-Qaida in the Islamic Maghreb [AQIM] and the Movement for Unity and Jihad in West Africa [MUJAO]),  who controlled of most of Azawad from June 2012 to January 2013, did not sign the Agreement.[97] This was because they “[insisted] that they need time to explain the agreement to and to convince their supporters in northern Mali”.[98] Also not represented was the Imghad Tuareg and Allies Self-Defence Group (GATIA), as they emerged after peace talks had begun.[99] The Agreement provided for direct elections of local representatives to enhance local participatory governance, disarmament, demobilization and reinsertion (DDR), security sector reform (SSR), and establishment of conditions for restoring stability and economic recovery in Northern Mali.[100] Areas which have seen new administrations – prefects and sub-prefects – are in the regions of Gao, Kidal, Menaka, Taoudenni, and Timbuktu.[101]

         Further hindering the Algiers Agreement is continued violence in Mali.[102] For example, in November of 2015, jihadists took 170 from the Radisson Blu Hotel in Bamako.[103] 20 hostages were killed during the conflict. Later, in February of 2016, a UN based in Kidal was attacked, which resulted in the deaths of five UN Peacekeepers and wounded many others.[104]

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

Although yet to be put into practice, the Algiers Agreement outlines several roles for civilian agencies (albeit no specific organizations are mentioned). These functions include those related to human rights advocacy. Attention is accorded to justice officials who are well trained in violence against women and girls, the aim being to respect of the rights of women, children and other victims of conflict.[105] Leaders of Malian society, especially women and youth, are to help influence dialogue between communities and help reconciliation. Civil society and law professionals are to be enlisted to help reduce corruption and impunity in the justice system.[106]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

It is unclear exactly how many combatants will be integrated into the security forces and at what time. However, a World Bank grant will finance ten thousand cases of ex-combatants.[107], [108] Additional funds are allocated to reintegrate 7,500 of these ex-combatants.[109] There seems to be substantial pressure both from within Mali and the international community for forced disarmament.

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

A Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission (CVJR) was established in January 2014 to replace the former Dialogue and Reconciliation Commission (CDR).[110] The CVJR is responsible for investigating crimes committed between 1960 and 2013 (since independence) and make recommendations to the government for prosecution. Its mission remains to contribute to peace consolidation and national unity.[111] Transitional justice processes have intermittently been marred by violence from sections of the rebel groups.[112] Perpetrators of the most heinous crimes are to be taken to the International Criminal Court (ICC).[113] There would be no amnesty for those suspected to have committed some named crimes, as per the Algiers Agreement. However, to be able to get some rebel groups to sign the peace agreement, arrest warrants against fifteen suspected criminals who were to face charges of sedition and terrorism were lifted. Some others were also released from detention in Bamako.[114] One top rebel named Ahmad Al Faqi Al Mahdi was held in trial from August 22 to 24, 2016. He was charged with the war crime of destroying religious and historical artefacts in Timbuktu, Mali in June/July 2012. He pleaded guilty, and asked that his people in Timbuktu and Northern Nigeria forgive him for the harm caused. On September 27, 2016, the ICC sentenced him to a nine-year imprisonment.[115]

 

NGO and Community Involvement

The Algiers Agreement invites the involvement of humanitarian agencies and organizations to aid in returning, repatriating, and reintegrating all persons displaced by the conflict.[116] No specific organizations are mentioned nor is a timeline for their involvement given.  The Agreement contains provisions for a semi-autonomous Northern Mali and direct elections[117] However, no guidelines for community involvement in reintegration of ex-combatants are  given.

Financing and Cost Estimate

The DDR is funded by the Government of Mali and international donors, including the World Bank. In April 2016, the World Bank voted a $15 million grant for the DDR project in Mali. Technical, logistical, training and supervisory support have also come from the United Nations, European Union, ECOWAS, African Union, France and Chad.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

The Mali case has some similarities and differences with Nigeria’s Boko Haram case. Both Mali and Nigeria are in the West African sub-region. Both are products of colonialism and share the challenge of having to keep their constituent parts together within their states. The two share most of the conditions under which conflict and violent extremism thrive in their respective northern areas: poverty, lack of education, unemployment, ungoverned spaces, exclusionary politics, marginalization and neglect by government. The two also share the problem of unfavorable climatic conditions which make nomadic and farming activities difficult, if not impossible. Both cases have jihadist (Islamic) undercurrents, although at different levels. Again, Nigeria’s Boko Haram is said to be working in collaboration with the Ansar Dine and MUJAO in Mali.[118] Mali’s DDR is ongoing and could be a contemporary of Nigeria if the latter is ready to go the way of the former, and to speed up processes for a peace settlement. In that case, the challenges faced by the former could serve as lessons for Nigeria.

It appears that the extent to which religion plays out significantly in the Nigeria case is minimized in Mali. While Nigeria’s population seems to be evenly divided between Muslims and Christians, Mali is over 90% Muslim. Although in the latter, there are jihadist fighters alongside Tuareg rebel groups, Boko Haram and its splinter groups in northern Nigeria are mainly jihadists. Also, Mali’s DDR seems to be focused on the Tuareg rebels who went into the negotiations and signed the peace agreement. It appears the jihadists groups are yet to be captured in the entire process. However, if Nigeria is to undergo peace negotiations and a DDR process, it would have to do so with jihadists.

Again, in Mali, both the government and rebel groups agreed to the peace settlement, however reluctantly. It is not clear in the Nigerian case whether the government is ready for a political settlement. Also, although the military is not neutral in the conflict, it seems to be playing a role in initial efforts to disarm and demobilize ex-combatants who have voluntarily surrendered to the Operation Safe Corridor. If Nigeria is to go the Mali way, it would first have to be ready to negotiate with the Boko Haram, make compromises, and agree to have the pressing issues driving the insurgency addressed.

 

Summary, Conclusions and Recommendations for Nigeria

Mali, like most African states, suffers from most of the postcolonial challenges of nation-building. Like Nigeria, it has been through some years of civil wars and insurgencies, particularly in the northern regions. The inability of successive governments to adhere to the provisions of peace agreements, coupled with marginalization and neglect of the north have been a major cause of instability in the north. Northern Mali battles with unfavorable socio-economic conditions, political exclusion, and poor climatic conditions. The result has been built-up dissatisfaction, distrust, and suspicion leading to a series of rebellions. Mali’s northern insurgency has ethnic, and like northern Nigeria’s, religious undercurrents. For the fourth time, Mali is on the road to peace, after the signing of a peace agreement between the government and rebel groups. Jihadist fighters were not included in the negotiations, although they were also visible actors in the northern insurgency. If the Algiers Agreement is strictly adhered to and the DDR process goes well, with its many challenges addressed, Mali could be on a steady road to lasting peace.

With regard to its applicability to Nigeria, Mali offers a warning as to the consequences of delay. If a peace agreement is brokered between Nigeria and Boko Haram, the Mali model shows that the Nigerian government would have to be conscientious of timelines. We consider delays in Mali’s DDR efforts as a primary factor in continued unrest and waning trust of the government to resolve conflict. We see a strong possibility of similar trends in Nigeria if DDR is not followed through swiftly following a peace agreement. Of course, we cannot say if the Mali model would be successful in Nigeria, as its own success or failure is yet to be seen. Rather, we hope that Nigeria will consider the example of Mali and avoid similar errors.
