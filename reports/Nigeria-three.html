Niger Delta: The Presidential Amnesty Program for Militants

Ibeabuchi Chukwuma

 

Abstract: The Presidential Amnesty Program (PAP) was launched in 2009 by former President Umaru Musa Yar’adua in the Niger-Delta region.[60] The region produces almost all of Nigeria’s crude oil and is made up of following states: Abia, Akwa Ibom, Bayelsa, Cross River, Delta, Edo, Imo, Ondo and Rivers. PAP was created after years of agitations by militant groups in the region. The region has long held grievances over oil, pollution, environmental degradation, underdevelopment and the destruction of marine life from oil exploitation and exploration, which has resulted in loss of local livelihoods.[61]

 

Insurgency Description

The Niger-Delta region of Nigeria, comprising nine oil-producing states, sustains the Nigerian economy with crude oil. This represents 98 percent of the country’s exports, over 80 percent of government's annual revenue and 70 percent of budgetary expenditure.[62] However, the region has experienced socio-economic challenges from the “resource curse,” a phenomenon that includes environmental degradation, oil pollution, poverty, and loss of daily livelihood for communities in the region. Over the past twenty years, both peaceful and violent protests from different groups in the region over resource control have emerged. A coalition of Niger Delta militant groups, including the Niger Delta People’s Volunteer Force and Niger Delta Vigilante, began to mobilize against the oil companies in 2003. By 2004, several of these groups coalesced to form the Movement for the Emancipation of Niger Delta (MEND). In January 2006, “MEND declared war on the oil industry pending the resolution of long term political grievances relating to poverty and underdevelopment, the poor regulation of an environmentally polluting oil industry, and the alienation of local people from rights to land and resources in the Niger Delta.”[63] Militant groups initiated attacks on oil industry infrastructure, kidnapped oil company personnel, and engaged in illicit oil bunkering and sale of crude oil. These actions significantly affected revenue and foreign exchange for the federal government; it is estimated that the government lost 100 billion dollars in revenue from 2003 to 2008 and suffered a drop in crude oil exports to 1.6 million barrels per day (bpd) in March 2009, down from 2.6 million in 2006.[64]

The Presidential Amnesty Program

In August 2009, President Umaru Yar’Adua issued a sixty-day amnesty offer “for any fighter willing to come out of the region’s million-square-mile network of oil-laden creeks and swamps, turn in his weapon and accept a $13 daily stipend in exchange.”[65] The amnesty package had an initial budget of N50-52 billion ($145 million) but later rose to N68 billion and was designed for 20,192 registered militants.[66] In the amnesty program, a presidential pardon, rehabilitation programs, education and training were offered to militants.[67] Any militants who surrendered arms, left the insurgency and registered with the government were deemed to be qualified for the Presidential Amnesty Program (PAP) and receive the payouts.

 

Amnesty Institutions

A number of federal government agencies were directly involved in the PAP. These agencies, including the Niger Delta Development Commission and Federal Ministry of Niger Delta Affairs, were created in 2009 to formulate and coordinate development and security initiatives in the region and provided technical support to the PAP.[68] President Umaru Yar’Adua later established the Presidential Committee on Amnesty and Disarmament of Militants, headed by the Minister of Defense – Maj. General Goodwin Abbe, a native of the Niger Delta, with a mandate to coordinate the disarmament process.[69] However, intense political competition among these agencies slowed the implementation and other aspects of the amnesty program.[70] The Joint Military Task Force, or “Operation Restore Hope,” was established in 2003 by the federal government to restore peace and prevent oil disruption in the Niger Delta, provided security during the amnesty implementation.[71]

 

Rehabilitation

Following the appointment of Timi Alaibe as new Chairman of the Presidential Amnesty Committee, work on the rehabilitation training center in Obubra, Cross River State, and public information campaigns about the amnesty program started in earnest.[72] NGOs and civil society organizations, including the NGO Foundation for Ethnic Harmony in Nigeria, in partnership with the University of Rhode Island’s Center for Nonviolence and Peace Studies, ran nonviolent conflict resolution training sessions. Unfortunately, these programs were deemed ill-suited for the needs of the ex-combatants.[73] The training initially focused on conflict resolution and peacebuilding initiatives, but ex-militias demanded skills for gainful employment. Consequently, educational and vocational classes were arranged for the ex-militants in foreign sites, such as Houston, London, Seoul and South Africa.[74]

 

Lessons Learned and Applicability to Boko Haram

The amnesty program succeeded in establishing a fragile ceasefire in the region. “Subsequent [sic] attacks on the oil industry, losses from oil bunkering, and the kidnapping of expatriate oil company personnel have declined, whilst oil production is climbing back up to 2006 output levels 2.2 million bpd.”[75] Nonetheless, the PAP was unfortunately initiated with competing political interests. Allegations of lack of transparency, widespread corruption and inflating cost plagued the program. Furthermore, the program inadvertently incentivized militancy: in order to receive a government payout, any disgruntled individual in the Niger Delta need only commit terrorist acts and subsequently request amnesty. Because of the exorbitant cost, flawed design, and the continued lack of sustainable economic development in the region, the program failed to achieve a lasting peace. While it achieved a degree of success in disarmament of the ex-militants, it lacked concerted effort in their full reintegration back into civil society. As a result, Niger Delta militias recently began resurfacing in the region, with attacks on oil infrastructure and kidnappings on the rise.

While full presidential amnesty was implemented in the Niger Delta, a similar program is unlikely to apply to Boko Haram in the northeastern Nigeria. Before President Buhari and the National Assembly conclude that a similar amnesty is worthy of duplication, government leadership should take into account the profound differences in the two insurgencies, including the devastating famine, the millions of internally-displaced persons, and thousands of civilian deaths. Moreover, the high cost of implementing the amnesty program in Niger Delta region should be a cautionary tale to a federal government with severe economic issues. Lastly, the Niger Delta insurgency has proven that there is no guarantee of prevention of recidivism: Niger Delta militants appear to be the rise once again. Although the PAP was successful in achieving a short-term peace in the region with cash payments, it did not address the long held grievances aforementioned. For instance, ex-militants trained abroad in the vocational skills and higher education returned home and faced recurrent paucity of opportunities in the region. Without a more comprehensive reintegration program and holistic strategy that addresses the variety of drivers of insurgency, northeastern Nigeria could once again descend into utter chaos with monetary rewards to ex-fighters. After years of relative peace, the Niger Delta region appears poised to do just that.
