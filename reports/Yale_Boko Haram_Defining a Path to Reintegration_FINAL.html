EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

 

Background

Since the early 2000s, Boko Haram – also known as Jama’atu Ahlus Sunnah Lid Da’awati Wal Jihad, or People Committed to the Prophet’s Teachings for Propagation and Jihad – has been a constant source of instability in northern Nigeria. Emerging in 2002 under the leadership of Mohammed Yusuf, the group began its resistance movement against the secular Nigerian government largely through proselytization and other non-violent means, interspersed with occasional “hit-and-run attacks” on police stations and security outposts throughout Nigeria’s northeastern states.[1] Following a strong government crackdown in 2009, which resulted in Yusuf’s highly publicized extra-judicial killing, Boko Haram has increasingly shifted strategies, “adopt(ing) the tactics of global Salafi Jihadist groups, including targeted assassinations, suicide bombings, and hostage taking.”[2] Beginning in 2011, the frequency and severity of the group’s attacks continued to grow, reaching its peak in 2014-2015.[3]

Compounding factors have complicated the Nigerian government and international community’s response to the Boko Haram insurgency. Dismal socio-economic prospects in northern Nigeria have left citizens feeling neglected and resentful of what they perceive to be a corrupt southern government;[4] environmental degradation has created a humanitarian emergency for thousands in the region;[5] and perceptions of Muslim marginalization have potentially increased the number of insurgency sympathizers.[6] Meanwhile, the Nigerian military has been criticized for its heavy-handed management of the conflict - accused of numerous human rights abuses against Nigerian civilians in its attempts to weaken the insurgency.[7] Internal dissent within Boko Haram itself has further complicated the government and international community’s response; numerous splinter factions within Boko Haram have demonstrated conflicting ideologies and competed for authority in Nigeria’s northern states.[8] These complications have made any attempt at a focused response to the insurgency more problematic.

Since 2015, government forces have been steadily reclaiming land in the northeast, leading many experts to assert that Boko Haram is now on the defensive.[9] Hundreds of former combatants are said to have voluntarily defected, leaving the Nigerian government with the difficult task of upholding justice and peacefully reintegrating former Boko Haram militants into local communities, all while simultaneously putting pressure on existing combatants to lay down their arms. Despite previous experience with reintegration and amnesty programs in Nigeria - e.g. in the Niger Delta region - the government has sought guidance from international actors regarding potential transitional justice strategies to address the Boko Haram challenge.

 

Report Structure

Lessons Learned

Through our research, several themes emerged regarding the key challenges and impediments to reintegration. In many cases, there was no movement toward reintegration prior to a formal peace deal between the government and insurgent group. This is particularly concerning given the lack of progress toward political settlement in Nigeria. Additionally, lack of public trust in the government and military, often explained by a history of human rights abuses, created additional barriers to reintegration. Lastly, we observed that disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration (DDR) programs which emphasize punitive measures without offering a path to a productive life can restart the cycle of disenfranchisement, which often precedes violent insurgency.

However, these reintegration models also illuminate a wide range of opportunities for the current conflict in Nigeria. Reintegration allows countries to formally involve underrepresented and disenfranchised groups and repair relationships with non-governmental community leaders. Reintegration models also offer economic incentives to ex-insurgents and communities alike, by building job skills and stimulating the economy. Because of the increasing interdependency of nations, reintegration not only requires a whole-of-government approach, but the disputing parties can often leverage the expertise of neighbors and the international community to create a sustainable solution.

KEYS TO REINTEGRATION SUCCESS

Throughout the course of this research, we have observed myriad trends that have led to the success or failure of previous reintegration programs. This section seeks to outline the most pertinent elements and their applicability to the Nigerian context. The following is not meant to be understood as direct policy recommendations, but rather as strategic frameworks from which to draw when considering the feasibility of any action taken towards Boko Haram. It is our hope that international actors, as well as Nigerian state officials, will consider these trends when contemplating future actions.

 

All reintegration models discussed in this report have involved experts in diplomacy, demobilization, de-radicalization, reintegration, criminal justice, human rights and cultural inclusion -- working proactively with governments on reintegration processes. We believe that a similar approach would benefit Operation Safe Corridor. International expertise in Nigeria could ensure that Operation Safe Corridor has credibility, and will facilitate international buy-in and financial support for Nigerian efforts to reintegrate former Boko Haram combatants. We believe forming an international monitoring mission will have positive collateral consequences and will facilitate long-term sustainability of any reintegration process. In conjunction with and in support of the monitoring mission, we outline the following trends which may positively influence success in Nigeria:

 

Communicate National Priorities

_      Invoke Strategic Leadership: Given the large-scale security risks and impediments to economic development in the northeastern Nigeria caused by the Boko Haram insurgency, the success of any reintegration efforts requires the leadership and full political commitment of the Nigerian government. The reintegration effort must go hand-in-hand with a comprehensive program for humanitarian relief.

_      Foster Nigerian-Led Solutions: Collaboration can spell success or failure in any reintegration effort. Success in Nigeria will be enhanced by the Nigerian government establishing and empowering an interministerial task force, led by the Ministry of Justice, to coordinate the reintegration program and effectively address the humanitarian situation in northeastern Nigeria. Yet this task force should avoid being military-dominated, as it must include representatives from throughout Nigeria and across the political and religious spectrum.

_      Seek International Assistance: Because of the widespread accusations of human rights abuses by the Nigerian security forces and the popular perception of disenfranchisement in northeast Nigeria, international assistance is vital to resolve the conflict and to restore credibility in the Nigerian government. We turn to the example of the Aceh Monitoring Mission in Indonesia as an example of what to do during a reintegration process. Similarly, a monitoring mission in Nigeria would function as a neutral third-party to: 1) promote compliance with international law; 2) strengthen existing Nigerian institutions; 3) improve the appeal of the reintegration process for ex-combatants; and 4) build confidence in foreign investors to facilitate economic reconstruction; 5) promote peaceful transition of ex-combatants into civil society. International assistance would also amplify concurrent efforts to combat the devastating famine.

_      Forge Political Consensus: No reintegration attempt can be successful without political transparency and popular support. Although reintegration is highly contentious, any reintegration program would need to be debated from the National Assembly to the local community level to ensure legitimacy, national support, and the appropriation of sufficient government funds. Villages and communities must understand the nature of the transitional justice and invest in reintegration. In order to achieve lasting success, communities must be willing and prepared to receive former Boko Haram victims and combatants, with appropriate incentives as necessary.

_      Mobilize Popular Support: Reintegration efforts are doomed if governments make no effort to gauge community support for reintegration efforts through a public information campaign. Given the controversial nature of the reintegration process, the highest levels of government must promote the reintegration effort to the Nigerian public as the foremost national security priority. Success would involve communities that commit to support of reintegration being first in line to receive the benefits of foreign investment.

 

Establish Transitional Justice Mechanisms

_      Establish a Truth Commission: Building on the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) model, we recommend establishing a truth-seeking body. The body should be overseen by the international monitoring mission and engage all parties to the ongoing conflict in dialogue – former Boko Haram combatants, Nigerian security forces, community leaders, and victims alike. By acknowledging the collective atrocities of the past, this body can ensure accountability, serve justice, and facilitate reconciliation.

_      Prosecute Worst Offenders: We suggest that all former combatants be profiled by a team of psychology, human rights, and criminal law experts, in order to categorize combatants according to the depth and length of their involvement with Boko Haram - thereby categorizing leaders versus low-level fighters in the insurgency. Following the Sri Lankan model, ex-combatants identified by the interdisciplinary team as highly involved in the insurgency should undergo a judicial process whereby they are sentenced to prison time, if sufficient evidence of their involvement is found. Such prosecutions shall be carried out by a special terrorism court, established within the existing framework of the Nigerian judicial system.

_      Allow Conditional Amnesty for Truth Telling: Again, building on the South African model, we argue that former Boko Haram combatants who have been deemed less highly involved should have the opportunity for conditional amnesty through whole-truth telling, in order to bolster intelligence gathering to combat the ongoing insurgency.

_      Practice Leniency for Child Soldiers: In recognition of the Paris Principles, the Nigerian government should consider child soldiers victims rather than perpetrators. Criminal prosecution of these child soldiers should be pursued only for war crimes or crimes against humanity.

_      Hold Security Forces Accountable: Finally, Nigerian security forces must be held accountable for their heavy-handed tactics against Boko Haram, including the extrajudicial killing of Mohammed Yusuf. They must also be held accountable for the human rights abuses committed against civilians of Nigeria’s northeastern states. While the Nigerian government may wish to avoid criminal prosecution of its soldiers, it is our recommendation that punitive measures be taken against those who performed the most egregious violations of human rights. Additionally, voluntary involvement in the TRC body and commitment to whole-truth telling will be a vital step toward reestablishing community trust and a precondition for amnesty. Statements made by Nigerian security forces should be available to the public and be corroborated by the international monitoring mission. Security forces who choose not to participate in the TRC will be at risk of future criminal prosecution for their actions.

 

Implement Rehabilitation and Reintegration Programs for Affected Civilians

_      Incorporate Similar Programs for Civilians: Just as Boko Haram fighters must ultimately be rehabilitated and reintegrated into Nigerian society, the physical and psychological damage of those affected by Boko Haram’s violence and Nigerian security forces’ reprisals must also be addressed. A rehabilitation and reintegration program for civilians should occur simultaneously with one designed for former insurgents.

_      Provide Community-Based Reparations: In order to both gauge community support for the reintegration of former combatants and to build a more positive economic climate in northeastern Nigeria, affected communities should be offered reparations in the form of public investments into infrastructure and the provision of public goods and subsidies to training programs. The Government may consider instituting a public service component for reintegration, so that Boko Haram ex-combatants can help alleviate the hardships that Boko Haram helped create.

_      Address the IDP Crisis: Currently, only approximately ten percent of the estimated 2.5 million internally displaced persons (IDPs) reside in IDP camps; the rest are forced to live in “host communities” -- essentially shantytowns with limited resources. More resources, primarily from international organizations (IOs), non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and other non-state actors must be brought into northeast Nigeria to establish additional IDP camps.

_      Address Food Scarcity: Food scarcity in northeast Nigeria is also a growing concern. Nearly one million Nigerians are severely acutely malnourished; an estimated 50,000 to 75,000 children under five years of age are expected to die of malnutrition in the near future if nothing is done quickly. Therefore, as with the IDP crisis, Nigeria’s food crisis must be addressed through IOs, NGOs and other non-state actors.

_      Offer Vocational Training and Civil Schooling: Many IDPs left homes and jobs behind when they fled Boko Haram. Vocational training and educational opportunities should be provided in IDP camps and throughout the various “host communities” in northeast Nigeria. This will provide IDPs with a vocation or set of skills that can be transferred to jobs when they return to their communities.

 

Engage Traditional Leaders and Institutions

_      Rebuild Traditional Institutions: Traditional leaders and institutions -- religious leaders, community leaders, and councils -- have been marginalized and supplanted by elected politicians, particularly in northeastern Nigeria. This has resulted in less community resilience and cohesion and allowed for the infiltration of Boko Haram into northeastern communities. To make matters worse, in many areas, Boko Haram has systematically decimated traditional leaders and institutions. Reestablishing these leaders and institutions is essential to a successful program and to guard against future violent extremist groups. While some areas require traditional leader empowerment, others require total reconstruction from the grassroots level.

_      Incorporate into Decision-Making: Traditional leaders and institutions should be part of a formal decision making process to determine the placement of former combatants into specific communities. This will assist with community buy-in and guard against possible retribution from community members.

_      Incorporate Xeer Model from Somalia: For reconciliation at the family and community level, we recommend the adoption of the Xeer method (discussed in the Somalia model). This provides a way for family members, traditional leaders, and traditional institutions to become actively involved in the rehabilitation and reintegration process.

_      Allow Traditional Religious Leaders to Determine De-radicalization: Religious leaders should inform the creation of the determinants of de-radicalization prior to an ex-combatant’s release from a de-radicalization program. Many northeastern community members do not trust that Operation Safe Corridor provides adequate de-radicalization counseling and are unwilling to welcome former militants back into their communities. Incorporating religious leaders in the de-radicalization process and allowing them to determine de-radicalization criteria will prove crucial for community acceptance.

_      Encourage Communications Link between Traditional Leaders and Government: Traditional leaders and institutions should become the primary means of communication between their respective communities and other forms of local, state, and federal government. This will ensure a link between communities that accept Boko Haram militants with government mechanisms. Additionally, this link will help solidify traditional leaders and institutions and their roles in moving Nigeria beyond Boko Haram and violent extremism.

 

Determine the Physical Process

_      Hybrid Approach to Reintegration: As elaborated previously, it is crucial that any reintegration efforts not only focus on ex-combatants, but also approach reintegration from the community level. Thus, a hybrid approach to reintegration, in which ex-combatants remain in a secured facility for a certain amount of time for the purpose of counselling and de-radicalization and pursue vocational training outside the facility, can build bridges between combatants and the affected communities.

_      Facility Management: Each reintegration facility should be led by a team of specialists from the monitoring mission, interministerial task force, representatives of civil society organizations, psychologists, independent justice officials, medical professionals, and an expert on women’s affairs.

_      Length of Reintegration Program: Given the level of indoctrination of Boko Haram combatants, we do not believe that twelve weeks is sufficient for ex-combatants to remain in the facility. A longer period is recommended and should be specified by the panel responsible for profiling combatants on an individual basis.

_      Differentiation between Beneficiaries: There should be adequate facilities to cater to different groups. Specifically, we recommend differentiation by level of threat, gender, and age.

_      De-radicalization through Collaboration: De-radicalization efforts should be led by psychologists and be supported by local traditional and religious leaders. By particularly including local leaders and experts into the process, de-radicalization will be more effective, as beneficiaries can identify better with the personnel because language and cultural barriers are reduced.

_      Assessment before Reinsertion: Before beneficiaries are reinserted into communities, an assessment by a panel responsible for their profiling and counselling should be made, giving a clear recommendation and evaluation on each beneficiary’s status.

 

Address Human Rights

_      Institute Public Service for Food Security: In recognition of the relevance of agriculture in northern Nigeria and the severe food insecurity in the region, beneficiaries should be obligated to participate in public service to remedy any consequences created by the insurgents. Such service could include working on farms and assisting the transportation of farm goods to markets.

_      Apply a Climate Change Lens to Economic Reintegration Efforts: Failing to consider the impact of climate change on livelihoods when demobilizing Boko Haram fighters and providing them with vocational training -- such as through the current Operation Safe Corridor model -– may eventually drive them back to insurgency. Developing climate resilient livelihoods for not only ex-Boko Haram fighters but all people in the northeast, which is deeply impacted by climate change and desertification, is vital to insure a stable, sustainable peace.

_      Support Climate Change Adaptation Efforts through Regional Institutions: We recommend that the Nigerian government continue to work with the Lake Chad Basin Commission to implement its Development and Climate Action Plan, to support conservation and sustainable livelihoods on the basin/watershed level through the provision of technical assistance on best farming, fishing, and grazing practices. By supporting these efforts, the Nigerian government will help to establish long-term sustainability in the region and build community resilience.

 

Focus on Long-Term Sustainability

_      Address Security Sector Reform: We recommend that the Nigerian government, in coordination with the international monitor, implement Security Sector Reform (SSR) for the Nigerian security forces to improve compliance with international standards and human rights laws, as well as to build trust between the military and Nigerian community. The monitoring mission inherently supports further international assistance and assists the Nigerian government in modifying the methods, processes, and outcomes of the Nigerian military forces.

_      Combat the existing “Boko Haram mindset” to reduce recruitment: There is no reason that groups like Boko Haram cannot continue to emerge, grow, and flourish unless the underlying components of Nigerian society that make these groups appealing to young people are addressed. We recommend that in madrasas, additional non-controversial topics, such as math, are introduced into the curriculum. We also recommend that the monitoring mission work with the almajiri, and begin a discussion on whether or not secular education can, or should be implemented in the region. We also recommend that the monitoring mission seek to advocate for further women’s education - this is the first step in combatting poverty, maternal death, and overpopulation of unemployed youth, all of which act as motivators to join Boko Haram. Building an economic base for these communities will allow reintegration efforts to be more effectively monitored, and will also increase the likelihood of community forgiveness.

_      Promote foreign investment -- financially and politically: We recommend that the monitoring mission work to ensure that Nigerian Foreign Direct Investment is increased, and allocated appropriately to reintegration efforts. In showing international support and solidarity for the long-term viability of ex-combatant reintegration, the monitoring mission will inherently align with the Nigerian government to encourage Boko Haram combatants to lay down their arms, given the investment in de-radicalization and reintegration. The effects of such partnership will be powerful in reintegration processes, both in the immediate sense, and as Nigeria seeks to build a future without insurgency.

 

 

 

 

 

 

INSIDE NIGERIA

Operation Safe Corridor: Nigeria’s Attempts to Reintegrate Boko Haram

 Justin W. Crocker

 

Abstract: Nigeria’s conflict with Boko Haram, ongoing since 2002 and reaching its peak of violence in 2014-2015, has caused more than 20,000 civilian deaths and the displacement of nearly 2.7 million refugees.[10]  As recent successes by the Nigerian military and Multinational Joint Task Force (MNJTF)[11] continue to reduce much of the area once dominated by Boko Haram, the Nigerian government in Abuja now struggles with ways to manage surrendered or captured insurgents. In September 2015, the Federal Government of Nigeria (FGN) established Operation Safe Corridor (OSC), a program designed to rehabilitate surrendered Boko Haram insurgents and eventually reintegrate them back into Nigerian society.  Specifically, OSC has been designed to counter Boko Haram’s radical interpretation of Islam, address many of the psychological factors faced by insurgents freshly off of the battlefield and provide vocational training that will assist former militants return to their communities as productive citizens.  However, while OSC shows positive steps for the FGN, the program has significant flaws that must be addressed.  The program is only designed for surrendered militants, is currently under resourced, and fails to address many of the human rights abuses perpetrated by Nigerian security forces.  This section will further explore OSC, outline the FGN’s goals for the program, and identify both challenges and potential solutions for OSC. Lastly, this section will evaluate OSC’s suitability as the continued method to rehabilitate and reintegrate members of Boko Haram back into Nigerian society.

 

Background of Boko Haram in Nigeria

            Discovery of massive deposits of commercial-quality oil in the Niger Delta in 1956 quickly propelled Nigeria to a more prominent geopolitical status. Today, while Nigeria’s economy is the largest in Africa and one of the fastest growing in the world, most Nigerians are getting poorer.[12]  According to the data from Nigeria’s National Bureau of Statistics (NBS), poverty rates rose from 42.7 percent in 1992 to 65.6 percent in 1996 to a staggering 69 percent in 2010.[13]  NBS statistics also show that northern Nigeria, the heartland of Boko Haram and rising Islamic fundamentalism, is disproportionately affected by poverty and unemployment.[14]  It was this socio-economic backdrop that prompted Islamic cleric Mohammad Yusuf to establish the Ibn Taimiyyah Masijid (Mosque) in Borno state’s capital of Maiduguri in 2004.[15]  Yusuf, a charismatic and energetic Salafist Muslim cleric, drew a large following by preaching against the oppressive Nigerian government. He blamed western education for corrupting Nigeria’s leaders and advocated for an Islamic society based on Sharia law. Yusuf’s messages resonated profoundly with many northern Muslims. Young, illiterate and unemployed men proved to be particularly susceptible. By 2007, Yusuf had attracted a significant following that included people from neighboring Niger, Chad, and Cameroon.[16] Yusuf and his followers remained relatively quiet until July 2009, when clashes with police over a period of four days resulted in over eight hundred people killed. Among the dead was Yusuf; however, his extrajudicial murder by police (which went viral because it was captured on videotape) became a catalyst for future violence.

            Boko Haram withdrew into the shadows but reemerged in mid-2010 with a much more violent identity under a new leader: Abubakar Shekau. In September 2010, the group conducted a well-planned attack on a prison in Bauchi in northern Nigeria, freeing more than seven hundred inmates, many of whom were Boko Haram members. Seven separate bombings on Christmas Eve in 2010 in Jos—a major city in Nigeria’s central region—killed at least thirty-two churchgoers. The violence continued to escalate and spread throughout Nigeria, and included one of Boko Haram’s first suicide attacks on the UN compound in Abuja in August 2011. The attack claimed twenty-three lives and resulted in another eighty-seven wounded. The group’s kidnapping of 276 girls from a school in Chibok, a remote town approximately seventy miles south of Maiduguri, in April 2014 propelled Boko Haram into international notoriety. The abduction prompted the EU and the UN to place the group on their respective terrorist organization lists. By early 2015, Boko Haram controlled most of Borno state and many areas in neighboring Yobe and Adamawa states in northeast Nigeria.

 

What is Operation Safe Corridor?[17]

            While the program was approved in concept under the Goodluck Jonathan administration in as early as 2013, significant progress establishing OSC -- location, cadre, structure of the deradicalization and trade training -- did not occur until mid to late-2015. Through research in Nigeria and a series of interviews with a member of the OSC staff, we have learned that specific goals of OSC include:

1.     Provide members of Boko Haram with an alternative to fighting.

2.     Assist the FGN in implementing a formalized disarmament, demobilization and reintegration (DDR) program in Northeast Nigeria and the greater Lake Chad Basin.

3.     Provide surrendered Boko Haram insurgents with deradicalization counseling and vocational training, thereby providing Nigeria with a trained and productive labor force.

4.     Assist in ending hostilities in northeast Nigeria.

 

Operation Safe Corridor is physically located in a Nigerian military camp in Borno state’s capital of Maiduguri. Currently, approximately one thousand former Boko Haram militants are said to be in the program, of which approximately eighty percent are male and twenty percent are female. The average age of the participants is said to be approximately thirty years old. OSC consists of a twelve-week de-radicalization and vocational training program involving thirty-seven Nigerian agencies. These agencies include, but are not limited to: Department for Counter Violent Extremist at the Office of the National Security Adviser National Emergency Management Agency (NEMA), National Orientation Agency, National Youth Service Corps, Nigeria Identity Card Management Commission, Federal Ministry of Health, Ministry of Interior, National Directorate of Employment, and Nigeria Prisons Services. While predominantly civilian agencies run the program’s de-radicalization and trade training programs, the Nigerian military controls OSC’s leadership and security mechanisms. Additionally, participation in OSC is reserved for those who surrender to Nigerian authorities. Captured Boko Haram militants--defined here as those who do not physically surrender--are placed in other detention facilities and are currently not eligible for OSC enrollment. We are unaware of exactly how Nigerian security forces distinguish between surrendered militants from those who are captured. Such information was not disclosed during our research in Nigeria nor by our interviewee.

The de-radicalization portion of OSC consists of psychological counseling and religious training. Newly surrendered militants are given initial psychological assessments to determine the extent of treatment required, particularly for younger militants and those who were forcibly conscripted into Boko Haram. Specific psychological counseling methods could not be determined by our research. Interestingly, OSC authorities claim to have incorporated religious leaders and scholars from the international community, notably Pakistan, into religious de-radicalization efforts. Moderate Salafist clerics and leaders perform de-radicalization training by speaking directly with former militants and focus on countering many of the distorted versions of Salafist Islam.[18]  The OSC authority with whom we spoke indicated that the primary component of OSC is religious de-radicalization. However, we did not identify any specific metrics used to initially and then subsequently evaluate participants at the end of the program.

The vocational portion of OSC involves training in predominantly low-skilled trade (e.g., agriculture, wood- and steel-working, as well textile manufacturing). This training is intended to provide former militants with skills necessary to become self-reliant once they are deemed de-radicalized and prepared for reintegration back into Nigerian society. Additionally, vocational training in a useful trade can ease reintegration into otherwise resistant communities. Finally, self-sufficiency theoretically addresses a few of the socio-economic reasons for initially joining Boko Haram.

From what could be discerned, surrendered militants are psychologically evaluated at least twice during their twelve weeks in OSC; once during their initial entry into the program and once at the culmination of the program to determine whether or not they are prepared to reintegrate back into Nigerian society. We could not discern specifically how OSC officials determine whether a former militant is effectively de-radicalized. According to our authority, approximately thirty percent of the militants currently entering OSC are deemed to not be completely de-radicalized after the twelve weeks. Those determined unfit for reintegration are placed back into the program, but it is unclear whether they complete the twelve-week program again or are inserted into a follow-on program on a case-by-case basis. Those who are determined to be prepared for reintegration are sent to unfamiliar communities for a “fresh start” and an opportunity to reestablish themselves. Specific numbers of those who had been effectively rehabilitated and reintegrated through OSC could not be discerned. Each community's willingness to welcome former militants into their society was also unclear.

 

Challenges with Operation Safe Corridor

Although much regarding OSC is still publicly unknown, we were able to discern some of its potential challenges and pitfalls. While most Nigerian civilians understand the merits of a program like OSC, many more have displayed strong resistance to the program. Most northern Nigerians believe that the harms caused by Boko Haram should be addressed before former militants receive treatment and vocational training. Others fear the risk of recidivism to be too high to reintegrate former insurgents back into communities in the near term. How the FGN addresses these concerns will be vital in ensuring OSC or a similar program is successful; without support from those citizens and communities charged with ultimately reintegrating former militants, any such program will undoubtedly fail.

Boko Haram’s insurgency ravaged northeastern Nigeria and dramatically altered the lives of millions of Nigerians. As mentioned earlier, nearly three million Nigerians have been forced from their homes. Many of these Nigerians are forced to live in under-resourced, internally-displaced persons (IDP) camps or “host communities”—essentially shantytowns with extremely limited means of life support—and are draining already scarce resources. Additionally, as more areas of northeast Nigeria become accessible as a result of increased security force operations, a growing humanitarian crisis is emerging. The latest report from the United Nation’s Office for the Coordination of Human Affairs (UNOCHA) estimates that over seven million people are in need of emergency life-saving assistance, of which an estimated 4.4 million remain in unsecure or inaccessible areas due to ongoing violence.[19]  An estimated one million northeast Nigerians suffer from severe acute malnourishment, including 250,000 children under six years of age. Médecins Sans Frontières and UNOCHA officials warn that unless immediate steps are taken to improve security and access in northeast Nigeria, fifty thousand children will likely die.[20]  For many northern Nigerians, these problems created and perpetuated by Boko Haram must be addressed before surrendered militants benefit from a program such as OSC. To do otherwise, in the eyes of many Nigerians, would amount to rewarding former Boko Haram militants for their violence while simultaneously punishing the millions of Nigerians affected by the conflict.

During a discussion among community and government leaders from Borno state in Maiduguri in June 2016, many of the participants expressed concern about the high risk of recidivism among militants deemed de-radicalized by OSC authorities and reintegrated into their communities. Specifically, most were skeptical of OSC’s ability to determine whether a former militant continued to hold radical religious views. Would a former militant subsequently be capable of resisting rejoining Boko Haram or similar violent extremist groups such as Ansaru and Al Qaeda in the Islamic Maghreb (AQIM)? Most participants believed reintegration in the near term was dangerous and called for a ten-year period to allow the population to heal first before reintegration should begin. While the feasibility of a ten-year period is likely unrealistic, the stakeholders’ sentiment and strong resistance to reintegration in the near term displays a strong lack of Nigerian community acceptance of the program. Therefore, we believe that OSC must be implemented in conjunction with similar programs aimed at developing vocational skills and access to jobs for the millions of IDPs affected by Boko Haram. Psychological counseling and rehabilitation must also be afforded to those who have witnessed Boko Haram’s violence and violent security force operations. Understandably, many Nigerians are strongly opposed to Boko Haram and, left unaddressed, can significantly inhibit public acceptance of OSC’s legitimacy. Establishing a rehabilitation and reintegration program in which both former militants and civilians can participate is critical to achieving public support for OSC.

Another challenge of OSC is that, while thirty-seven different civilian agencies are involved, the program is perceived to be a military-run initiative. OSC is commanded by a Nigerian Army Brigadier General, has a predominantly military officer staff, and it is physically located on a military camp guarded by Nigerian soldiers. Nigeria’s military has been widely criticized for its brutality and disregard for human rights, particularly during its fight with Boko Haram. In a highly critical report on Nigerian security force war crimes and human rights violations, Amnesty International documented twenty-seven extrajudicial killings committed by security force members in 2013 and 2014 alone. The report claimed that at least twelve hundred people, mostly men and young boys, had been killed and another seven thousand had died in under-resourced and overcrowded detention facilities.[21] The most notorious of these incidents occurred in March 2014 after Boko Haram attacked a detention facility at Giwa Barracks in Maiduguri and released an estimated seven hundred prisoners. In the days that followed, Nigerian soldiers and members of the Civilian Joint Task Force (CJTF) began house-to-house searches of local residences in an effort to recover the released prisoners. Amnesty International’s investigation presented overwhelming evidence that at least 640 men and boys, most of whom were released during the Giwa Barracks attack, were extra-judicially killed by soldiers and CJTF volunteers.[22]  

Given this history of abuse, it is highly unlikely that many Boko Haram militants will willingly surrender to Nigerian security forces. The benefits of surrender and participation in OSC must outweigh the potential cost of continuing to fight under Boko Haram’s banner. But as long as the Nigerian military is involved in OSC, it will be difficult for militants to willingly surrender. It is important for OSC’s future that military involvement be limited, with the ultimate goal of being completely removed in the near future. As a first step, physical security for OSC’s camp should be transferred to Nigeria’s National Police Force, Civil Defense Corps, or another security agency run and operated by Nigerian civilians. Additionally, the OSC camp should be immediately removed from a military base and transferred to an area deemed acceptable by the new agency responsible for physical security. These changes will immediately and tangibly display Nigeria’s sincere commitment to rehabilitating and ultimately reintegrating surrendered militants back into Nigerian communities. The militants will no longer perceive the OSC as a trap to overcrowded and under-resourced prisons, but as providing a real and sustainable path forward.

In addition to limiting military involvement, OSC officials must also address the legal status of surrendered Boko Haram members and strike the difficult balance between retribution and rehabilitation. Public support for OSC is limited due in large part to northern Nigerians’ demands for retribution for the harms perpetrated by Boko Haram. Amnesty for surrendered militants, regardless of whether their involvement with Boko Haram was forced, is nearly unanimously rejected by Nigerians. Prosecuting thousands of militants individually and fairly, however, will prove difficult due to the lack of clear evidence on individual fighters and the FGN’s limited legal resources. But blanket punishment, regardless of the extent of each militant’s involvement in the insurgency, is also an unsuitable answer as it will undoubtedly decrease the number of those willing to surrender. Many low-level fighters and supporters of Boko Haram, particularly those forced into the group or coerced into directly supporting the group’s activities, may be unwilling to surrender if their punishment is equal to that of mid-level leaders or those who took part in rape, murder, and other war crimes. The FGN must develop a robust method for delivering prosecutorial justice to Boko Haram militants while simultaneously avoiding a homogenous approach that disproportionately alienates low-level fighters and supporters. Without striking this careful balance, both public support and militant willingness to surrender and participate in OSC will likely prove inadequate.

 

Conclusion

Operation Safe Corridor has many egregious flaws that can and must be addressed if the program is to be successful. OSC represents a positive step forward for the FGN, which just two years ago seemed completely incapable of defeating Boko Haram militarily. The inauguration of President Muhammadu Buhari in May 2015 ushered in a reinvigorated effort against Boko Haram and reversed six years of the group’s gains. However, Boko Haram will not be defeated with military force alone. Similar to other successful counterinsurgencies, Nigeria will have to incorporate a way for Boko Haram militants to lay down their arms and eventually return to Nigerian society. We believe OSC represents the beginning of such a program and should be built on using lessons learned from conflicts throughout the world. With assistance from international governments and other non-state actors, OSC could prove to be the program most responsible for bringing Boko Haram’s seven-year insurgency to an end.

 

Climate Change, Violence, and Boko Haram in the Lake Chad Basin Region

Abdul-Majeed Ibrahim & Lauren Wyman

 

Abstract: Lake Chad is the largest lake in Central and West Africa, and is the source of livelihoods for roughly thirty million people in Nigeria, Chad, Cameroon, and Niger. In the last six decades, the lake has been shrinking, and presently covers only one twentieth the area it covered in the 1960s. This is due to both anthropogenic stressors as well as climate change, contributing to over-exploitation, resource scarcity, loss of livelihoods and conflict over land and water on a regional level. This fragile humanitarian situation exacerbates the risks posed by the Boko Haram militancy, as already vulnerable populations are forced to migrate from their lands. This vulnerability also contributes to strengthening Boko Haram, as stranded youth may be tempted to join out of economic necessity rather than for ideological reasons. Policies and programs to reintegrate Boko Haram fighters into society should consider the humanitarian and ecological context at play - socioeconomic stabilizers have been decimated. Resilient and self-reliant communities are needed for reintegration programs to be sustainable. Tools to strengthen community and ecosystem resilience include climate-smart agriculture, livestock insurance and agro-advisories.

 

Key Words: climate change, adaptation, desertification, livelihood loss, gender, Lake Chad, violence

 

Background and Context

Lake Chad is the largest lake in Central and West Africa, bordered by four countries – Nigeria’s northeast, Cameroon’s far north, Chad’s Lac region (western Chad) and Niger’s southeast.[23] The Lake Chad basin encompasses 966,955 km2 of floodplains, wetlands and aquifers.[24] The population in the Lake Chad region has more than doubled over the last decade and about thirty million people in the basin depend on the lake for their livelihoods.[25] Subsistence fishing, agriculture and livestock farming are the primary economic activities in the region, which is a hub for trade and commerce.

In the last six decades, the lake has been shrinking, and along with it the quality of life and source of income for the region’s population. The lake presently covers 1,350km2, only one twentieth the area it covered in the 1960s.[26] The United Nations Environmental Programme attributes 50 percent of the lake decrease in size to overuse by humans for irrigation and the rest from changing climate and rainfall patterns.[27] Historic data shows that the lake has recovered from shrinking on multiple occasions, but scientists doubt this will occur due to anthropogenic stressors (i.e. conditions resulting from human influence on the environment) and climate change.[28]

 

Impact of anthropogenic stressors and climate change

Over the last half-century, Lake Chad has been shrinking due to over-exploitation and manipulation by national governments and by local populations (see Figure 1 below). In particular, damming of source rivers like the Chari River and the Komadugu-Yobe for irrigation and fresh water have interrupted the lake’s natural flow regime. Additionally, overgrazing, land-clearing for agriculture and poor farming practices have reduced basin soil quality and water retention, thus putting undue pressure on the basin’s natural resources.[29] An over reliance on extractive practices like fishing has led to biodiversity loss in the lake and decreased system resilience. As the population living around the lake has increased rapidly over the past decade, due to normal population growth as well as forced migration, competition for the lake’s resources has made it nearly impossible for the lake to recover on its own.[30]

Climate variability, shocks and long-term changes compound the human-derived environmental degradation in the Lake Chad basin. Specifically, the Lake Chad Basin is one of the most climate-sensitive regions on the African continent. There is high inter-annual rainfall variability, and the average yearly rainfall has decreased over the last century.[31]  The region alternates between periods of high rainfall and flooding and drought, which makes agriculture and pastoral livelihoods highly vulnerable. Temperatures in the region over the last forty years have increased six degrees Celsius during the dry season.[32] This is compounded by desertification, which is encroaching in the northern part of the lake.[33] Over the course of this century, increasing temperatures and evapotranspiration of the Lake will reduce available water by up to another ten percent.[34] Climate models predict a shift in ecosystem composition around the lake, with arable lands decreasing by up to 135,000 km2 by 2099.[35]

Figure 1: Chronology of Lake Chad variability: 1960 to 2007. Source: UNEP (2004).[36]

 

Conflict Over Shrinking Natural Resources

Seasonal fluctuations -- recession and inundation of the floodplains -- make the lake a good habitat for fish as well as fertile ground for farming. Lake recession exposes the lake bed’s fertile soils for farming, and during inundation, nutrients are restored to the soil. Flood recession cropping is a major production system in the Lake Chad Basin, where farmers mostly cultivate sorghum and berbere peppers.[37]

The shrinking of the lake has created tensions among competing resource users - fishermen, farmers, and cattle herders. Farming activities on the exposed lake bed modifies the aquatic community in the lake.[38] Specifically, ploughing close to the lake increases siltation and eutrophication, reducing lake depth and causing fish kills from toxic algae blooms.

The modified aquatic ecosystem alters species biodiversity and the number of fish species that are able to inhabit the lake. Fish evade fishing nets by hiding under marshes and tall grasses growing in the lake due to siltation. Fishing thus becomes difficult and fishermen lose their fishing gear to marsh weeds. In response to this pressure, fishermen start to disregard national boundaries and migrate towards other national territories to fish.[39]  Some fishermen turn to farming to take advantage of the exposed fertile lakebed, which contributes to competition for farmland with traditional farmers. Adding to the mix, pastoral communities clash with farmers as traditional pasture lands have been encroached by farmers and, likewise, cattle often destroy farmland through trampling when they move to drink water from the lake. Farmers, fishers, and cattle herders migrate toward and compete for the shrinking lake’s resources without regard for the imaginary boundary lines.[40] Resource extraction has intensified as a result and crude methods are being used in resource extraction to maximize output.

 

Climate Change as a Threat Multiplier for Violent Extremism in the Region

Boko Haram has taken advantage of porous borders of the Lake Chad basin countries and expanded to Cameroon’s far north, Niger’s southeast, and Chad’s Lac region.[41] Suicide bombing and raids on communities have displaced an estimated 2.3 million people (as of November 2016) in the Lake Chad basin region, of which 1.9 million are in Nigeria’s northeast.[42] By displacing primarily agricultural populations, the insurgency exacerbates climate-driven food shortages -- affecting the fragile food and nutrition security situation of the region, where 6.3 million people were food insecure as of August 2016.[43] Boko Haram interferes with markets and transportation infrastructure, thus further complicating food shortages and access of vulnerable populations.

Communities hosting IDPs face resource competition and land overuse as the influx of people exacerbates food, shelter and livelihood needs.[44]  Forced displacement in this context strips victims of their social and physical capital and networks, undermining their ability to cope with the conflict and the changing climate.[45] 

The impacts of resource scarcity and the humanitarian crises in the region are cyclical, however, as lack of economic opportunity and the prevalence of hunger has led to many Nigerians joining Boko Haram. In fact, some foot soldiers in the Boko Haram sect have been identified as people displaced by severe drought in the regions.[46] While Boko Haram started in urban areas, hunger and natural resource competition may have contributed to young Boudouma men joining the group.[47]  See Figure 2  for a schematic on how Boko Haram contributes to, and is exacerbated by, the humanitarian and climate situation in the Lake Chad Basin.

Figure 2: How does Boko Haram exacerbate climate impact on populations in the Lake Chad Basin region? Climate change on its own causes variable rainfall and drought, which shrinks the lake, and loss of biodiversity, intensive resource extraction and migration. Boko Haram activities cause forced migration, violence, destruction of infrastructure and destruction of natural resources which exacerbates climate impact. A climate change-Boko Haram interaction exacerbates the effects of each on the population by causing natural resource competition and degradation, food insecurity and conflict. Source: the authors.

 

National and Regional Environmental Policies and Management Bodies

President Buhari has made climate change a national priority for Nigeria - promoting sustainable growth while reducing carbon dioxide emissions on the international, federal, regional and local levels, across sectors. However, there are gaps in climate change adaptation coverage, particularly on the local level and in the security and defense sectors.

On an international level, Nigeria is party to the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCC) and signed the Paris Accords in September of 2016. On a federal level, Nigeria has a national bureaucratic infrastructure for environmental protection, including Nigeria’s Climate Change Policy and the national development plan, Vision 2020.[48]

On a regional level, the government of Nigeria has partnered with nine West African countries to develop sustainable regional climate change policy and research, through the West African Science Service Centre on Climate Change and Adapted Land Use (WASCAL). The Lake Chad Basin Commission (LCBC), founded in 1964, is a regional mechanism to settle natural resource disputes, promote conservation and regional development and peace and security in the region. However, the LCBC has struggled to carry out its mandate due to lack of funding and technical capacity.[49] Additionally, there is little cooperation between the four countries on lake resource management.[50]

On the national level, the National Assembly has various committees that focus on climate change, natural resources and Lake Chad. Specifically, in the Senate there are the following committees:  Climate Change; Poverty Alleviation and Social Welfare; and Water Resources; in the House, there are: Climate Change; Environment and Habitat; IDPs, Refugees and Initiatives on the North East; Lake Chad; and Water Resources. The government has recognized the need to more effectively mainstream climate change into other governmental sectors, particularly in development planning.[51]

On a local level, there are a variety of adaptation projects spearheaded by regional organizations like the Lake Chad Basin Commission, such as the organization’s Development and Climate Action Plan. However, the implementation of consistent Nigerian governmental climate change adaptation projects in conflict zones, particularly in the northeast, have fallen short due to lack of adequate infrastructure, political will and the presence of Boko Haram. While Nigerian policy recognizes the intersectionality between the current conflict with Boko Haram and natural resource clashes due to climate change, it lacks political impetus to approach defense and security issues with a climate change adaptation lens.[52]

 

Climate resilient livelihoods - toolkit

Traditional rain-fed agricultural and pastoral livelihoods are highly vulnerable to a changing climate.[53]  However, there are a variety of tools that farmers and pastoralists can adopt to reduce the impacts of climate change on their livelihoods. A few suggestions are: 

1.     Climate-smart agriculture (CSA) is a common mechanism to improve livelihood resilience to climate change by adapting agriculture to increasing rainfall variability, decreasing water availability for irrigation and higher prevalence of extreme weather events.[54] CSA sustainably increases productivity by utilizing drought-resistant and high yield crops, intercropping and evaporation prevention through cover crops.[55] In the National Agricultural Resilience Framework (NARF), Nigeria’s Federal Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development outlined a path for the country to adopting and implementing climate-smart agricultural innovations and technologies.[56]

 

2.     Index-based livestock insurance is a common tool whereby cash payments are made to cattle herders if their livestock die during drought. The International Livestock Research Institute has successfully implemented pilot projects in Kenya and it is now being done in Ethiopia.[57]

 

3.     Weather Information Services and Agro-advisories on best agronomic practices - best sowing time, soil quality management, fertilizer application, integrated pest management, crop residue management - sent via automated voice messages can help farmers adapt their agricultural livelihoods to changing rainfall patterns and cropping season. [58]  Following the model of India’s  Integrated Meteorological Advisory Service (IAAS), the Nigerian Meteorological Agency (NIMET) can provide weather forecasts and the agricultural universities in northern Nigeria can convert the weather forecast into appropriate farm-level advisories.[59] The farm-level advisories can then be disseminated through local radio stations, and telecom giants like MTN, Globacom and Airtel.


Concrete recommendations:

1.     Create cross-sectoral working groups in the National Assembly to address counter-terrorism with a climate lens. Any counter-terrorism strategy must promote a cross-sectoral approach that considers the wide-ranging impacts of a changing climate. Thus, in the National Assembly it is important to create a cross sectoral committee to tackle counter-terrorism in the northeast. One possibility is to expand the scope of the current committee on IDPs, Refugees and Initiatives on North East in the National Assembly to involve individuals from the Climate Change, Lake Chad and the Environment committees.

 

2.    Support environmental conservation efforts through the Lake Chad Development and Climate Resilience Action Plan. It is important to work with the Lake Chad Basin Commission to implement its Development and Climate Action Plan, to support conservation and livelihoods on the basin/watershed level and to build community resilience through the provision of technical assistance on best farming, fishing and grazing practices.

 

3.    Apply a climate change lens to reintegration programs. The government of Nigeria should push to further incorporate the climate change lens into conflict and peacebuilding policies and actions in northeastern Nigeria, building off of the National Agricultural Resilience Framework. Failing to consider the impact of climate change on livelihoods when demobilizing Boko Haram fighters and providing them with vocational training - as through the current Operation Safe Corridor model - may drive them back into the insurgency. Developing climate resilient livelihoods that utilize tools such as climate-smart agriculture and livestock insurance in the northeast, which is the most impacted region from climate change and desertification, is vital to ensure a stable, sustainable peace.


 

Niger Delta: The Presidential Amnesty Program for Militants

Ibeabuchi Chukwuma

 

Abstract: The Presidential Amnesty Program (PAP) was launched in 2009 by former President Umaru Musa Yar’adua in the Niger-Delta region.[60] The region produces almost all of Nigeria’s crude oil and is made up of following states: Abia, Akwa Ibom, Bayelsa, Cross River, Delta, Edo, Imo, Ondo and Rivers. PAP was created after years of agitations by militant groups in the region. The region has long held grievances over oil, pollution, environmental degradation, underdevelopment and the destruction of marine life from oil exploitation and exploration, which has resulted in loss of local livelihoods.[61]

 

Insurgency Description

The Niger-Delta region of Nigeria, comprising nine oil-producing states, sustains the Nigerian economy with crude oil. This represents 98 percent of the country’s exports, over 80 percent of government's annual revenue and 70 percent of budgetary expenditure.[62] However, the region has experienced socio-economic challenges from the “resource curse,” a phenomenon that includes environmental degradation, oil pollution, poverty, and loss of daily livelihood for communities in the region. Over the past twenty years, both peaceful and violent protests from different groups in the region over resource control have emerged. A coalition of Niger Delta militant groups, including the Niger Delta People’s Volunteer Force and Niger Delta Vigilante, began to mobilize against the oil companies in 2003. By 2004, several of these groups coalesced to form the Movement for the Emancipation of Niger Delta (MEND). In January 2006, “MEND declared war on the oil industry pending the resolution of long term political grievances relating to poverty and underdevelopment, the poor regulation of an environmentally polluting oil industry, and the alienation of local people from rights to land and resources in the Niger Delta.”[63] Militant groups initiated attacks on oil industry infrastructure, kidnapped oil company personnel, and engaged in illicit oil bunkering and sale of crude oil. These actions significantly affected revenue and foreign exchange for the federal government; it is estimated that the government lost 100 billion dollars in revenue from 2003 to 2008 and suffered a drop in crude oil exports to 1.6 million barrels per day (bpd) in March 2009, down from 2.6 million in 2006.[64]

The Presidential Amnesty Program

In August 2009, President Umaru Yar’Adua issued a sixty-day amnesty offer “for any fighter willing to come out of the region’s million-square-mile network of oil-laden creeks and swamps, turn in his weapon and accept a $13 daily stipend in exchange.”[65] The amnesty package had an initial budget of N50-52 billion ($145 million) but later rose to N68 billion and was designed for 20,192 registered militants.[66] In the amnesty program, a presidential pardon, rehabilitation programs, education and training were offered to militants.[67] Any militants who surrendered arms, left the insurgency and registered with the government were deemed to be qualified for the Presidential Amnesty Program (PAP) and receive the payouts.

 

Amnesty Institutions

A number of federal government agencies were directly involved in the PAP. These agencies, including the Niger Delta Development Commission and Federal Ministry of Niger Delta Affairs, were created in 2009 to formulate and coordinate development and security initiatives in the region and provided technical support to the PAP.[68] President Umaru Yar’Adua later established the Presidential Committee on Amnesty and Disarmament of Militants, headed by the Minister of Defense – Maj. General Goodwin Abbe, a native of the Niger Delta, with a mandate to coordinate the disarmament process.[69] However, intense political competition among these agencies slowed the implementation and other aspects of the amnesty program.[70] The Joint Military Task Force, or “Operation Restore Hope,” was established in 2003 by the federal government to restore peace and prevent oil disruption in the Niger Delta, provided security during the amnesty implementation.[71]

 

Rehabilitation

Following the appointment of Timi Alaibe as new Chairman of the Presidential Amnesty Committee, work on the rehabilitation training center in Obubra, Cross River State, and public information campaigns about the amnesty program started in earnest.[72] NGOs and civil society organizations, including the NGO Foundation for Ethnic Harmony in Nigeria, in partnership with the University of Rhode Island’s Center for Nonviolence and Peace Studies, ran nonviolent conflict resolution training sessions. Unfortunately, these programs were deemed ill-suited for the needs of the ex-combatants.[73] The training initially focused on conflict resolution and peacebuilding initiatives, but ex-militias demanded skills for gainful employment. Consequently, educational and vocational classes were arranged for the ex-militants in foreign sites, such as Houston, London, Seoul and South Africa.[74]

 

Lessons Learned and Applicability to Boko Haram

The amnesty program succeeded in establishing a fragile ceasefire in the region. “Subsequent [sic] attacks on the oil industry, losses from oil bunkering, and the kidnapping of expatriate oil company personnel have declined, whilst oil production is climbing back up to 2006 output levels 2.2 million bpd.”[75] Nonetheless, the PAP was unfortunately initiated with competing political interests. Allegations of lack of transparency, widespread corruption and inflating cost plagued the program. Furthermore, the program inadvertently incentivized militancy: in order to receive a government payout, any disgruntled individual in the Niger Delta need only commit terrorist acts and subsequently request amnesty. Because of the exorbitant cost, flawed design, and the continued lack of sustainable economic development in the region, the program failed to achieve a lasting peace. While it achieved a degree of success in disarmament of the ex-militants, it lacked concerted effort in their full reintegration back into civil society. As a result, Niger Delta militias recently began resurfacing in the region, with attacks on oil infrastructure and kidnappings on the rise.

While full presidential amnesty was implemented in the Niger Delta, a similar program is unlikely to apply to Boko Haram in the northeastern Nigeria. Before President Buhari and the National Assembly conclude that a similar amnesty is worthy of duplication, government leadership should take into account the profound differences in the two insurgencies, including the devastating famine, the millions of internally-displaced persons, and thousands of civilian deaths. Moreover, the high cost of implementing the amnesty program in Niger Delta region should be a cautionary tale to a federal government with severe economic issues. Lastly, the Niger Delta insurgency has proven that there is no guarantee of prevention of recidivism: Niger Delta militants appear to be the rise once again. Although the PAP was successful in achieving a short-term peace in the region with cash payments, it did not address the long held grievances aforementioned. For instance, ex-militants trained abroad in the vocational skills and higher education returned home and faced recurrent paucity of opportunities in the region. Without a more comprehensive reintegration program and holistic strategy that addresses the variety of drivers of insurgency, northeastern Nigeria could once again descend into utter chaos with monetary rewards to ex-fighters. After years of relative peace, the Niger Delta region appears poised to do just that.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

COUNTRY MODELS

Mali: Lessons from the Reinsertion of Ex-Combatants Project

Gertrude Ansaaku & Garret Nash

 

Abstract: Located in West Africa, Mali shares notable geopolitical similarities to Nigeria and the Boko Haram conflict. Both contexts involve economic and political marginalization of northern territories, organized responses which revolve around independent states and religious rhetoric, and violent conflict. Unlike Nigeria, Mali has successfully brokered a peace agreement between the government and rebel factions. Yet a year later, violence in Mali continues. There is continued tension between the government of Mali and the still marginalized north, although violence is not restricted to that area. Furthermore, the full weight Mali’s DDR plans are yet to be carried out, resulting in a population losing faith in the government’s ability to end violence. Many rebel groups are still in possession of weapons. In order to discuss how the Mali model could be applicable to Nigera’s Boko Haram conflict, this section first provides a brief overview of Mali and events leading up to the current conflict. Then, an overview of the 2015 Algiers Agreement DDR strategies as outlined in the 2015 peace agreement are then discussed. Finally, a comparison between Mali and Boko Haram is given. Lessons learned from Mali, which can be applied to the Boko Haram context in Nigeria, concludes this section. 

 

Model Name

The reintegration effort in Mali is called the Mali Reinsertion of Ex-Combatants Project, short for Mali Disarmament, Demobilization and Reintegration and Security Sector Reform. There is a cantonment phase, or encampment of ex-combatants, which has similarities with the Operation Safe Corridor in Nigeria for disarmament before the Reinsertion (or Reintegration). While some of the ex-combatants will return to civilian life, others will be integrated into the security forces.

 

Time Period

The armed conflict began in Mali in March 2012. Although a peace agreement was brokered in May/June 2015, no timeline for a successful DDR program was established. Currently, disarmament has yet to occur and conflict remains sporadic.[76] According to human rights watch, violence has spread from northern regions southward, including to Bamako, the capital.[77] Mali gained international attention when in February of 2016, almost ten months since the signing of the Algiers Agreement, jihadists took 170 hostages, (20 of which were killed) in the capital city.[78]

Background

Mali, a landlocked West African country, is a product of European colonialism. With present-day Senegal, it fell under French colonization in the late 1800s. Its current form, boundaries, institutions, systems and structures reflect a colonial heritage distinct from its pre-colonial nature, which was characterized by trade and nomadic movements. It gained independence in 1960 -- the same year as Nigeria. Its population is estimated at 17.5 million. About 90 percent of Malians are Muslim, with traditionalists and Christians in the minority.[79]

The Republic of Mali faced frequent rebellions, coups and twenty-three years of military dictatorship until democratic elections in 1992. Although Mali is self-sufficient in food and one of Africa’s major cotton producers, its chronic foreign trade deficit makes it heavily dependent on foreign aid and remittances from Malians abroad.[80] About 30 percent of its population is unemployed. Although agriculture is a major backbone of its economy, Mali faces recurring droughts, dust-laden harmattan haze (especially during the dry season), occasional Niger River flooding, deforestation, soil erosion, desertification, and inadequate supply of potable water.[81],[82] There are issues of physical insecurity, high population growth, corruption in government and public service, weak infrastructure and low levels of human capital, which hinder sustained economic growth.[83]

The following sections look at the nature, scope and causes of Mali’s northern insurgency and how the DDR process in Mali is progressing after the peace agreement was reached. The aim is to identify similarities and differences with Boko Haram in Nigeria in order to determine the applicability of the Mali DDR model to Nigeria. The last section offers policy recommendations for the reintegration of Boko Haram into the Nigerian society based on the challenges and lessons learnt from Mali.

 

The Northern Insurgency

Mali’s northern insurgency dates back to the Tuareg rebellions of 1963, 1990-91 and 2006. In these cases, demands were made on Mali for northern secession amidst the declaration of an independent Azawad. Peace settlements reached were not adhered to, which led to distrust, suspicion, anger and frustration on the part of the northerners. In particular, provisions on the grant of some autonomy to the north were violated several times until January 2012, when another northern rebellion erupted.[84]

The 2012 insurgency was sparked by widespread dissatisfaction and frustration over the deprivation and marginalization of the North, and unfulfilled promises made by the Government of Mali to resolve earlier rebellions.[85] Unlike the past, this insurgency has taken a more radicalized form.[86] A Tuareg rebel group called the Mouvement Nationale pour la Liberation de l’Azawad (MNLA) started attacks in the northern towns of Menaka, Tessalit, and Aguel’hoc in January 2012.[87] The rebellion also spread to Gao, Kidal, and Timbuktu, all in the north. Most of the rebels were returnees from Libya, after serving in Muammar Gaddafi’s army before the civil war that led to the collapse of the Gaddafi regime. A military coup which ensued in March 2012 after a military encounter with the rebels gave room for a take-over by a loose coalition of jihadist groups linked with the Al-Qaeda and the Tuareg rebels.[88] The major among them were the Ansar Dine and the Mouvement pour l'Unicité et le Jihad en Afrique de l’Ouest (MUJAO).[89] The insurgency differed from earlier ones in the way it was radicalized with religious (Islamic) undercurrents and involved well-armed cohorts of rebels. The rebels considered their insurgency a jihadist mission.[90]

 

International Involvement and Peace Settlement

In early January 2013, the jihadist group Ansar Dine and MUJAO captured Konna, a town strategically close to to the capital city, Bamako.[91] The transitional government of Mali asked for aide, which spurred a French-led intervention.[92] French and Chadian troops drove them away from major towns in the North and restored the territorial integrity of Mali. Subsequently, a ceasefire agreement was reached in Ouagadougou in June of 2013 with the rebel groups paving the way for the democratic elections which brought the new president, Ibrahim Boubacar Keita, and a new legislature to office.[93]

Later, an African Union (AU) mission established there was repurposed as the United Nations Multinational Integrated Stabilization Mission in Mali (MINUSMA). There was also a European Union (EU) training mission and security assistance from the United States (US) and France in the form of a counter-terrorism campaign (Barkane) which targeted cross-border jihadist movements in Mali and four other Sahel countries.[94]

Mali’s peace talks experienced several deadlocks due to disagreements between the government and key rebel groups over the creation of a Malian federation and ambiguities and rivalry among the various diplomatic initiatives led by MINUSMA, ECOWAS, AU, Algeria, Burkina Faso, and Morocco.[95] A peace agreement was finally reached in Algiers in June 2015, two years after the ceasefire agreement.[96]

The Algiers Agreement was signed between two coalitions of several Tuareg rebel groups; the Coordination of Azawad Movements (CMA) and the Platform of armed groups (the Platform). However, foreign-origin jihadist groups (al-Qaida in the Islamic Maghreb [AQIM] and the Movement for Unity and Jihad in West Africa [MUJAO]),  who controlled of most of Azawad from June 2012 to January 2013, did not sign the Agreement.[97] This was because they “[insisted] that they need time to explain the agreement to and to convince their supporters in northern Mali”.[98] Also not represented was the Imghad Tuareg and Allies Self-Defence Group (GATIA), as they emerged after peace talks had begun.[99] The Agreement provided for direct elections of local representatives to enhance local participatory governance, disarmament, demobilization and reinsertion (DDR), security sector reform (SSR), and establishment of conditions for restoring stability and economic recovery in Northern Mali.[100] Areas which have seen new administrations – prefects and sub-prefects – are in the regions of Gao, Kidal, Menaka, Taoudenni, and Timbuktu.[101]

         Further hindering the Algiers Agreement is continued violence in Mali.[102] For example, in November of 2015, jihadists took 170 from the Radisson Blu Hotel in Bamako.[103] 20 hostages were killed during the conflict. Later, in February of 2016, a UN based in Kidal was attacked, which resulted in the deaths of five UN Peacekeepers and wounded many others.[104]

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

Although yet to be put into practice, the Algiers Agreement outlines several roles for civilian agencies (albeit no specific organizations are mentioned). These functions include those related to human rights advocacy. Attention is accorded to justice officials who are well trained in violence against women and girls, the aim being to respect of the rights of women, children and other victims of conflict.[105] Leaders of Malian society, especially women and youth, are to help influence dialogue between communities and help reconciliation. Civil society and law professionals are to be enlisted to help reduce corruption and impunity in the justice system.[106]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

It is unclear exactly how many combatants will be integrated into the security forces and at what time. However, a World Bank grant will finance ten thousand cases of ex-combatants.[107], [108] Additional funds are allocated to reintegrate 7,500 of these ex-combatants.[109] There seems to be substantial pressure both from within Mali and the international community for forced disarmament.

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

A Truth, Justice and Reconciliation Commission (CVJR) was established in January 2014 to replace the former Dialogue and Reconciliation Commission (CDR).[110] The CVJR is responsible for investigating crimes committed between 1960 and 2013 (since independence) and make recommendations to the government for prosecution. Its mission remains to contribute to peace consolidation and national unity.[111] Transitional justice processes have intermittently been marred by violence from sections of the rebel groups.[112] Perpetrators of the most heinous crimes are to be taken to the International Criminal Court (ICC).[113] There would be no amnesty for those suspected to have committed some named crimes, as per the Algiers Agreement. However, to be able to get some rebel groups to sign the peace agreement, arrest warrants against fifteen suspected criminals who were to face charges of sedition and terrorism were lifted. Some others were also released from detention in Bamako.[114] One top rebel named Ahmad Al Faqi Al Mahdi was held in trial from August 22 to 24, 2016. He was charged with the war crime of destroying religious and historical artefacts in Timbuktu, Mali in June/July 2012. He pleaded guilty, and asked that his people in Timbuktu and Northern Nigeria forgive him for the harm caused. On September 27, 2016, the ICC sentenced him to a nine-year imprisonment.[115]

 

NGO and Community Involvement

The Algiers Agreement invites the involvement of humanitarian agencies and organizations to aid in returning, repatriating, and reintegrating all persons displaced by the conflict.[116] No specific organizations are mentioned nor is a timeline for their involvement given.  The Agreement contains provisions for a semi-autonomous Northern Mali and direct elections[117] However, no guidelines for community involvement in reintegration of ex-combatants are  given.

Financing and Cost Estimate

The DDR is funded by the Government of Mali and international donors, including the World Bank. In April 2016, the World Bank voted a $15 million grant for the DDR project in Mali. Technical, logistical, training and supervisory support have also come from the United Nations, European Union, ECOWAS, African Union, France and Chad.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

The Mali case has some similarities and differences with Nigeria’s Boko Haram case. Both Mali and Nigeria are in the West African sub-region. Both are products of colonialism and share the challenge of having to keep their constituent parts together within their states. The two share most of the conditions under which conflict and violent extremism thrive in their respective northern areas: poverty, lack of education, unemployment, ungoverned spaces, exclusionary politics, marginalization and neglect by government. The two also share the problem of unfavorable climatic conditions which make nomadic and farming activities difficult, if not impossible. Both cases have jihadist (Islamic) undercurrents, although at different levels. Again, Nigeria’s Boko Haram is said to be working in collaboration with the Ansar Dine and MUJAO in Mali.[118] Mali’s DDR is ongoing and could be a contemporary of Nigeria if the latter is ready to go the way of the former, and to speed up processes for a peace settlement. In that case, the challenges faced by the former could serve as lessons for Nigeria.

It appears that the extent to which religion plays out significantly in the Nigeria case is minimized in Mali. While Nigeria’s population seems to be evenly divided between Muslims and Christians, Mali is over 90% Muslim. Although in the latter, there are jihadist fighters alongside Tuareg rebel groups, Boko Haram and its splinter groups in northern Nigeria are mainly jihadists. Also, Mali’s DDR seems to be focused on the Tuareg rebels who went into the negotiations and signed the peace agreement. It appears the jihadists groups are yet to be captured in the entire process. However, if Nigeria is to undergo peace negotiations and a DDR process, it would have to do so with jihadists.

Again, in Mali, both the government and rebel groups agreed to the peace settlement, however reluctantly. It is not clear in the Nigerian case whether the government is ready for a political settlement. Also, although the military is not neutral in the conflict, it seems to be playing a role in initial efforts to disarm and demobilize ex-combatants who have voluntarily surrendered to the Operation Safe Corridor. If Nigeria is to go the Mali way, it would first have to be ready to negotiate with the Boko Haram, make compromises, and agree to have the pressing issues driving the insurgency addressed.

 

Summary, Conclusions and Recommendations for Nigeria

Mali, like most African states, suffers from most of the postcolonial challenges of nation-building. Like Nigeria, it has been through some years of civil wars and insurgencies, particularly in the northern regions. The inability of successive governments to adhere to the provisions of peace agreements, coupled with marginalization and neglect of the north have been a major cause of instability in the north. Northern Mali battles with unfavorable socio-economic conditions, political exclusion, and poor climatic conditions. The result has been built-up dissatisfaction, distrust, and suspicion leading to a series of rebellions. Mali’s northern insurgency has ethnic, and like northern Nigeria’s, religious undercurrents. For the fourth time, Mali is on the road to peace, after the signing of a peace agreement between the government and rebel groups. Jihadist fighters were not included in the negotiations, although they were also visible actors in the northern insurgency. If the Algiers Agreement is strictly adhered to and the DDR process goes well, with its many challenges addressed, Mali could be on a steady road to lasting peace.

With regard to its applicability to Nigeria, Mali offers a warning as to the consequences of delay. If a peace agreement is brokered between Nigeria and Boko Haram, the Mali model shows that the Nigerian government would have to be conscientious of timelines. We consider delays in Mali’s DDR efforts as a primary factor in continued unrest and waning trust of the government to resolve conflict. We see a strong possibility of similar trends in Nigeria if DDR is not followed through swiftly following a peace agreement. Of course, we cannot say if the Mali model would be successful in Nigeria, as its own success or failure is yet to be seen. Rather, we hope that Nigeria will consider the example of Mali and avoid similar errors.


 

Algeria: Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation

Daniel Edwan

 

Abstract: Analysis of the Algerian civil war (1991-2006), known in Algeria as the “national tragedy,” for the purpose of gaining insights into reintegration models is limited at the national policy level. On September 2005, the Algerian people overwhelmingly approved President Bouteflika’s proposed Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation, which granted amnesty for all individuals, whether Islamists, civilian militia, or national security forces, from prosecution for crimes committed during the war with a few exceptions. Despite its critics, the Charter provided a legal framework that began a national reintegration process that depended on current institutions to implement. Following the Charter, the Algerian government applied revised and new de-radicalization policies and strategies in order to suppress further Islamist radicals.[119] The net effects of these approaches over time successfully ended the civil war and significantly reduced violence and threats of terrorism. The two primary elements of the Algerian reintegration model are: first, comprehensive national amnesty followed by the regional distribution of the reintegration process to existing institutions; second, the implementation of de-radicalization policies across the country’s religious infrastructure.

 

Insurgency Description

            From 1992-2006, Algeria was locked in a protracted brutal civil war in which armed Islamic radical groups, pro-regime militias, and government security forces fought for power. The Islamist insurgency consisted of four distinct groups: the Armed Islamic Movement (MIA); the Islamic Salvation Army, AIS, (the self_declared armed wing of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS) political apparatus); the Armed Islamic Group (GIA); and, later, the Salafi Group for Preaching and Combat (GSPC). Although all groups launched violent campaigns against government forces, they appeared at different times and varied in their ideology and motives.[120] The MIA and AIS were moderate and targeted government forces for limited objectives to restore the FIS and validate the elections. The GIA and GSPC (jihadist Salafist organizations), with true intentions of establishing a caliphate, attacked government forces and civilians not loyal to the Islamists.[121] The period between 1995 and 1998 was characterized by a series of massacres directed against civilians in rural and isolated communities. The massacres were intended to terrorize the population that was hostile to the Islamists, or those who formerly supported the Islamist cause but who had withdrawn their support.

            The members of Islamist radical groups were generally classified as “those ill-treated by the security forces who were looking for revenge; and the ideologically driven devout activists or self_proclaimed ‘defenders of Islam.’”[122] Many young men were the product of poverty, boredom and repression, and engaging in the ‘jihad’ was a way of gaining wealth and prestige in their community. The war was a period of tremendous brutality, but for many it also offered chances to progress in society and improve their livelihoods.

 

Causal Elements

            Algeria’s brutal civil war (1991-2006), in which an estimated 200,000 were killed, was the result of three things: an inadequate post-French colonial power structures, a secular authoritarian regime, and the rise of Islamic radicalism. Characterized by a history of political violence, Algerian conflicts “evolved from resistance against the French occupation during the independence struggle (1954-1962), to small_scale violence against state personnel during the 1980s, to a full scale war against the Algerian state and civilians during the 1990s.”[123] Since gaining independence from France in 1962, the National Liberation Front (FLN), formed around a core of military elites, has remained in power as an authoritarian regime, in which pluralism and opposition were not tolerated. The Algerian national identity is both Arab and Muslim, in which latent ideological tensions between Arab secularism and Islamic Fundamentalism would slowly surface. Islam was reduced to ‘official Islam.’ Meanwhile, Islam in the public sphere was virtually abolished, encouraging the development of extremist religious narratives.[124]

            Violent resistance emerged for the first time in the 1980s, the decade during which “militant Islam in Algeria was promoted by a new post_independence generation, who rebelled against the FLN’s monopoly of power.”[125] To this generation of Muslims under the age of twenty-five, “political Islam was an attempt to restore the values that they advocated: social justice; the redistribution of political power; and the ‘threatened’ Islamic identity.” [126]  Contributing to these factors was social inequality and poor economic growth in which 72 percent under the age of twenty-five were unemployed.[127] The deteriorating socio-economic circumstances culminated in widespread protests and a series of violent attacks on government offices that were violently suppressed by security forces. In 1988, as an effort control further Islamic radicalization and stabilize the country, President Benjedid supported political reforms to the Constitution “based on three principles: a) the separation of power between the state and the FLN, which would function as an independent political party; b) free participation in municipal and legislative elections; and c) more room for associations and social movements to express their ideas.”[128] The FLN’s attempt to appease the Islamic movement underestimated the size, scale and mobilization of its political power that would backfire with the rise of the Islamic Salvation Front (FIS).

            The FIS founded shortly after the constitutional amendment in 1989 called for a return to the values of equal opportunity, justice and accountability. Mobilizing its political message of “Neither National Charter nor Constitution: Islam is the Solution,” through an urban network of mosques and social services, the FIS won “188 of the 231 seats in the first round of the general elections held in December 1991, compared with only eighteen seats won by the governing party, the FLN.”[129] In January 1992, the FLN military’s Higher State Council seized power, declared a state of emergency, compelled President Benjedid to resign and cancelled the elections. The FIS was dissolved and the military detained over 18,000 former FIS members and suspected Islamic sympathizers without trial in concentration camps.[130] The FLN justified it actions to supporters by stating the FIS would not uphold democratic principles because “Islam and democracy are incompatible.”[131] The FLN’s hostile actions and egregious hypocrisy of justifying their actions to uphold democracy while suppressing the Constitutional mandate for free and public elections was the tipping point of the Algerian civil war.

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

The Algerian military armed civilians and encouraged the organization of self-defense militias in order to expand government control.[132] The Algerian government mobilized all civilian agencies in the execution of the civil war.

 

International Agency Involvement

The IMF, US, France and UN Funds provided outside material support to the government during the conflict.

 

NGO Involvement

            NGOs were marginalized during the conflict and their support was not government sponsored.

Criteria for Reintegration

            Reintegration after the civil war was based on a foundational principle of national amnesty and included a provision of exemption from prosecution for crimes committed during the war -- including Islamists, militia, or security forces.

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

            In 2005, the Charter for Peace and National Reconciliation was proposed as a public referendum. The prevailing idea was for the country to look forward without declaring winners or losers. Of note, the regime would remain blameless for the conflict.[133] “According to government figures the charter was supported by 97 percent of the voters in a popular referendum with a turnout of 82 percent.”[134]

Key measures of the Charter included:

_      Measures ending state prosecution against individuals implicated in instances of terrorism;

_      Reduced or commuted sentences for individuals condemned and not eligible to release;

_      Care for the families of individuals in the battle against terrorism;

_      Exclusion of individual who committed rapes, set off explosives in public places, or carried out assassinations from the benefit of those measures.[135]

            Besides the exemption of individuals, whether Islamists, militia, or security forces, from prosecution for crimes committed during the war, it also provided harsh punitive measures against those who attempt to discredit the regime or security force involvement.[136] Specifically anyone that attempts to “undermine the good reputation of its agents,” or “tarnish the reputation of Algeria internationally, shall be punished.”[137] These measures remain in effect to the present day. Unsurprisingly, these legal parameters significantly affect government transparency and limit international agency efforts to investigate human rights abuses and provide support. Specifically, the Algerian government announced that the names of Islamist terrorists or the reports of rape or massacres would not be made public.[138]

 

Community Engagement

            Another significant aspect of the Charter was the issue of the “disappeared,” or the victims of terrorism. This amplified growing skepticism about the regime’s desire and capacity to facilitate a transparent reconciliation and reintegration. Families of the disappeared formed associations “demanding information on the fate of their loved ones and insisting that truth must precede reconciliation.”[139] An Algerian National commission was formed, uncommon due to the government's use of existing ministerial structure, to adjudicate the claims of the disappeared. After four years of deliberation, the vast majority of the 13,541 applications were denied due the regime’s claims of a national state of self-defense and the admission of “no records, no witnesses, the court cases would never lead to convictions,” and it would be “impossible to settle all the unresolved cases in court.”[140] These comments present the stark reality that the Algerian judicial system was incapable of conducting a fair trial for the thousands involved. Algerian amnesty, therefore, appears to be the result of a limited bureaucratic capacity as opposed to an expansive desire for forgiveness.

 

Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

The Charter provided the legal framework for the government to pursue an underlying strategy to “isolate the mainstream Islamists from the violent ideologists, and to reintegrate them in society.”[141] Additionally it provided a means for an acceptable amnesty for the regime and its security forces. The FLN elite were faced with the dilemma of vindicating their adverse actions of 1992 against the FIS. In addition, the FLN elite were creating a publically accepted revised and more pervasive set of laws and policies to suppress dissent over their conduct of the civil war. The regime was not only successful at achieving this goal, but it found a means in the counter-terrorism doctrine to remain a viable international partner, despite significant human rights skepticism from many aid organizations.[142]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

            Available research materials indicate that the Algerian government did not constitute additional reintegration infrastructure such as special designed camps to provide ex-combatants employment, education, and income support. The Charter only provided the means for ex-combatants to regain their civil liberties. Otherwise in pursuit of subverting Islamist radicalism, national measures in the form of de-radicalization strategies were implemented. 

Government de-radicalization strategies were designed to support a management of religious affairs through the propagation of a moderate, less politicized form of Islamic teachings. The Algerian government employed five noteworthy techniques: (1) The government widely publicized a former radical leader’s testimony to convince to jihadists to reintegrate; (2) The government sponsored hundreds of external Muslim preachers to Algeria to convey a moderate non-violent version of Islam;[143] (3) The government heavily monitored sermons, religious school curricula, and appointed imams as civil servants through the Ministry of Religious Affairs; (4) The government consulted FLN elite and scholars for strategies that promote a peaceful and tolerant Islamic teachings; and (5) The government sponsored a non-political variant of Islamic Salafism as a means to undermine radical Salafism.[144]

 

Cost Estimate

            The secrecy of Algerian government financial distributions and their outright claim that the conflict was a civil war make the study of Algerian community reintegration models unfeasible. Cost estimates reference Algeria’s reintegration program are negligible since the government used a national reconciliation process using existing governmental institutions.

 

Financing

Armed groups collected taxes in areas under their control. In other areas, armed group extorted businesses and established roadside checkpoints. There were significant links across borders to transnational and organized criminal organizations. The government was on the verge of bankruptcy in 1993 and utilized debt rescheduling and the World Bank’s Structural Adjustment Program (SAP) to reverse its financial situation. This made the necessary funds available to defeat armed groups.[145]

 

Challenges

The most significant challenge was on the Algerian regime itself. The idea of national reconciliation negotiated by many of the same military leaders that started the conflict and the attempts at resolving human rights atrocities perpetuated an adverse international image of the country. There are similar strategic challenges for the Nigerian government, in that, the Nigerian military with numerous documented human rights abuses is the government entity responsible for Operation Safe Corridor. This controversial arrangement could result in Nigerian adverse international image.   

The Charter provided the regime renewed authority to suppress Islamic radical political parties from assembly and participation. Specifically, Article 26 of the Charter “excludes from political life anyone who committed acts of terrorism or manipulated Islam for political purposes.”[146] The suppression of political rights is contrary to democratic processes and will likely serve as a future grievance.

The use of local militias in the Algerian civil war, declared as ‘Patriots’ by executive decree created a set of problems and human rights abuses. In some cases, militias were involved in assassinations, extortion and the use of excessive force.[147]

Although national reconciliation was declared, the lack of implementing guidance down to the local level (especially regarding employment) created significant issues among former militants who were denied jobs and passports. The idea that a blanket policy of reconciliation would permeate to the local level after over a decade of conflict proved illusory.[148]

 

Successes

            The Charter proved to be an effective end of the civil war, in which the Algerian government claims that an estimate of six thousand former combatants, not including security forces, utilized the amnesty program.[149] The Algerian Ministry of Foreign Affairs claimed the following: “Thanks to this policy, thousands of repented agreed to surrender of their own free will, thereby allowing the Nation to spare hundreds or even thousands of lives, as well as to enable the return and reintegration into society of a large number of repented.” [150] Despite significant international criticism, sustained violence in Algeria has diminished since 2006 indicating that the country was able to move forward. Significant residual grievances have not yet threatened stability.

 

Similarities to Boko Haram

There are numerous contextual similarities between the Nigerian Boko Haram and the Algerian civil war. The most fundamental similarity is that both conflicts contain an Islamist radicalization element in competition with a secular regime. Due to this, Algeria’s de-radicalization strategies could provide implementation value to Nigeria’s reintegration process. In particular, Nigeria could publicize former Boko Haram leader’s testimony. This de-radicalization strategy could result in increased community support and convince to jihadists to reintegrate. Additionally, Nigeria may benefit sponsoring external Muslim imams to preach a moderate non-violent version of Islam impacting a larger aspect of the radicalization environment.

In both conflicts, there are extensive alleged governmental security force human right violations. Additionally, both conflicts appear to be characterized by the lack of judicial infrastructure and/or prosecutable evidence. Due to these similarities, Nigeria may ultimately require a similarly comprehensive national reconciliation effort that is supported both internally and externally. Internal support enables resolution of grievances among all parties, while external support translates into government legitimacy and international funding streams. Additionally, both Nigerian and Algerian governments empowered armed civilian militias. Lessons from Algeria indicate that a government-sponsored civilian force left unaccountable may act in accordance with their own interests rather than the government's. This paper proposes a comprehensive set of transitional justice methods to achieve reconciliation for the Boko Haram conflict. Even though these methods differ from Algeria’s blanket amnesty approach, the idea of creating an internally and externally supported transitional justice system is founded on lessons learned from the Algerian civil war.

 

Differences from Boko Haram

             The Algerian regime, although secular, was still steeped in the singularly-dominant Muslim culture. Meanwhile, Nigeria is starkly divided along Muslim and Christian lines (roughly 50-50). The cultural tension between the southern Christians and ruling Nigerian government versus the Islamist areas in northern Nigeria may be a barrier to reintegration. On a positive note, relative to Algeria, the Nigerian government has established limited local reintegration agencies to support policy implementation at the local level. This is important to de-radicalize and integrate the former militant into society, and is preferable to a heavy reliance on current institutions in which reintegration expertise is not centralized or coordinated.

 

 

Somalia: The Xeer Model

Ellen Chapin & William Wright

 

Abstract: Resilient chaos in Somalia provides a rich subject for analysis, particularly regarding the reintegration of a violent insurgency into civil society. Al-Shabaab and the Somali government’s response share many common themes with Boko Haram and the Nigerian government. Both are brutally violent insurgencies located in profoundly underdeveloped regions, seeking to promote their brands of extremist Islamism as a source of stability in the face of ineffective government institutions. However, while the conflict in Nigeria has its roots in religious divisions, disenfranchisement, and heavy-handed government backlash, Somalia is an almost entirely Muslim nation that is fractured along clan lines. Somalia has been successful in reinvigorating and galvanizing some traditional, pre-Al-Shabaab mechanisms, specifically the Xeer justice framework, which may be a useful model for Boko Haram reintegration and transitional justice. While an incomplete solution for reintegration of Boko Haram, we strongly recommend the adaptation of a Xeer-like traditional justice system composed of community leaders in northern Nigeria to address the unique needs of a population who have felt under-served by the Abuja government and abused by the Nigerian military. Xeer has been far from a panacea in Somalia; as insurgent violence continues, metrics that specify the degree of its success are nearly impossible to ascertain. The incorporation of a Xeer-like system must be implemented in concert with broader efforts to address the consequences of Boko Haram as well as the conditions that precipitated the violence.

 

Time Period

The United Nations has called the current situation in Somalia the “world’s worst humanitarian disaster.”[151] Since 1991, clan warfare has plagued the country, and with the emergence of Al-Shabaab, stability, peace and prosperity remain elusive. At the end of January 2009, Sheikh Sharif Ahmed was elected President of Somalia with the hope that his administration would bring a degree of stability to Somalia, but thus far, violence has continued unabated. At the end of October 2016, the United Nations estimates that there are as many as 1,106,751 IDPs throughout Somalia.[152]

 

Insurgency Description

Since the ousting of President Mohamed Siad Barre in 1991, approximately 300,000 Somalis have died due to violence, famine and disease.[153] The formerly allied rebel groups, which banded together to destroy Barre’s administration, split off and declared a civil war.[154] This resulted in a humanitarian catastrophe, with hundreds of thousands of deaths and internally displaced peoples (IDPs). Inter-clan and intra-clan fighting and looting decimated the Somali landscape: the entire economic infrastructure has been destroyed and political institutions no longer exist.[155]

To this day, violence remains ongoing, due to the 2006 ascension of the notoriously violent Al-Qaeda-affiliate Al-Shabaab to the forefront of the battered Somali landscape. Al-Shabaab is an offshoot of the Islamic Courts Union (ICU), which splintered into several smaller factions after the ICU was defeated by Somalia's Transitional Federal Government (TFG) and the TFG's Ethiopian military allies.[156] Al-Shabaab describes itself as waging jihad against “enemies of Islam.” As a result, Al-Shabaab is currently engaged in combat against multiple parties on multiple fronts. Specifically, Al-Shabaab continues a battle with the Federal Government of Somalia and the African Union Mission to Somalia (AMISOM).[157] Al-Shabaab has been designated as a terrorist organization by Australia, Canada, the United Arab Emirates, the United Kingdom and the United States.

 

Causal Elements

The insurgency has roots in Somalia’s clan-based society and in the ousting of Siad Barre in 1991. The United Somali Congress (USC) announced Ali Mahdi as the interim president after the toppling of Barre in 1991, which had many unexpected results. Immediately, given ideological differences, the USC split along sub-clan lines.[158] The Habar Gedir-based faction, led by General Mohammed Farah Aideed and the Abgal-based group led by Ali Mahdi began a brutal intra-clan struggle for power.[159] At the same time, thirteen other clans and subclans also became engaged in the struggle.

Al-Shabaab capitalized on this clan system to rise to power, and their intention remains to rule Somalia under Sharia Law. Al-Shabaab's predecessor was “al-Ittihad al-Islami (AIAI), which worked to create an Islamist emirate in Somalia and was partially funded by former al-Qaeda leader Osama bin Laden.”[160] Gaining power after the Ethiopian forces invaded Somalia, both groups leveraged their various levels of religiosity and the faithfulness of different clan groups to build a coalition of followers. They still ascribe to this—their new leader, Ahmad Umar, is understood to represent Al-Shabaab’s more nationalist branch, which aims only to rule Somalia.[161]

Civilian Agency Involvement

            Largely as a result of the destruction of Somalia’s existing infrastructure, the collective efforts of federal, state, and local agencies have failed to slow the spread of Al-Shabaab’s influence.[162] No individual group has had success in quelling violence, so the military has been the primary instrument of Somali national power. Unfortunately, the Somali military has yet to demonstrate either the capability or willingness to address the social, economic, and cultural factors that led to Al-Shabaab’s rise.[163]

 

Military Involvement

In 2007, the Ethiopian military was joined by AMISOM, a U.N.-backed African Union mission that was tasked with protecting Somalia’s fledgling transitional government. The early deployment of fifteen hundred Ugandan troops quickly grew into the African Union’s largest-ever peace-support mission, encompassing contingents from Burundi, Sierra Leone, Djibouti, Kenya, and Ethiopia, among others, and embracing an aggressive peace-enforcement mandate centered on taking the fight directly to Al-Shabaab.[164]

The most recent insurgency began in early February 2009 when conflict arose between the forces of the Federal Government of Somalia, assisted by African Union peacekeeping troops, and various militant Islamist groups and factions.[165] The violence has displaced thousands of people in the southern part of the country. The conflict has also seen violence between various types of Islamic groups, including Al-Shabaab versus more moderate Sufi coalitions.

More recently, Al-Shabaab in January of 2016, “killed more than 100 Kenyan troops (from the African Union) and drove off with their trucks and weapons.” With high-profile piracy incidents and the 2013 Westgate Mall hostage crisis in Nairobi, Al-Shabaab has maintained international prominence.[166] As a result, the U.S. has employed and escalated President Obama’s frequent strategy of “light-footprint” warfare.[167] This strategy is characterized by extensive surgical strikes against terrorist leadership (by both drones and special operations forces) and training for indigenous troops.[168] With this strategy, there is often limited risk to U.S. forces, minimal media exposure, and little political discourse.[169] For the past several months, as Al-Shabaab has continued terrorist attacks in Somalia and neighboring Kenya, the more globally-oriented Islamic State (IS) has created an “insurgency within an insurgency” and battled with Al-Shabaab to establish the IS brand of Sharia law.[170]

 

International Agency & Expert Involvement

International agencies have been largely involved in the humanitarian crisis created by the insurgency. In the 1990s, feeling public pressure, U.S. President George H.W. Bush ordered emergency airlifts of food and supplies to Somalia.[171] The UN Security Council similarly approved a military mission, “Operation Restore Hope,” led by the United States to try to help the country by protecting food shipments from the warlords.[172] However, not long after, the United Nations declared that it would be pulling its international staff and aid workers from Somalia because of the dangerous conditions. Then, in 2005, food shipments began being hijacked off the coast of Somalia by rebel forces. Food aid programs, including the UN’s World Food Program (WFP), were suspended.[173]

Following these efforts, violence only continued. In 2008, the UN Security Council approved sending other countries' warships to Somalia’s territorial waters in order to combat the threat of Somali pirates, who had begun hijacking ships regularly.[174] That same year, the head of the UN Development Programme in Somalia was killed by gunmen in Mogadishu.[175] In response, American military began flying drone aircrafts from a base in Ethiopia.[176] Most recently, in July 2016, the UN Security Council adopted Resolution 2297, which extended AMISOM’s authorization until May 2017 with no major changes.[177]

NGO Involvement

In the wake of disintegration of the Somali state in 1992, numerous NGOs were formed. Research has shown that NGOs experience a number of common challenges regarding: internal decision-making processes, recruitment, retention of staff, layoffs, accountability, evaluation, structural growth, and fund-raising activities[178]

 

Impetus for Reintegration

While hostility is ongoing and active Al-Shabaab groups remain in power, sociologists studying insurgency and reintegration practices (such as those at the UN Development Fund for Women) have recognized an increase in efforts to encourage “defection” of children and young adults recruited into the insurgency.[179] As within similar terrorist groups, the members cycle out as new recruits become available. Consequently, many have worked to identify best practices for reincorporating these men,women, and children back into society.

The most commonly discussed model is the Xeer system. Despite the lack of a functioning government, local structures within Somali society have served to maintain a minimum level of stability, rule of law, and justice. The Xeer serves as a dispute settlement mechanism based on clan/family customary law and elements of Sharia law, the body of law derived from the Qur'an and from the teaching and examples of the Prophet Mohammed.[180] Given that the Al-Shabaab insurgency forced local leaders out of their traditionally powerful roles, Xeer seeks to empower those leaders again--much like Nigeria, where community leaders have been relatively disempowered. Tribal leaders can thus unify their community, and solidify the relationships between citizens and ex-combatants.

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

While reintegration is a critical component of post-Somali insurgency, the African Union emphasizes the importance of a balance between disarmament and prosecution. In order to make any model relevant, the best practices for AU integration emphasizes “the need for complementarity between the international tribunals, hybrid courts and domestic legal systems with regards to prosecutions and investigations.”[181] In many ways, Rwanda, South Africa, and Colombia faced a similar fundamental transitional justice issue as Somalia: there are simply too many defendants to try fairly and appropriately. Domestic courts simply lack the necessary financial and human resources to try all cases. Because of the ongoing insurgency as well, many judges and educated people have fled the region, continuing the cycle of unprosecuted cases. Moreover, “many legal codes were literally burnt by the Islamic Courts—as a result, there is a general lack of trust in the ability of the national courts to assure a fair and transparent trial.”[182]

The Xeer system is still the most commonly used and reinforced justice mechanism—so punishment is an incorporated component of Xeer. Somalis use Xeer to solve approximately 80-90 percent of all controversies involving crimes, especially in rural areas where lack of transportation may prevent people from using other justice mechanisms.[183] This ensures that all citizens have access to the justice system, and that ex-combatants are able to make amends in the eyes of all those in their returned home.

 

Community Engagement

One of the most important aspects of the Xeer system is the involvement of local community members. The clan elders, acting as judges, lead Xeer processes. There is also an effort to incorporate differing tribes in some cases, so that a lack of bias is ensured—in those instances, “the Xeer is an unwritten agreement created bilaterally between two clans.”[184] Clan members transmit these agreements from generation to generation—this type of tradition sets precedent, thus forms part of customary law.[185] As a result, community members know that they have and will be heard in existing law. Additionally, Xeer hearings are held in public.[186] This type of inclusion facilitates group participation, and allows those who are prosecuted be publicly punished, then publicly forgiven.

One of the more problematic aspects of Xeer, though, is that it marginalizes and excludes other members of the community. Some of these exclusions involve efforts to maintain objectivity—much like in Western systems, persons with close family relationships with those involved in the controversy, individuals who have a personal grievance against one of the parties, and persons who have already sat in judgment of the same case are not allowed to attend Xeer hearings or processes.[187] Given the religious roots of the system, women are also not permitted to participate in the discussion and judgment.[188] This merits further discussions in applicability beyond Somalia.

 

Reconciliation

The Xeer system is associated with the idea of restorative justice. One of its main goals is to reestablish the social order—therefore, any punishment or penance is designed with reintegration in mind. The ultimate end goal, for ex-combatants and beyond, is for the community to be satisfied with justice and to seek to move forward after the proceedings. Often, victims are compensated through a Diya (blood compensation), which consists of either money or livestock—any kind of payment from them, or from their families.[189] Those who are involved in the practice speak highly of the way in which Xeer promotes reconciliation. According to one ex-combatant, “in the clan justice, the decision is made to stop the violence: the aim is to make peace and set up an agreement.”[190] Considering the importance, acceptance and legitimacy that the clan system and customary practices have had throughout Somali history, the traditional Xeer system could be considered a means to establish accountability, while facilitating forgiveness and acceptance of the accused.

 

Cost Estimate and Financing

In terms of application in other contexts, Xeer is particularly compelling for its cost-effectiveness and ease of start-up. Beginning any new program, especially one being funded by international NGOs, or through supranational organizations, can be complicated financially and logistically. However, Somalia can simply continue and revive the existing cultural norms as soldiers lay down their arms. The system is already in place, so no major new structures are needed, such as the construction of courts and the election of judges. Additionally, an unintended consequence of the application of Xeer to ex-combatants is the possibility of banding different tribal groups together. By creating further commonalities and cooperation among groups, the number of recruits for Al-Shabaab may decrease, given the lack of clan fighting and proximity to returned soldiers. Combined with the lack of financial cost, Xeer’s integration into the social fabric of Somalia makes the use of such a system ideal.

 

Lessons Learned / Applicability to Boko Haram

Somalia’s Xeer model may hold potential for Nigeria. While Islam is not nearly as prevalent in Nigeria, and the Islamic system of law is not a part of the federal, or even local system, procedures like Xeer are a crucial component of small villages, and the politics therein.[191] Nigerian traditional rulers often derive their titles from the rulers of independent states or communities that existed before the formation of modern Nigeria. Although they do not have formal political power, in many cases they continue to command respect from their people and have considerable influence.[192] Given the influence of these rulers, one could consider empowering them to act as adjudicators in reintegration. Empowering these local leaders could potentially solve a problem that Operation Safe Corridor has encountered: a lack of community buy-in and support. Community focus may also curb the potential for recidivism, and allow communities to accept former combatants more readily than they currently do. Additionally, provided that the Xeer system has no root in religion in Nigeria, a Xeer-like model would circumvent some of the problematic aspects of Xeer—most importantly, the lack of women’s participation. Including the entire community in such a proceeding will further allow reintegration by all—mothers, sisters, wives, and beyond for those who are seeking reconciliation.

While there is a punitive aspect to Xeer, its most important aspect is one of forgiveness. This could be especially beneficial in reintegrating non-traditional ex-combatants—specifically women who have been kidnapped by Boko Haram, and child soldiers. With many Chibok girls returning to their villages, and others choosing to stay with their husbands, both would benefit from a transitional justice system that inherently reaffirms the culture of their villages, and does not punish them for being kidnapped. The same principle applies to child soldiers—as of 2016, the United Nations verified the recruitment and use of 278 children (143 boys and 135 girls) by Boko Haram (225) and the Civilian Joint Task Force (53).[193] The same report cites that “twenty-one girls were used in suicide attacks claimed by Boko Haram, 11 of which were documented in the fourth quarter.”[194] Additionally, “of the 1,010 children (422 boys and 588 girls) encountered or rescued during the course of military operations in north-east Nigeria, 204 (117 girls and 87 boys) had been recruited and used by Boko Haram.”[195] All of these children would benefit from the Xeer process—some are still children, and returning to their families will be difficult. But with the community’s forgiveness and support, reintegration is possible. The best way to do so, as opposed to simply mandating their return, is to involve those who are receiving them—their families, and their communities.

Research and analysis of Operation Safe Corridor indicate that local leaders expressed reluctance in accepting ex-combatants back into communities. Especially in the Somali and Nigerian contexts where traditional local leadership has been largely degraded and dismantled, a model (like Xeer), which incorporates and empowers those leaders, may be ideal to implement in Nigeria. Unfortunately, the enduring violence and chaos in Somalia demonstrates the limits of Xeer. While the incorporation of a Xeer-like system may be useful in a Nigerian context, it likely will not cure all the interrelated insurgency issues. A Xeer-like system should be introduced in concert with a broader Nigerian whole-of-government approach to address the consequences of Boko Haram as well as the conditions that precipitated the violence. Of note, however, central government involvement and oversight of Xeer may restrict the flexibility of such a profoundly localized concept. Policymakers and proponents of reintegration will have to weigh these factors when choosing to implement lessons from Somalia’s Xeer system.

 

 

 

South Africa: Truth and Reconciliation Commission
Aprille Knox

 

Abstract: From 1948 until 1990, South Africa’s apartheid system “legally enforced racial segregation” through policies that denied civil and political liberties to black South Africans.[196] Initially peaceful opposition movements were met with violent repression by the state, leading to an increasingly unstable and brutal political environment. After decades of unrest, the apartheid system finally began to crack under the weight of international sanctions and economic decline.[197] During the period from 1990-1994, numerous negotiations were held between the National Party -- the perpetrators of apartheid -- and the African National Congress -- the primary liberation movement -- in order to establish a unity government, paving the way for a peaceful power transition.[198] Armed forces were consolidated under the newly established South African National Defense Forces and a conditional amnesty program was put in place through the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), established as a fact-finding and truth-seeking body responsible for holding accountable all perpetrators of human rights abuses throughout the apartheid era. While the South African example is markedly different from that of Nigeria, as one of the most frequently cited examples of transitional justice, the TRC offers many lessons to be learned and applied towards the reintegration of former Boko Haram combatants. This paper provides an overview of the context, programs, and outcomes of the South African model, including recommendations of how and why some of its components - namely truth telling and conditional amnesty - may be applicable to the Nigerian context.

Time Period: December 1995 – 2002[199]

 

The apartheid system began in earnest in 1948, when the National Party (NP) came to power, establishing a whites-only political system.[200] As time went on, the NP increasingly enacted ethnically divisive policies – including the Group Areas Act (establishing physical separation of racial groups); the Bantu Authorities Act (forcibly displacing black South Africans to their “native homelands”); and the Natives Act (requiring all black South Africans to carry identification at all times), to name just a few.[201]

“Intensification of repressive, discriminatory laws and practices under segregation and apartheid prompted growing black protest and resistance.”[202] In response to the NP’s racist policies, the African National Congress (ANC) (see insurgency description) initially promoted non-violent demonstrations to gain greater political representation and rights for the black South African majority.

Insurgency Description[203]

African National Congress (ANC): The ANC was established in 1912, initially as an organization of black, mission-educated elite. Although its composition and tactics have shifted over time, its overall objectives have remained constant: 1) to overthrow white supremacy and 2) to establish a truly democratic government in South Africa.[204] The ANC initially embraced peaceful forms of protest -- strikes, sit-ins, and other forms of non-violent demonstration. However, as time went on, the organization adapted in response to the NP’s repression, starting by targeting state infrastructure and eventually developing a militant wing -- Umkhonto We Sizwe (MK).[205]

 

Pan Africanist Congress (PAC): The PAC was established in 1959 as a splinter group of the ANC. While the group espoused many similar values and desires, it did not support the pan-racial movement. The PAC supported black nationalism - building a country for and by black South Africans alone, thereby most notably excluding the large Indian population that had, largely, been supportive of the ANC’s cause.[206] The PAC may be most known for its involvement in the Sharpeville Massacre of 1960, “when policemen killed at least sixty-nine defenseless people -- many of whom were shot in the back - during a peaceful PAC demonstration against pass laws.”[207] It was after this event that the apartheid government banned both the PAC and ANC. After being banned from South Africa, the PAC also established a militant arm - the Azanian People’s Liberation Army (APLA).[208]

 

National Party (NP): The NP, a whites-only government party that ruled South Africa from 1948-1994, were the perpetrators of apartheid. Under their rule, black South Africans were stripped of their rights as formal segregationist policies were imposed on the population.[209] The NP employed the South African Defense Forces (SADF) to violently contest any signs of political opposition, through whatever means necessary.[210]

            However, peaceful protests were met with violent opposition from the apartheid government. “Bannings, detentions, and harassment of liberation movement leaders and activists became commonplace,” leading the ANC to eventually move underground in the 1960s.[211] It was during this period that armed resistance began to grow in the form of the ANC’s militant wing – Umkhonto We Sizwe (otherwise known as “Spear of the Nation” or MK).[212]

The 1970s and 1980s saw growing agitation and violence across all sectors of society. The NP increasingly used hit squads, often killing innocent civilians, and pursued a divide-and-rule strategy,[213] sparking divisions amongst black opposition parties, which the apartheid government used to frame conflict as “black-on-black” violence.[214] Fortunately, the international community was able to see through the NP’s façade, and increasingly pressured the apartheid regime to relinquish power – largely through the imposition of economic sanctions and verbal condemnations.[215] By the late 1980s – as the Cold War was coming to an end – the apartheid system was finally beginning to crack under international pressure. Negotiations between newly elected NP president, F. W. de Klerk, and ANC leader, Nelson Mandela, led to Mandela’s release from prison in 1990, as well as the development of the Convention for a Democratic South Africa (CODESA), which was tasked with negotiating a new constitution and peaceful government transition.[216]

Yet, violence continued. Despite the signing of the National Peace Accord in 1991, the period between Mandela’s release from Robben Island in 1990 and South Africa’s first truly democratic elections in 1994, was the bloodiest period in apartheid’s history.[217][218] It was only after democratic elections were held that the apartheid era, and the violence and racism associated with it, started to come to an end.

 

Impetus for Reintegration / Reconciliation

In the years between Mandela’s release and South Africa’s first democratic elections, numerous negotiations were held between all factions - though primarily the NP and ANC - in order to successfully put the conflict to rest and ensure a smooth political transition. Chief among these were negotiations between the SADF and MK regarding disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration procedures for former armed combatants.[219] [220] The major result of these negotiations was the establishment of the South African National Defense Forces (SANDF), which formally incorporated all previously existing armed forces.[221] It was agreed that in total, 17,000 MK troops, 6,000 APLA members, 10,000 homeland militia fighters, and 85,000 SADF and staff would comprise the new SANDF, which began formal integration in 1994.[222]

Meanwhile, the newly elected South African government faced the difficult task of coping with the collective human rights abuses of the past, while paving the way forward for a more peaceful and productive future. Despite the election of a “national unity” government in 1994, South African society needed more to “make unity a reality” - “the transition (from) a past marred by human rights abuses to one based on the principles of democracy and respect for human rights could not be had simply by a transition in government.”[223]

The peaceful democratic transition was not the result of military victory. Instead, “the ANC (recognized that it) could not impose its peace; this would only ever come about if they managed to persuade the National Party to give up the reins of power voluntarily.”[224] During negotiations, the NP advocated blanket amnesty for all party members, which the ANC wholeheartedly rejected.[225] Instead, the ANC advocated “conditional amnesty” - which was predicated on truth-telling for politically justified acts.[226]

What emerged, as a result of the National Unity and Reconciliation Act, was the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) - a three-chambered body tasked with tackling justice, truth-telling, and reparations. The TRC was divided into three committees:

1.     The Human Rights Violations (HRV) Committee - “responsible for conferring victim status on those individuals who qualified under the Act and came forward to the Commission to make a statement.”[227]

2.     The Amnesty Committee - “responsible for fulfilling the imperative contained in the interim Constitution that ‘amnesty shall be granted in respect of acts, omissions and offenses associated with political objectives and committed in the course of the conflicts of the past.’”[228]

3.     The Reparation and Rehabilitation (R&R) Committee - “responsible for making recommendations to the government regarding the provision of reparations and rehabilitation of victims.”[229]

The TRC was seen as “the price the liberation forces had to pay in order to secure a peaceful transition to a majority rule.”[230] But in addition to appeasing the NP’s demands, it was also used as a tool for “creating a collective memory for the society, establishing and nurturing a culture of human rights …, transforming the society, and enhancing reconciliation.”[231]

Civilian Agency, Military, International Agency, and NGO Involvement

Civilian Agency: Initial reintegration and reconciliation discussions were primarily led by leaders of the competing factions - largely the ANC and NP. The TRC was intentionally formed as a stand-alone body outside of the government court system. It was established by the passing of the National Unity and Reconciliation Act by the South African Constitutional Court.[232] Although it “operated outside of South Africa’s domestic legal system, … (its) amnesty decisions were binding on domestic courts.”[233] The TRC itself was chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu and led by 17 commissioners - nine men and eight women appointed by President Mandela, of which no more than two could be non-South African citizens.[234] The commissioners were supported by a staff of approximately 300.[235]

Military: The SADF and MK were integral in defining South Africa’s disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration (DDR) strategies for former combatants. All armed factions were required to submit a roster of enlisted personnel to the Department of Defence, which used this “Certified Personnel Register” to monitor the DDR process and integration of SANDF forces.[236] This proved challenging in that not all units had sufficient personnel record systems in place and much debate was had regarding who should be considered a combatant. No military units were directly involved in the implementation of South Africa’s TRC. However, former combatants from all forces were eligible for participation.

International Agency: International agencies and governments were highly involved in helping put an end to the brutal apartheid regime through the imposition of sanctions, sports bans, and other forms of global condemnation.[237] However, reintegration and reconciliation strategies were largely homegrown.

Non-governmental Organization: The South African government has been widely criticized for its limited inclusion of non-governmental and civil society organizations throughout the 1990s, in both the design of the TRC and ex-combatant reintegration programming.[238]

 

Criteria for Reintegration and Reconciliation

The Joint Military Coordinating Council (JMCC) - comprised of representatives from the SADF, MK, APLA, and homeland militias - facilitated the integration of new SANDF forces.[239] Former-combatants age sixteen and older, including women, were allowed to participate, many of whom first needed to be repatriated from abroad.[240] All ex-combatants — according to the Certified Personnel Register (see military involvement above) — were brought before the SANDF Placement Board for psychological and physical examination. “Those with psychological disabilities, suffering from ill health, without sufficient military training or without the minimum military or educational qualifications were demobilized.”[241]

In addition, in 1995 the SANDF began a policy of voluntary release, allowing any newly enscripted SANDF soldiers to voluntarily leave the service, without retribution.[242] Demobilized ex-combatants were entitled to the following:

1.     A small “gratuity” - usually between $3,500 and $11,000 USD, based on time served.[243]

2.     Voluntary counselling - “on personal matters, careers, social services and finances that stretched over a period of two weeks.”[244]

3.     An optional 18-month vocational training program - focused on building life skills and improving adult literacy.[245]

Generally, demobilization and reintegration of former combatants was viewed as an afterthought, receiving secondary importance compared to the establishment of the SANDF. According to scholars Motumi and McKenzie, “demobilization (was) poorly planned, badly executed and wholly inadequate in meeting the needs of ex-combatants. It failed to take into account some of the lessons learnt from demobilization processes in other developing countries and, in fact, repeated some of the more obvious mistakes.”[246]

Both victims and perpetrators had to apply for benefits from the TRC. All individual victims of the apartheid regime were required to submit applications to the HRV committee in order to receive victim status - which thereby entitled South African citizens to reparations.[247] Similarly, to prevent blanket amnesty, the TRC required all perpetrators of violence throughout the apartheid era to individually apply for amnesty for specific acts committed.[248] Amnesty applications were required for all parties implicated in violence throughout this time period - NP and ANC alike.

In order for amnesty to be granted, applications had to meet the following criteria:

_      The act “must have been committed in pursuit of a political objective”[249];

_      The act must have occurred within the designated timeframe (1960-1994);

_      Applicants had to agree to “full disclosure of the facts surrounding the abuse”[250];

_      Applicants had to admit fault[251]; and

_      The application must have been received prior to the deadline (originally December 1996).[252]

Only acts considered “violation(s) of human rights”[253] - including abductions, torture, and killings - were considered for amnesty.[254] Less serious crimes were not prosecuted, though neither were they forgiven.

 

Transitional Justice & Punitive Component

Given the sheer size and scope of atrocities committed under apartheid, the South African government realized that not all perpetrators could or should be held accountable through traditional courts of law.[255] Rather than focusing on punitive, criminal justice, the TRC’s conditional amnesty model embodied a form of “restorative justice” - “a justice that would direct attention to the needs and participation of the victim and, in that way, help repair the damage done.”[256] In applying for victimhood status, all victims intentionally “(gave) up the right to later prosecute perpetrators in courts of law.”[257] In exchange, the acknowledgement of crimes would be followed by reparations.

Furthermore, by requiring all perpetrators to apply individually for amnesty, no group at large was absolved of responsibility for its actions throughout the apartheid era.[258] All parties were culpable for their actions - whether crimes were committed by the state or by liberation movements.[259] However, the TRC did decriminalize “the actions of the majority of those who opposed the former state.”[260] Those who had previously been sentenced for crimes including arson, public violence, and attempted murder were released.

 

Reconciliation

The TRC’s truth-seeking agenda was intentionally designed to promote reconciliation.[261] The South African government aimed to eliminate the possibility of collective amnesia by tackling the atrocities of apartheid head-on, thereby establishing a nationally-recognized and agreed upon history.[262]

Reconciliation consisted of four sub-dimensions:

1.     “Interracial reconciliation (rejection of interracial stereotypes or prejudice);

2.     Political tolerance (willingness to allow one’s political foes full rights of political contestation);

3.     Support for human rights principles (and, in particular, the rule of law); (and)

4.     The extension of legitimacy to the political institutions of the new South Africa (Parliament and Constitutional Court).”[263]

 

Community Engagement

All South African citizens were eligible to participate in the TRC process - either by submitting victim statements or amnesty applications. In addition, TRC hearings were made public and broadcast on national television.[264]

 

Cost Estimate & Financing

To achieve its lofty goals, the TRC had an annual operating budget of USD$18 million, funded by taxpayers and international donors.[265] Initially, reparations to victims and their families were projected at USD$640 million[266] - “the commission proposed that each victim or family should receive approximately $3,500 USD each for six years.”[267] Reparations were to be funded through a ‘wealth tax’ - drawing taxes from industries that unduly benefited from apartheid.[268] However, this program did not come to fruition. “Instead the government established a reparations fund with money from the State and from donors; using this fund, it paid a lump sum of R30,000 each to about 23,000 persons who registered with the TRC as ‘victims’.”[269]

 

Results

In total, 135,927 former combatants were integrated into the newly established SANDF.[270] In addition, “close to 6,000 soldiers were formally demobilized from the SANDF in 1995, the majority of which were originally from the MK or APLA.”[271] The demobilization process continued into the late 1990s and early 2000s, when an additional 30,000 former combatants were demobilized.[272] Unfortunately, demobilization and reintegration programs were poorly planned and executed. A 2003 survey of demobilized ex-combatants found that 66 percent were unemployed - largely relying on their families for financial support and housing - while more than a one-third were suffering from some form of psychological distress.[273]

As part of the TRC, more than 20,000 victim statements were submitted, of which approximately 2,000 were heard publicly.[274] In total, more than 7,000 applications for amnesty were received, of which 849 were granted and 5,392 were rejected.[275] “The most common reason for denying an application (was) that no political motive was attached to the action for which amnesty was sought.”[276] In the end, no group escaped without blame for its actions, including the ANC.[277] When formal proceedings concluded in 2002, the TRC published a seven-volume report, detailing the findings of the seven-year process.[278] However, some issues remain unresolved - particularly in the case of reparations.

 

Lessons Learned

As one of the most widely studied transitional justice models to date, the South African model’s efficacy has been hotly contested. Regardless of whether South Africa can be deemed a wholehearted success or not, the country’s trials and tribulations have helped pave the way forward for similar programs, reinforcing the following lessons:

_      Need for Bottom-Up Reintegration: South African reintegration and reconciliation strategies have been criticized for their top-down nature. In the case of reintegration, critics argue there was lack of sufficient coordination with former combatants or civil society organizations prior to implementing reintegration programming.[279] The government did not sufficiently engage ex-combatants or their families in order to fully understand the needs of these populations. Similarly, many believe that the “TRC did more to isolate NGOs from the process than to draw upon them.”[280] Rather, more voices should have been brought to the planning table, to ensure all civil society voices and perspectives were heard.

 

_      Increase Female Involvement: When analyzing the outcomes of the TRC in particular, it is clear that women’s needs were not fully addressed. Women only accounted for one percent of all victim statements submitted throughout the TRC process - despite being highly involved in the liberation struggle - leaving the extent of sexual violence largely unknown.[281] Additional outreach, specifically targeting underrepresented minorities, may have been an appropriate strategy for addressing this gap.

 

_      Address Community Grievances: Rather than viewing apartheid as a system that worked against communities, the TRC “individualized” victims - categorizing apartheid as an action of the state against individuals.[282] Given that not all victims came forward with statements, this limited both the TRC’s ability to hold accountable all perpetrators, as well as its acknowledgment of systematic racial discrimination and oppression. Community-based systems, as described in the Indonesian and Colombia models, may have been more efficient in addressing victims’ grievances.

 

_      Challenge of Holding All Perpetrators Accountable: Initially, the majority of amnesty applications received came from former MK and APLA combatants - many of whom were at that time imprisoned for crimes committed during the apartheid era.[283] With time, more SADF forces came forward with applications, largely spurred by the successful prosecution of SADF strongman, Eugene de Kock - “a notorious apartheid assassin who, during his trial, provided extensive information about other senior state operatives who were involved in gross human rights abuses.”[284] Without the imminent threat of successful prosecution, many perpetrators of human rights abuses sought to avoid involvement in South Africa’s reconciliation process.

 

_      Need for Appropriate Financial Planning: Although the TRC promised reparations to victims and their families, the commission failed to deliver on these commitments in a timely fashion - or to the level originally suggested.[285] Thus, failure to adequately meet the needs of victims has been seen as one of the South African model’s greatest failings.



Applicability to Boko Haram

Given the size and scale of apartheid atrocities, South Africa’s relatively peaceful transition to democracy in its aftermath, and international support for the ultimately “victorious insurgency” group, the South African model is generally inappropriate for reintegration and reconciliation of former Boko Haram combatants. However, while this model may not be suited for wholesale replication, there are certainly components that may be applicable to the Nigerian context, if appropriately adapted.

1.     Truth Telling and Dialogue: The TRC’s truth-seeking agenda exhibits wide potential across varying country contexts - as evidenced by its emulation in other country models, including Mali, Indonesia, and Colombia, as well as in its less formal implementation through the Somalian Xeer system. It provides an opportunity to bring multiple parties together in dialogue, to discuss the collective atrocities of the past. Given accusations of human rights abuses by both Nigerian military forces and Boko Haram combatants, incorporating a space for open and honest dialogue, addressing the grievances of both sides, may be beneficial for the reintegration process and assist in preventing collective amnesty.

 

2.     Reparations: Although relatively unsuccessful in the case of South Africa, reparations provide an opportunity to acknowledge community suffering and losses. Reintegration programs that focus solely on perpetrators - often providing financial assistance as ex-combatants transition back into normal society, as in the case of the Sri Lankan model - may be met with resentment by local populations who have suffered at the hands of soldiers who are now reaping financial benefits. Community-level reparations may provide the necessary incentive for community members to accept former Boko Haram combatants back into their villages. Furthermore, the more limited scale of the conflict in Nigeria may be better suited for these financial incentives.

 

3.     Conditional Amnesty: Based on numerous conversations with experts on and from the region, it appears evident that Nigerian civilians are largely unwilling to accept former Boko Haram combatants back into their communities without some form of punitive justice. Given Nigeria’s previous attempt at amnesty in the Niger Delta, the government does not appear keen to follow this same path for Boko Haram. While blanket amnesty cannot, and arguably should not, be promoted, it may be possible to adapt South Africa’s conditional amnesty model for use with Boko Haram. According to this model, all former combatants would have to fully acknowledge past acts and a fact-finding judicial body would need to be created, with authority of subpoena, search, and seizure to corroborate perpetrators’ accounts. All findings should be made public, thereby holding perpetrators accountable, while limiting the strain of criminal proceedings on a currently weak Nigerian legal system.

 

 

 

 

Aceh, Indonesia: Reintegration of the GAM

Marisa Lowe

 

Abstract: The Free Aceh Movement and Boko Haram share similar underlying grievances, but the broader circumstances for these two post-conflict reintegrations diverge significantly. In 2005, the Free Aceh Movement, a separatist group based in the northern region of the island Sumatra, signed a peace agreement with the Indonesian government. The Free Aceh Movement had fought for 29 years advocating for self-determination and rule by Sharia law, and against economic marginalization and military abuses in the region. While its grievances are similar to Boko Haram, the Acehnese case is unique due to the 2004 tsunami, corresponding influx of international aid, peace resolution, and granting of regional autonomy. These differences aside, Aceh’s programmatic challenges and successes should inform a reintegration program for Boko Haram in Nigeria. Official reintegration efforts focused on political access and economic assistance over social, cultural, psychological, institutional, and other elements. In Aceh, target populations, for both combatants and victims, were difficult to define. Community-based projects were a viable form of economic assistance, but proved futile for social reconciliation and inadequate for extensive impact on welfare and poverty. The Aceh Monitoring Mission, an international body meant to ensure the implementation of the peace agreement, provided support and confidence to the transition process. The Aceh Monitoring Mission and combined efforts of the Government of Indonesia, international organizations, foreign governments, and Acehnese people are credited with Aceh’s transition to lasting peace.

 

Insurgency Description

The Free Aceh Movement (GAM) was formed in Aceh, Indonesia in 1976 with objectives of self-determination and implementation of Sharia law for the Aceh region. From 1976 to 2005, GAM-related violence and corresponding retribution by the Indonesian military came in waves. Military campaigns launched in response to GAM activity killed thousands and resulted in gross human rights violations. Following the 2004 tsunami that killed 167,000 in Aceh, the GAM and Government of Indonesia (GoI) reached a peace agreement: the Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding (MoU), signed in 2005.[286] Reintegration efforts began with this peace agreement and continue to the present day.

 

Causal Elements

GAM is only the most organized, recent iteration of Acehnese insurgency. GAM’s expressed grievances included economic marginalization (particularly involving Aceh’s vast oil and gas resources), lack of religious and political autonomy (seeking a legal system based on a more conservative interpretation of Islamic scripture), and human rights abuses by the military.[287] Surveys of the Acehnese people revealed consistent understandings of the conflict’s causes among communities.[288]

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

In accordance with the MoU, the Aceh Reintegration Agency (BRA) was formed under the Governor of Aceh.[289] Since 2005, BRA itself has seen variations in structure, form and activities.[290]

 

International Agency Involvement

International support flooded Aceh following the destructive 2004 tsunami.[291] The international agencies involved in reintegration included the International Organization of Migration (IOM), European Union (EU), World Bank (WB), and United Nations Development Programme (UNDP). The participation of these organizations in reintegration efforts varied from funding to monitoring to technical assistance to establishing and running programs. IOM was invited by the GoI to oversee the release of political prisoners and help provide for the reintegration of former combatants. IOM also facilitated critical public information sharing by printing and distributing copies of the Helsinki MoU. IOM also managed the Village Prosperity Through Peace Program (Makmu Gampong Kareuna Dame) for quick-impact projects (QIP) and traditional reconciliation activities for communities.[292]

The World Bank began the Kecamatan Development Project in October 2006, which was transformed into the Kecamatan-Based Reconstruction and Reintegration Program (KDP) and used by BRA to provide community-based projects for victim reintegration efforts.[293]

The UNDP’s Strengthening Sustainable Peace and Development in Aceh (SSDPA) Program provided technical assistance to BRA, as well as programs for livelihood improvement of ex-combatants and victims of conflict.[294] The UNDP, in conjunction with the EU, also created the Strengthening Access to Justice in Indonesia (SAJI) project, which aimed to clarify legal framework for customary law, support transitional justice, and increase awareness and access to legal counsel.[295]

The Aceh Monitoring Mission (AMM) was deployed to Aceh from 2005 to 2006 to oversee the implementation of the MoU, including reintegration. The AMM was mandated by the MoU and was supported by the EU, Norway, Switzerland, and some ASEAN countries. The AMM served as an unarmed supervisor, deploying two hundred monitors across eleven field offices, facilitating discussion between the GoI and GAM, and responding to violations. The AMM was deemed a tremendous success in dealing with the many security questions of Aceh’s transition from conflict to peace.[296]

 

NGO Involvement

            Civil society representatives were given initial seats at the BRA table, although their roles and continued representation remain unclear.[297] International aid organizations often funded civil society organizations to provide services, such as the UNDP’s Gender-Responsive Approach for Reintegration and Peace Stabilization, which provided funds to support the reintegration of female ex-combatants and women left out of the official DDR process.[298]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

            The three populations for reintegration identified in the MoU were former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians.[299] Through political negotiations, BRA’s Economic Empowerment program also came to include GAM non-combatants and the Pembela Tanah Air (PETA) pro-Indonesian militants.[300] Beyond individual programs, community-based projects provided broader support across the Acehnese population.

            It was expected that GAM would provide a list of three thousand combatants to be served by BRA’s ex-combatant reintegration efforts. The creation of such a list did not occur for three reasons. First, the number of GAM combatants was far greater than three thousand. Second, GAM feared the peace process would collapse and former combatants would be punished. Third, GAM sought to control the reintegration process, as opposed to receiving directives from the government. Remarkably, instead of receiving the list of GAM combatants, BRA provided allowances to GAM that were spread more widely among its network.[301]

GAM, BRA, and Acehnese civil society agreed on a ten-point criteria for “affected civilians.” The criteria included loss of life, physical damage, loss of wealth, displacement, injury, illness, and loss of employment.[302] Unfortunately, these criteria had little effect on the actual distribution of assistance.[303]

BRA solicited victim applications and was inundated with over 50,000 applications covering 600,000 individuals.[304] Unable to deal with such volume, BRA turned to the WB-funded KDP project to facilitate victim assistance. KDP provided community-based assistance to villages, using facilitators to help identify conflict victims and their needs. Villages were required to hold four community meetings in order to identify, analyze, and address their needs with a project. These meetings were meant to emphasize community ownership and the last meeting was devoted to report accountability after project completion. Allocation size was based on the following criteria: a village’s level of conflict, history of successful KDP grants, exposure to violence, village size, and conflict victims’ needs. Following the first round of village funding, BRA-KDP reverted their program to individual allocations.[305]

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

In accordance with the MoU, GAM combatants were given amnesty. Political prisoners were released with the exception of “disputed cases.” Article 2.2 and 2.3 of the MoU mandated the creation of a human rights court (HRC) and a truth and reconciliation commission (TRC). Article 1.4.5 held that military personnel who had committed civilian crimes would be tried in Aceh civilian court.[306]

The implementation of the human rights court and truth and reconciliation commission outlined in the MoU stalled. For ten years, victims and activists sought the creation of HRC and TRC institutions for justice, in addition to the public release of official government reports on human rights abuses by security forces.[307] In November 2015, the Aceh provincial parliament announced the selection of a team for the TRC to operate between 2016 and 2021.[308] There have been no reports of the commission’s commencement.

Similar to Somalia’s Xeer model, UNDP SAJI engaged the informal legal system and worked closely with the Aceh Customary Council (MAA) to strengthen the quality of Aceh’s adat system.[309] Adat courts were officially abolished in 1951, but adat law is still applied informally in a village setting. The adat process involves a formal complaint, hearing sessions, and a signed letter of agreement.[310] Thirteen of twenty-three districts in Aceh now provide funding support for adat justice mechanisms, and 91 percent of justice users have reported satisfaction with quality.[311]

 

Community Engagement

            Ex-GAM combatants returned to their original communities. The level of community acceptance for former combatants was high, greater than 90 percent.[312] Through KDP, communities were engaged in discussions to determine funding for community-based projects.

 

 Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

Official BRA funds focused on economic development and did not include allocations for social reintegration.[313] International aid organizations, like IOM, however, facilitated traditional reconciliation ceremonies.[314] IOM, through their Information, Counseling & Referral Services (ICRS), gave one million rupiah (US $110) to villages in conjunction with KDP for traditional peusijeuk ceremonies.[315] The peusijeuk involves pouring sacred water over participants, symbolizing the “cooling down” or lasting peace after conflict.[316] Aside from the peusijeuk, many reconciliation ceremonies involved Islam, including sermons from imams and readings from the Qur'an. In total, 74 percent of former GAM members reported attending a reconciliation ceremony in their village.[317] Evaluations of KDP-treated communities did not reveal a significant difference in social cohesion.

            The MoU provided Aceh with special autonomy and allowed the formation of local political parties. Since then, two GAM-affiliated Aceh governors have been elected.[318]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

            BRA provided economic assistance and capital to former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians without much training for the use of such capital. Given this criticism, in 2009, BRA began providing limited vocational training in certain districts. Aside from BRA, IOM has provided vocational assistance to former prisoners and combatants.[319]

Incentives

            The allocation of cash to former combatants, political prisoners, and affected civilians provided incentives for participation, overburdening BRA far beyond its capacity. Reintegration of ex-combatants in Niger Delta experienced similar problems with cash incentives, although the Acehnese case included victims and converted to community-based projects as a solution.

 

Cost Estimate

            Cost numbers vary widely, based on program, agency, and timeframe inclusion. BRA’s pool for allocation totaled to $150 million.[320] Another report from the Asia Foundation estimated that $365 million was spent between 2005 and 2009 by GoI and international donors on peacebuilding.[321] Financing was provided by the Government of Indonesia, foreign governments, and international organizations.

 

Results & Metrics

            There is copious data on Aceh, including household perception surveys, case studies, aid and donor reviews, economic surveys, violence incident reports, and more.[322]

The Aceh Reintegration Livelihood Surveys (ARLS) was a large-scale household and village head survey measuring the impact of the World Bank’s KDP program. The survey was meant to capture community and household welfare, individual behavior and attitudes, and community cohesion. Attitudes towards the KDP program were positive; 94 percent of villagers treated said the program was helpful. Attitudes towards welfare were also positive; individuals in treated communities believed their welfare had increased with KDP. There was no direct, measurable evidence, however, of program impact on welfare. The survey displayed no resulting increase in social cohesion and trust in government.[323]

Economic metrics reveal that post-conflict efforts have had little positive effect. Aceh’s Gross Regional Domestic Product (GRDP) growth has been negative since 2004, save for 2006, which was affected by the influx of post-tsunami recovery funds. Aceh remains poorer than the rest of Indonesia, and its Human Development Index (HDI) has not improved at the same rate as other Indonesian provinces. Foreign Direct Investment remains incredibly low. The difference in poverty between high intensity and low intensity conflict areas within Aceh, however, is significant; high intensity conflict districts generally have lower poverty rates than low intensity conflict districts.[324]

The National Violence Monitoring System (NVMS), a World-Bank program, uses local news reports to detect and record violent incidents across Indonesia.[325] According to NVMS, Aceh has seen a 94 percent decline in annual fatalities since conflict ended in 2005.[326] NVMS has also revealed patterns in the existing post-conflict violence in Aceh: first, violent incidents have grown more frequent but less fatal; second, violence is highly concentrated in areas of high intensity conflict during the war; third, violence is often triggered by elections, resources, and ethnic and religious tensions.[327]

 

Challenges

Defining the Target Population: The MoU was a pioneer in acknowledging victims as a target population for reintegration. Between former combatants, political prisoners, and “affected civilians,” however, the line between the target population and community for reintegration was difficult to define. Damages could be calculated as lives lost, monetary losses, unemployment, physical destruction, and psychological harm. Approximately 15,000 individuals lost their lives and damages exceeded $10.7 trillion.[328] A Harvard University medical team found that 33 percent of Acehnese suffered from depression and 48 percent from anxiety.[329] Forty-nine percent of the Acehnese population considers themselves to be a victim of the conflict.[330] The vastness of victimhood made verifying individual allocation targets next to impossible.

Individual Allocations vs. Community-Based Projects: BRA-KDP experimented with both individual allocations and community-based projects. Individual allocations were challenging because of verification and coordination between different programs. The Multi-Stakeholder Review of Post-Conflict Programming in Aceh (MSR) suggested that individual allocations did not have a significant effect on economic welfare.[331]

Community-based projects, on the other hand, had more widespread and measurable impacts on perceptions of welfare. Communities that received KDP saw an 11-point decrease in “poor” populations perceived by village heads. The community-based programs were more likely to include marginalized and minority groups, although there was no evidence of stronger social cohesion or government relations as a result.[332]

Holding Government Forces Accountable for Human Rights Abuses: The Acehnese NGO Coalition for Human Rights registered over ten thousand cases of human rights abuses during the conflict.[333] Despite mandates in the MoU for a HRC and TRC, neither system has been instituted.

Successes

            The Aceh peace and reintegration process has been viewed as a success, primarily because of lasting peace since the signing of the MoU in 2005. Community-funded projects had a greater effect on economic welfare than individual allocations, although the measured effect was small.

 

Similarities to Boko Haram

Religion: While both GAM and Boko Haram espoused literalist Islamic ideals, their different approaches to religion were significant. The practice of more conservative Islam in Aceh served as a distinguishing factor between the province and the rest of Indonesia. One of the initial core demands of the GAM was the implementation of Sharia law in an autonomous Aceh.[334] Since gaining special autonomy, Sharia law has been enforced in Aceh province, punishing individuals for offenses such as alcohol, homosexuality, and gambling.[335] Boko Haram, on the other hand, arguably uses Islam as more of a tool for resonance than an objective for practical implementation.

Military Abuses: In response to both GAM and Boko Haram, the military committed abuses which in turn galvanized support for the respective insurgencies. Notably, during the Aceh conflict in 2000, the Indonesian justice system convicted and jailed twenty-four soldiers for committing a massacre at an Islamic school.[336] While the trial was seen as a test of the Indonesian government’s commitment to human rights and willingness to bring justice to abuses in Aceh, human rights abuses and gross violence continued after 2000 until 2005. The failure to publish reports of military abuses and set up a HRC or TRC has continued to agitate the Acehnese people and human rights advocates.

Oil: The sharing of wealth from oil resources in Aceh, like northern Nigeria, was a major source of conflict. One way to view the GAM insurgency is that of an uprising against “oppressive natural resource exploitation.”[337] Oil extraction led to greater government appropriation of revenue and increased military presence in Aceh. Despite its role as an impetus for conflict, wealth sharing was not a primary topic of negotiation in the MoU. While the near depletion of Aceh’s oil resources reduced its significance in the post-conflict setting, the lack of any economic plan or prospects for ex-combatants and Aceh overall remains a liability.[338]

Poverty: Both Aceh and Northern Nigeria are poor relative to the rest of their countries. For Aceh, widespread poverty and lack of economic development and foreign investment are continuing challenges for the region and its stability. Increasing economic inequality in the province is especially concerning given correlations between violence and resource tensions in NVMS data.

 

Differences from Boko Haram

            Tsunami: The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami Aceh drastically affected the circumstances for post-conflict reintegration. First, the utter devastation caused by the tsunami inflicted widespread injury to fighters and non-fighters alike. Everyone was a victim. Second, the tsunami’s destruction spurred a massive outpouring of international aid to Indonesia. Post-conflict rehabilitation thus coincided with post-tsunami rehabilitation and it is hard to distinguish between the effects of the two.

            International aid organizations provided tremendous supplemental support to the post-conflict reintegration process. While BRA focused on financial allocations and economic development for the target populations outlined in the MoU, international programs extended aid to psychological elements, traditional reconciliation ceremonies like peusijeuk, skills training, and victim populations unacknowledged by the MoU and BRA.

Community Attitudes: As mentioned above, more than 90 percent of ex-combatants were accepted into their former communities. This is due in part to existing community support for GAM, as well as IOM’s public information campaigns. At the height of the conflict, GAM controlled between 70 and 80 percent of Aceh and received considerable community support. Community support was galvanized by shared grievances of the GoI, including brutal counterinsurgency operations, corruption, ineffectiveness, and a failure to implement special autonomy for Aceh.[339] Acehnese communities supported the GAM in their insurgency and their reintegration. This stands in stark contrast to the reported lack of community support for Boko Haram in Nigeria.

Organizational Cohesion: From insurgency to negotiation to reintegration to political representation, GAM leadership maintained adequate control to communicate and navigate on behalf of the group. The splintered nature of Boko Haram, on the other hand, is a concern for any attempt to deal with the insurgency as a unit. Some disagreement within GAM has triggered violence in Aceh. Ex-combatants who split with the Aceh Transition Committee (KPA), the BRA’s post-MoU organization, due to disagreements about payouts, have been responsible for grenade incidents, electoral violence, and crime.[340]

Peace Agreement & Political Representation: The conflict between GAM and GoI underwent multiple failed peace processes before the signing of the MoU. The peace negotiation processes were intensive and compromising. Among the many assessments of Acehnese peace and reintegration, there is consensus that a predominant factor for peace was the political representation granted to Aceh in the MoU. The designation of Aceh as a special province provided the region and its people with greater political access and influence in Jakarta, and long-desired cultural, social, and political autonomy in Aceh. From Jakarta’s perspective, this autonomy has had its drawbacks, including the implementation of Sharia law in Aceh, which has corresponded with human rights violations like the caning of women and fears of increasing violent extremism in Indonesia.[341]

 

Recommendations for Boko Haram in Nigeria

            Given Aceh’s programmatic challenges and successes and its similarities to the case of Boko Haram in Nigeria, the following are specific recommendations for a reintegration program:

1.     Coherent Strategy & Objective: The MoU described a desire for sustained peace, but beyond political concessions and DDR, it is unclear how the GoI and GAM envisioned sustained peace.[342] The official reintegration agency, BRA, primarily facilitated economic assistance with little economic impact. While Aceh reintegration was successful at maintaining relative peace, its success in other areas, such as welfare, economy, and social cohesion, is less clear. The multiplicity of programs and stakeholders allowed for greater coverage, but also represented different objectives, strategies, and roles, which were confusing if not conflicting. The lack of a clear strategy for reintegration meant that effects were less limited and streamlined. Ultimately, without coherent objectives, there is no clear path for program evaluation and improvement.

 

2.     Monitoring Mission: Like Colombia, Aceh had an international monitoring mission that is credited with much of Aceh’s successful transition from conflict to peace. Beyond its role as a supervisor and facilitator of disarmament, demobilization, and reintegration, the AMM served to instill trust and confidence in the implementation process. While few violations of the MoU occurred, the presence of the AMM assured participants that any minor slip-up would not jeopardize overall progress towards peace.

 

3.     Truth & Reconciliation Commission: Mimicking the South African solution, the MoU called for the creation of a TRC, a promise which has yet to be fulfilled. Given problems in Aceh, including lacking social cohesion and trust in government, greater reconciliatory efforts are needed. A structured process of evaluation, reconciliation, and even punishment, is necessary for a government to pivot from adversary to authority, especially given the military abuses that occurred.

 

4.     Community-Based Projects: Like Sri Lanka, Aceh utilized community-based projects for reintegration, which had greater impact on perceived welfare than individual allocations. The difference between perceived and actual impact and its benefits remains a question for further research. The case of Aceh suggests small, relative success with community-based projects over individual allocations in the context of widespread conflict and victimhood.

 

Colombia: Peace Agreement with the FARC

Lauren Wyman

 

Abstract: The peace process between the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC) and the government of Colombia can shed light on appropriate paths to take when considering the reintegration of Boko Haram combatants into Nigerian society. While the nature of the militancy in Colombia is different than that of Boko Haram - Marxist/Leninist versus pseudo-religious - the insurgencies are similar in their use of kidnapping, child soldiers and links with illicit activities as well as their presence in traditionally marginalized areas. The Colombian peace agreement was defeated in a national referendum, however the government and the FARC revised the agreement and it was passed through Congress on November 30, 2016. Nigeria can look to the Colombian peace process for a multi-sectoral approach to peace, including land reform, a path to political participation, victims’ reparations, truth telling commissions as well as demobilization monitored through a verification mission of government, FARC and UN officials. The Colombian peace agreement aims to build peace from both the bottom-up and top-down, fostering a culture of peace and reconciliation, which could inform the Nigerian path to reintegration of Boko Haram fighters. The Nigerian government may consider assessing public opinion prior to reintegration.

 

Time Period: 1964 - present

Since the 1960s, the civil conflict between the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC), the government, and paramilitary groups have resulted in over six million displaced and 220,000 dead throughout the country.[343] The government of Colombia has launched numerous peace talks with the FARC, starting in 1982, but each has crumbled.[344]  The fourth and most recent round of peace talks, initiated in 2012 by President Juan Manuel Santos, were ratified by the federal government and by the FARC, but were rejected by the public through a national referendum on October 2, 2016. Many Colombians opposed the peace deal for being too conciliatory toward the FARC, criticizing the lack of jail time and the guarantee of seats in Congress to the FARC political party.[345]

The opposition to the peace agreement was spearheaded by former President Alvaro Uribe, whose hardline stance on the FARC is well known in Colombia.[346] However, the government and the FARC returned to the negotiating table in Havana, Cuba, after the 2016 treaty was rejected. On November 11, 2016, a revised peace agreement was produced that incorporated the opposition’s suggestions. The agreement was ratified by Congress on November 30, 2016.

 

Insurgency Description

The FARC (in Spanish: Fuerzas Armadas Revolutionarios de Colombia, Ejercito del Pueblo) is a Marxist-Leninist militant organization formed in the 1960s to institute land reform and redistribution in the countryside.[347] It emerged as an offshoot of the Colombian Communist Party and was violently repressed by the right-wing government starting in 1964, through a military attack on the group in the Tolima department in May of that year.[348]  The FARC emerged after a particularly violent stage in Colombian history known as La Violencia - the Violence - in which conservative and liberal politics ballooned into a rural war and land grab. [349]

The FARC fueled its operations through kidnapping and demanding ransom for targets, primarily politicians and social elites. In its early years, the FARC often provided medical care, education and defense services for marginalized communities in areas where government was lacking. The organization started to incorporate narcotrafficking into its strategy in the 1980s as well as illegal mining and oil extraction to fund operations.[350] At the height of the FARC’s power in the late 1990s and early 2000s, there were nearly twenty thousand FARC soldiers. As of 2016, the number of soldiers is approximately seven thousand, including three thousand women. Despite the decrease, there is a resilient network of FARC sympathizers throughout the country.[351] Of note, the Colombian populations inhabiting the rural countryside, especially indigenous communities, have been victims of attacks of not only the FARC, but also attacks by paramilitary groups and by the Colombian military.[352] 

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

On a federal level, the National Victim’s Unit (UARIV) keeps track of IDPs and connects conflict victims to social services, like healthcare and education.[353] This organization works also with local governments to implement Law 1448 of 2011, which recognizes the rights of people – particularly IDPs - to settle in land that was taken from them by the FARC or paramilitary groups.

Other civilian agencies involved include the High Commission for Post-Conflict, Human Rights and Security, which was formed in anticipation of the peace accords being ratified by the public and is intended to facilitate the national transition to peace. Governmental agencies supporting social protection programs in conflict zones include the National Planning Department, the Department of Social Prosperity, the Institute for Family Welfare and the National Department of Health, which provide services for conflict victims, including food assistance, emergency healthcare, school feeding programs and land redistribution. Additionally, other federal agencies like the Ministry of the Environment are developing agricultural and environmental projects to promote peace and climate change adaptation.

 

Military Involvement: Plan Colombia & Aerial Spraying

During the 2000s, under the presidency of Alvaro Uribe, the Colombian military launched a massive offensive known as Plan Colombia. This was a $9 billion influx of military aid, primarily from the US, to bolster Colombian counter-FARC military capacities, as well as intelligence. Through Plan Colombia, dozens of high-level FARC leaders were killed, which significantly weakened the group. [354] In recent years, the government has targeted coca plantations and sprayed them with herbicides, destroying the crops to reduce the sources of FARC funding. However, human rights groups decried the practice for causing illnesses (including cancer) and for disproportionately impacting indigenous and Afro-Colombian communities.[355]

Within the proposed peace accords, the Colombian military are to be stationed around concentration zones, where FARC members will demobilize and hand in their weapons to the United Nations. However, it is also important to note that the military was accused of war crimes as well during the fifty-two-year long conflict.[356] Due to the tension and the history of human rights abuses on both sides, the Colombian government asked the UN to accompany the turning in of arms during the peace process and to run the concentration zones.

 

Impetus for Reintegration

Plan Colombia severely weakened FARC. By eliminating FARC leadership, this military offensive reduced the social cohesion of the group, putting them on the move frequently, and forcing the FARC to resort to defensive tactics like bombings, rather than offensive attacks to seize territory. In recent years, linked with the hardline military campaign and with national advertising campaigns, the FARC had experienced a rush of desertion, reducing their numbers to less than half of their peak and providing the federal government with intelligence.[357] 

President Juan Manuel Santos, backed by the international community, made it a priority of his administration to finalize a peace agreement with the FARC. With hardline national policy on narco-trafficking, profits from coca and cocaine production were decreasing. The FARC had also proclaimed that it hoped to transition to bring its battle to the political sphere as its own political party.

 

Criteria for Reintegration

All FARC combatants will be eligible for reintegration under the current peace agreement, as long as they hand in their weapons, help identify missing persons, and confess their crimes to a peace tribunal, through a truth and reconciliation process, based closely on South Africa’s Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Those who admit to their crimes are eligible to alternative sentences and restorative justice to make amends to victims - by working on community farms, demining, reforestation, substituting illicit crops, etc.[358] However if the ex-combatants lie or do not turn in their weapons, they will be subject to up to twenty years in prison. As of now, the FARC is demobilizing in its entirety; there are no known FARC dissidents who are refusing to hand in their weapons and accept peace. Certain crimes disqualify ex-combatants from amnesty, including sexual violence, kidnapping, torture, extrajudicial killings, forced disappearances and recruitment of minors.[359] The original peace agreement received a lot of backlash for its “soft” stance on the FARC - specifically that most soldiers would receive no jail time.

In terms of the reintegration of child soldiers, within fifteen days of the signing of the new agreement, the government will develop a special program for their effective reintegration. The National Reincorporation Council will be in charge of this program.[360]

 

Reconciliation, Social & Political Elements

The approach that the government has taken to negotiations has been that the FARC should make reparations for the damage caused by the prolonged conflict and contribute to making the post-conflict society better. The agreement recognizes the differential needs of LGBTI and women ex-combatants and the psycho-social needs of victims of sexual and gender-based violence. The agreement emphasizes that in post-conflict society, it is important to create spaces for communities to express their suffering and reconcile with ex-combatants.[361] Additionally, in the new agreement, the FARC will turn in all of its assets to the government to compensate victims. This echoes the South African model, where reparations were planned to come from a wealth tax on industries unduly benefited from apartheid.

For the sake of this brief, I will go into two key elements of the reconciliation process outlined in the peace agreement that can inform our recommendations on Boko Haram: concentration zones and political participation.

Concentration Zones:  After the peace agreement is signed, demobilizing FARC soldiers will migrate to twenty-three concentration zones, which are located in isolated regions of the country. Similar to the Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCS) in Sri Lanka, the concentration zones will serve as a halfway zone for the FARC, where they will officially demobilize and transition to civilian life. Unarmed civilians can enter the concentration zones to provide social services like healthcare and education; the military is not allowed within one-kilometer of each zone. Within 180 days of the signing of the peace agreement, the FARC must turn in all weapons to the United Nations. Once the final peace agreement is signed, fighters will go to these zones for up to six months and then will be able to move on a restricted basis throughout the country. A monitoring and verification mission made up of UN, FARC and Colombian governmental officials has access to each zone to ensure compliance.[362] Demobilized FARC soldiers, after leaving the concentration zones, are eligible for 90 percent of minimum wage for two years as well as grants to start businesses.[363]

Political Participation:  After demobilization, FARC leaders will be able to run for office and thus participate in the Colombian political sphere.[364] Over the next two election cycles, the FARC will have non-voting seats in the national Congress, after which they will be subject to the normal electoral process. Under the new peace agreement, the FARC political party will receive less government funding than in the original agreement.[365] 

 

Community Engagement

Public opinion on the peace agreements was polled frequently and captured in a national referendum on October 2, 2016. As mentioned above, the public rejected the peace agreement 50.2 percent to 49.7 percent - primarily in zones that are linked to former President Alvaro Uribe, specifically the Antioquia department. While the polls suggested for months that the peace would win by a landslide (NB: the international community was already congratulating Colombia on achieving peace before the referendum) many people throughout the country had deeply opposed it. Remarkably, many of the communities that were most impacted by the conflict were the ones who most strongly supported the peace.[366]

 

Lessons Learned

Colombia’s peace is an ongoing process: the final agreement was just passed by Congress on November 30, 2016. However, several lessons about the process of forming a peace agreement can be gleaned from Colombia’s experience:

1.     Engage with opposition parties when developing the peace agreement so that it is amenable across the political spectrum. The campaign to refuse the peace accords, spearheaded by former President Alvaro Uribe, was a stronger voice politically than Santos’ team had originally imagined. That the government and the FARC took the opposition’s suggestions into consideration while revising the peace agreement shows the strength of the process.

 

2.     Consider how the agreement is being marketed on a national level. Through the peace negotiations, several fears surfaced in the public mind about what would happen post-conflict - specifically that Colombia would turn into a Chavista state, ruled by left-wing rebels. Many Colombians are extremely familiar with the recent economic turmoil in Venezuela, thus feared that the agreement was painted by opposition forces as turning Colombia into the next Venezuela.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

The nature of the militancy in Colombia is profoundly different than that of Boko Haram. Not only did the FARC start as a left-wing, non-religious political movement in rural areas of the country, its links with the drug trade and other militant groups throughout Colombia distinguish it from the pseudo-religious Boko Haram institution. FARC has been active since the 1960s and has suffered high rates of abandonment in recent years whereas Boko Haram only began in earnest in the last six years.

However, there are crucial parallels between the groups, including the recruitment of child soldiers, kidnapping, attacks on military and government infrastructure, heavy military repression by the national government and socio-economic drivers of participation (particularly lack of economic opportunity). Additionally, in both insurgencies, the national militaries allegedly committed extensive human rights abuses.[367]

The following are recommendations that the Nigerian government can extract when thinking about how to reintegrate Boko Haram combatants:

1.     Accompaniment by external verification missions. Any reintegration and demobilization program should be accompanied by impartial external verification missions due to the history of human rights abuses by both the Nigerian military and by Boko Haram. This would follow best practices from Indonesia, where the Aceh Monitoring Mission (AM) instilled confidence in the implementation of disarmament, demobilization and reintegration through engaging impartial third parties.

 

2.     Assessing public opinion before reintegration is vital. As with the national referendum on the peace agreement, which demonstrated that many Colombians were not ready for peace, it will be important for the FGN to survey and address public opinions on reintegration of Boko Haram combatants. An utter lack of popular support will likely condemn reintegration to failure. Considering that reintegration will occur on a community level, the FGN could poll the receiving community’s opinion on reintegration of Boko Haram combatants.

 

3.     Open up alternate routes for dissident voices to be heard. Boko Haram, like the FARC, has been heavily repressed by the national government. Opening up routes for peaceful participation in national politics is a key aspect of demobilization and reintegration. Thus, as with the reintegration of the FARC, former combatants should not be barred from holding political office on the local or national levels or from voting. Ex-combatants cannot be entirely disenfranchised. They must be empowered to express their grievances and goals in a political and peaceful manner, rather than resorting to violence.

 

4.     Incorporate a cross-sectoral approach. While the Colombian peace agreement has just been passed by Congress, its cross-sectoral approach to reintegration may prove to be highly effective in the case of Boko Haram. That the agreement emphasizes rural development as well as political participation and restorative justice through community-based initiatives means that peace will be implemented at the individual, local, regional, and national levels simultaneously. 

 

5.     Open up spaces for people to talk about the conflict and reconciliation. The Colombian government is opening up campaigns to create spaces on the local level for people to come together and talk about their experiences and seek reconciliation, following the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission model. By doing so, the government hopes to foster a national culture of forgiveness and peace, which the Nigerian government could look to as a positive example.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Sri Lanka: Reintegration of the LTTE

Marie Sophie Pocha

 

Abstract: After the 2009 civil war came to an end with government forces defeating the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE), Sri Lanka decided to reintegrate former combatants back into communities. The government set up 24 Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCs) across the country where over 10,000 fighters have been reintegrated since 2009. The Sri Lankan Model is considered one of the most successful approaches to reintegration, because there has been no resurgence of violence since the conflict. However, the lack of strong transitional justice measures remains a reason for criticism and is still a polarizing topic in Sri Lanka and globally. Other parts of the reintegration program, however, can be considered useful examples, namely the community-based approach, the relevance of recreational and social activities, as well as the active participation of private companies in the training efforts for ex-combatants. When it comes to the reintegration of Boko Haram combatants, Sri Lanka does offer some parallels to the conflict, as the LTTE also recruited child soldiers, kidnapped members of the local communities in the north east and committed suicide attacks. Furthermore, the Sri Lankan government faced the issue of few financial means to fund the reintegration program. Still, the Sri Lankan civil war ended with the defeat of the LTTE combatants, which constitutes a significantly different power balance between the parties as a basis for a reintegration program.

 

Model Name: National Framework Proposal for Reintegration of Ex-combatants into Civilian Life in Sri Lanka.

 

Time Period

The Sri Lankan Civil War lasted over twenty-five years and officially ended in May 2009.[368] The government of Sri Lanka began designing their national plan for the reintegration of ex-combatants in March 2009, and completed their plan by the end of July 2009. The government determined that reintegration efforts should take up to one year, preferably ten to twelve months.[369] The reintegration program officially started in October 2009 with the first ex-combatants coming to Protective Accommodation and Rehabilitation Centers (PARCs), the rehabilitation facilities set up by the government during and after the civil war.

 

Insurgency Description

The Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) fought for the independence of northeastern Sri Lanka to establish their own Tamil state. Their insurgency was a reaction to the increasing discrimination of the Tamil minority by the Sinhalan majority in the 1950s through 1970s.[370] The insurgency was comprised of approximately ten thousand fighters in the 1980s and 1990s. They participated in direct armed conflict with the sizeable Sri Lankan army and also gained notoriety for their suicide attacks. The LTTE suicide bombing force is widely acknowledged to have invented the suicide jacket and carried out several hundred suicide attacks. While Sri Lankan civil war is considered an ethnic conflict rather than a religious conflict, there is debate on the relevance of religion in the hostilities, including whether religious nationalism was a relevant driver.[371] Throughout the insurgency women and girls known as the “Birds of Freedom” played an important role and needed to be considered in the reintegration efforts as well.[372] The existence of child soldiers added further complexity to the conflict.

 

Civilian Agency Involvement

The National Framework Proposal for Reintegration of Ex-combatants into Civilian Life (the Framework) was developed by a committee comprised of participants from: the Ministry of Disaster Management and Human Rights; the Ministry of Defence Public Security, Law and Order; the Ministry of Constitutional Affairs and National Integration; and the Ministry of Foreign Employment Promotion and Welfare. The International Labour Organization (ILO) lent both technical and financial support to the framework development process. In addition, previous efforts to develop rehabilitation strategies by the ILO and the Sub-Committee on Livelihoods of the Consultative Committee on Humanitarian Assistance (CCHA) and the Ministry of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources and the ILO as well as the Ministry of Employment and Labour were also taken into account when developing the Framework in 2009.[373] The Bureau of the Commissioner General for Rehabilitation, which had already been established during the conflict in the early 2000s, was tasked with the execution of rehabilitation efforts of terrorists and remains the main agency to oversee the reintegration facilities and activities up until today.[374]

 

Military Involvement

At the outset of the creation of the Framework, the military had recently defeated the LTTE. The role of military in the reintegration process was supposed to be primarily focused on disarmament efforts, including the collection and destruction of weapons in order to increase public safety and security. In furtherance of these goals, ex-combatants could not be employed by the military.[375] 

Security forces were also supposed to contribute to reconstruction and development in the conflict-affected areas. The Lessons Learnt Commission found later, however, that security forces continued to be involved in industries such as private businesses and agriculture that were not part of their mandate, which led to additional grievances for the local population. They described a feeling of unease due to the military’s continuing presence and authority (i.e. over the police forces) in the region and the lack of a civilian administration, which they deemed was crucial to feel more comfortable in their communities again.[376]

For military personnel installed in the reintegration facilities, extensive training and coaching regarding treatment of ex-combatants was mandatory. Based on the low number of reported incidents and reports of mistreatment of detainees in the PARCs, experts have commended the military participation in the reintegration efforts, overall.[377]

While the military in Sri Lanka has also been accused of human rights abuses and war crimes, similar to the conflict between Boko Haram and the Nigerian government, it is important to emphasize that the Sri Lankan army did not have a similarly prominent role in causing the conflict as the Nigerian security forces have had. Thus, a lower level of involvement of the security forces in Nigeria is advisable, while training of security forces in the respectful treatment of arrestees should be pursued to respond to the grievances that caused the conflict and ensure lasting peace.

 

International Agency/Expert Involvement

The ILO lent its technical and financial assistance to the development of the reintegration Framework.[378] The Framework considered international best practices, including lessons learned from Saudi Arabia and Singapore. Several experts from Singapore weighed in and high-level functionaries travelled to Singapore to learn from the program. One of the fundamental parts of the model are the six modes of rehabilitation that Sri Lanka adopted and then adapted to their local circumstances. These six modes included (1) education, (2) vocational reintegration, (3) psychosocial and creative therapies, (4) social, cultural and family reintegration, (5) the spiritual and religious modes and (6) recreational measures. Sri Lanka added one additional mode to the Singaporean model, community rehabilitation, which is why the Sri Lankan model is often called the “6+1 model”.[379]

 

NGO Involvement

NGOs were initially only part of the government’s agenda on a rather limited basis. One of the reasons for this decision could be that NGOs did not enjoy the full trust of the people during the conflict and that people had only little confidence in NGOs’ abilities to promote positive change and stay in the village communities on a long-term basis. In addition, NGOs were often perceived as partisan during the conflict and considered allies of the LTTE, which led to even more distrust by the Sinhalese and Muslim population.[380]

 

Impetus for Reintegration

The reintegration program in Sri Lanka was initiated between the LTTE insurgency and the Sri Lankan government towards the end of the civil war. Sri Lanka claimed victory and the LTTE declared defeat in May 2009, after which several thousand ex-combatants were captured and “surrendered.” Over the twenty-five years of the civil war, several other paramilitary groups had developed. These groups were also considered in the reintegration and rehabilitation efforts.[381]

 

Criteria for Reintegration

The reintegration efforts in Sri Lanka targeted both combatants that were captured as well as those who surrendered. Even though the program did differentiate between the two groups and recognized that each group needs different program designs, captives and those who had surrendered were granted the same rights to participate in the reintegration program. While the program primarily targeted ex-LTTE-combatants who were the largest group of people that were to be reintegrated, the program also targeted other paramilitary groups in the region that had emerged during the civil war.[382]

 

Transitional Justice and Punitive Component

Transitional justice and punitive aspects of the reintegration process of LTTE combatants and other paramilitary groups have been a major object of criticism over the past seven years. While the Framework itself gave the Attorney General the responsibility of designing a transitional justice process tailored to the Sri Lankan experience, the Framework already stated that “[I]t has to be highlighted that, except in cases where a failure to prosecute violates a treaty obligation, the granting of amnesties in appropriate cases to perpetrators of egregious human rights violations is most likely not precluded by international law.”[383] This already signaled that the Sri Lankan government was not likely to prosecute human rights abuses of the Sri Lankan military. Up until today no credible process has been initiated. President Rajapaksa, who was president from 1995 through 2005 did not initiate any independent investigations for alleged crimes nor did he permit any prosecution of any military officials, disappointing the expectations he had raised with his promises.[384]

Forced disappearances played a major role in the Sri Lankan civil war, especially in the last four years of the conflict. The northeastern parts of Sri Lanka as well as the Colombo area were particularly affected by LTTE-led forced disappearances. Until mid-2016 no sincere action had been taken by the government to establish a transparent domestic process to address the disappearances.[385] But in August 2016, the Sri Lankan parliament voted in favor of a bill to create an independent Office on Missing Persons (OMP) comprised of seven members with experience in human rights and humanitarian law, whose mandate is to look into the large number of disappearances in Sri Lanka, specifically throughout the Sri Lankan civil war.[386]

 

Community Engagement

Community engagement played a significant role in the reintegration program in Sri Lanka. Due to the need to save on costs, communities bore significant responsibility for reintegration efforts. A variety of private companies also participated, particularly in the economic reintegration efforts. Throughout the entire reintegration process contact between former combatants and members of the community was encouraged. Such contact could manifest itself in letters, phone calls and visits. To further the participation of the community in the reintegration process, the BCGR held public reintegration festivities once former combatants were released. Upon reinsertion into the community, the responsibility to socially engage former combatants was slowly handed over to the local population in order to allow for a smooth transition back into civilian life for the combatants and a healthy relationship between the different groups.[387] Surveys of former combatants later showed that combatants felt welcomed and well recognized in the villages they returned to.[388]

Ex-combatants were reinserted into their communities of origin or at least into familiar regions to encourage re-engagement with their familial and cultural heritage, unlike Operation Safe Corridor where former combatants are placed in new and unfamiliar environments after they have completed their de-radicalization period. A common criticism that arose within the community was that former combatants received preferential treatment compared to Tamil civilians, because they had greater access to support services and facilities set up by the government, even though the local population had suffered from the decade-long conflict. Accordingly, efforts were made to combat this perception by avoiding special treatment of former combatants when possible.[389]

 

Psychological Elements

Ex-combatants were profiled before entering and upon completion of their reintegration programs. Initially, they were evaluated and classified for psychological counseling based on their crimes and their roles in the organizations. Counselors provided the government with reports on ex-combatants’ progress within two months. Support was available throughout the year-long program and after ex-combatants had been reinserted into the community.[390] It was crucial during the process to differentiate between investigation and rehabilitation to ensure confidentiality crucial to meaningful treatment. Moreover, staff training in ethics and respectful treatment of former combatants was fundamental to engender trust and mutual respect. The fact that beneficiaries felt satisfied with their treatment by facility staff (96.4%) and security personnel (94.6%) indicates that the strict separation between investigation and rehabilitation was successful.[391]

 

Education, Skills, and Job Training

Upon arrival at one of the 24 PARCs that the government installed in the northern and northeastern part of Sri Lanka, the ex-combatants were profiled psychologically and socio-economically. Based on their profiles, they were put into different groups that then focused on vocational training and skill development as well as spiritual classes that would facilitate social reintegration. This system also contributed to the program’s de-radicalization efforts.[392]

In general terms, the government found that rural areas would offer better employment prospects than cities. The main employment opportunities for direct employment of ex-combatants identified by the Framework were agriculture and livestock development, rural industrial development, fisheries, tourism, construction activities, micro-businesses, foreign employment and “green jobs” – meaning agricultural and manufacturing jobs.[393] The Framework emphasized that ex-combatants should have the freedom to choose which type of training they wanted to pursue. These choices included the option to found their own businesses and receive micro-loans.[394]

PARCs offered vocational training as well as higher education to ex-combatants. They could also receive language courses to make them more attractive for the labor market. Once ready for reinsertion into society, the ex-combatants were equipped with basic-needs kits that held a variety of non-cash resources, like household items, hygiene products, clothing etc. Cash payments were also made to the ex-combatants so that they would not return home empty-handed, thereby ensuring they would have some freedom of choice.[395] While this improved their ability to resettle, it also led to the dissatisfaction of other members of the community who did not enjoy the same access to monetary support.

 

Incentives

Ex-combatants of the reintegration program received cash payments for the rehabilitation period in similar amounts as IDPs and resettled communities’ cash grants. Extra costs due to disabilities were taken into account when calculating the amount paid to ex-combatants. There is no further financial assistance given to ex-combatants after the rehabilitation program.[396] The nature of the cash payments was different from the payments made as part of the Niger Delta Amnesty Program. As elaborated above, these payments were rather counterproductive and actually gave people additional incentive to participate in the insurgency. In Sri Lanka, however, the one-time cash payments were made in order to give former combatants an opportunity to resettle in their communities. It is important to note that the Sri Lankan civil war had ended in the defeat of the LTTE combatants, so the risk of a resurgence or of combatants re-joining the insurgency was deemed low. Since the conflict between the Nigerian government and Boko Haram is still an active conflict and because amnesty programs have a negative connotation in the Nigerian society, it would be unwise to draw a simplified comparison between Sri Lanka and Nigeria with regards to cash payments.

 

Follow-up

The ex-combatants were given basic legal documentation and identification cards. In addition to providing legal status to help monitor reintegration efforts, these documents also conferred beneficial psychological effects, and gave ex-combatants a sense of belonging.[397]

Cost Estimate

According to statistics published by the Bureau of the Commissioner General of Rehabilitation (BCGR), the program accumulated costs of USD 9,136,370 between January 2009 and September 2012 to manage and run the rehabilitation centers in Sri Lanka. The annual cost fluctuated depending on the number of ex-combatants in the rehabilitation program.[398]

 

Financing

Due to the fact that the government lacked financing for such a resource-intensive rehabilitation program, collaboration with the private sector was fundamental for this process. The BCGR established over forty partnerships with private companies in Sri Lanka that helped with custodial rehabilitation and community reintegration. A variety of companies offered vocational training programs for LTTE ex-combatants, including language classes, IT training, cooking courses, training in carpentry, masonry and other labor-intensive professions. Participating companies included Holcim (construction/manufacturing sector), Vusaa (IT sector) and a range of smaller local companies.[399] The funding issues that the government was facing actually led to a stronger involvement of the community in the reintegration program and to a program with lower cost.[400]

 

Results/Metrics

The Lessons Learnt Commission’s report was released in 2011 and showed that there were still several areas of concern regarding the execution of the rehabilitation programs. One issue was the lack of teachers that were able to provide the necessary training in the PARCs. While the behavior and effort of the PARC personnel overall was deemed commendable, it lacked instructors to further the staff training efforts.[401] The education in the camps was not perfectly tailored to the background of the ex-combatants. Another issue related to the availability of permanent shelter for ex-combatants, as they reintegrated back into communities with few financial means and the government had not provided sufficient facilities for shelter.[402] The report also found that security forces still intervened with civilians’ private and public life, even during the reconstruction period after the war.

In a survey to determine the success of the reintegration efforts by the Sri Lankan government, combatants gave feedback on their perception of the success of their reintegration a few years after the conflict had ended (published 2015). Using the sample of the northern cities Jaffna and Kilinochchi and Kulatunga, the report found that 90 percent of the ex-combatants felt that they were being well recognized in the villages. In these villages, the proportion of ex-combatants was at most 25 percent.[403] Furthermore, it was found that while some faced problems finding permanent employment, for the most part males integrated well back into society.[404] The unemployment and low level of income of ex-combatants is still an area of concern. However, this may be due to the environmental economic situation. Women, on the other hand, faced bigger challenges to reintegrate into society, because they were not allowed to use the skills they had acquired during their time as combatants in the labor market. Rather than being able to use their technical knowledge and physical abilities in professions like bricklaying or computer technicians, they were expected to be domestic helpers and seamstresses. Households who had to support returning female fighters did not receive sufficient government support, putting additional constraints on the families.[405] Kulatunga’s work also showed that leisure, free time and social events were highly relevant factors to make the reintegration process successful.[406]

 

Lessons Learned

A considerable area of improvement is the transitional justice process in Sri Lanka and the prosecution of those responsible for human rights abuses during the civil war. The president faces pressure to implement a transparent process.

Overall, security has greatly increased in Sri Lanka, and there has been a high level of post-war stability with no acts of terrorism since the end of the civil war in 2009.[407]

Several ex-combatants have had problems with unemployment and with setting up their own businesses, although the government had determined specific professions and geographic areas as more promising labor markets and provided training and assistance. These sectors, however, were selected intuitively rather than based on a thorough economic analysis of the labor market. Unemployment was not a problem that only or primarily affected former combatants, but rather a common problem in eastern and northeastern Sri Lanka. Rather than a lack of training and skills, it was the surrounding economic circumstances and the mismatch between the needs of the labor market and the qualification of former combatants that caused difficulties for former combatants to find employment. Therefore, it is important to accompany reintegration programs with investments into economic growth for the broader community rather than make adaptations to the reintegration program itself. New employment opportunities in the communities will have additional benefit by further strengthening community support of reintegration efforts. Furthermore, a thorough economic analysis of the needs of the labor market is necessary to ensure that former combatants have a better chance to find employment upon reinsertion. During such analysis, it is crucial to consider the medium term needs of companies, especially if economic reintegration and training efforts are carried out over several years.

The reintegration program in Sri Lanka had substantial issues finding qualified personnel that were proficient in the languages of former combatants. Finding suitable and qualified personnel for the reintegration facilities is crucial.[408]

Women, children and disabled ex-combatants seem to have experienced problems reintegrating back into society and have faced distinct issues of difficult family dynamics, alcohol problems and abuse.[409] Differentiating between different groups of beneficiaries is important. Long-term care for former combatants, especially women and disabled ex-combatants, to deal with changing family dynamics and social problems could ameliorate the situation for female and disabled beneficiaries.

 

Applicability to Boko Haram

There are several parallels between the nature of the insurgency and the potential reintegration efforts that can be drawn between Sri Lanka and Boko Haram. However, there are also some crucial differences. The Sri Lankan civil war ended with the government forces defeating the LTTE combatants, while the conflict between the Nigerian security forces and Boko Haram is still ongoing. Given the different power balance, the attitudes towards reintegration of both the affected population and the combatants might be substantially different in each case.

Despite this stark contrast, both countries share many limitations, including financial constraints. Nigeria can learn from Sri Lanka’s strategy to involve the private sector strongly in the reintegration and rehabilitation efforts to offer meaningful vocational training opportunities. One way to accomplish that could include conducting a thorough analysis of the needs of the labor market before and during the execution of the reintegration program to determine which sectors could offer employment opportunities for former combatants in the future. The Nigerian government could receive funding from private enterprises, particularly in the agriculture and manufacturing sector, for education and training programs, which would also help sensitize the population for the rehabilitation process. In northeastern Nigeria processing facilities for agricultural products offer an opportunity for growth. They would not only bring employment, but also facilitate knowledge and technology-transfer, while at the same time taking advantage of the region’s fertile agricultural environment. Some ways to attract companies could be tax incentives and tax guarantees for long-term investors. If there were to be positive spillover effects for the local population resulting from the economic reintegration, these measures would potentially be substantially more effective.

An additional parallel between the Sri Lankan conflict and the Boko Haram insurgency is the prevalence of suicide attacks and child soldiers, which complicates the conflict. When it comes to reintegrating child soldiers into the community, Sri Lanka took the approach to plan a separate program for children, that lasted ten to twelve months. Distinguishing by gender and age would be beneficial when it comes to the reintegration of former Boko Haram combatants, because it would allow the psychological and community elements of a reintegration program to be tailored to the specific groups being reintegrated. In Sri Lanka, child soldiers were taken from rural families, which contributed to an unwillingness to accept ex-combatants back in their communities. Given the trauma that the families in the communities had suffered, raising community support was crucial. As is pointed out in the discussion of Operation Safe Corridor, receiving the support of the community through the engagement of local leaders could be specifically helpful. Inviting community members to actively participate in the reintegration process as was done in Sri Lanka through public festivities and joint activities, could be an additional path to community acceptance.
