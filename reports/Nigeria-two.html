Climate Change, Violence, and Boko Haram in the Lake Chad Basin Region

Abdul-Majeed Ibrahim & Lauren Wyman

 

Abstract: Lake Chad is the largest lake in Central and West Africa, and is the source of livelihoods for roughly thirty million people in Nigeria, Chad, Cameroon, and Niger. In the last six decades, the lake has been shrinking, and presently covers only one twentieth the area it covered in the 1960s. This is due to both anthropogenic stressors as well as climate change, contributing to over-exploitation, resource scarcity, loss of livelihoods and conflict over land and water on a regional level. This fragile humanitarian situation exacerbates the risks posed by the Boko Haram militancy, as already vulnerable populations are forced to migrate from their lands. This vulnerability also contributes to strengthening Boko Haram, as stranded youth may be tempted to join out of economic necessity rather than for ideological reasons. Policies and programs to reintegrate Boko Haram fighters into society should consider the humanitarian and ecological context at play - socioeconomic stabilizers have been decimated. Resilient and self-reliant communities are needed for reintegration programs to be sustainable. Tools to strengthen community and ecosystem resilience include climate-smart agriculture, livestock insurance and agro-advisories.

 

Key Words: climate change, adaptation, desertification, livelihood loss, gender, Lake Chad, violence

 

Background and Context

Lake Chad is the largest lake in Central and West Africa, bordered by four countries – Nigeria’s northeast, Cameroon’s far north, Chad’s Lac region (western Chad) and Niger’s southeast.[23] The Lake Chad basin encompasses 966,955 km2 of floodplains, wetlands and aquifers.[24] The population in the Lake Chad region has more than doubled over the last decade and about thirty million people in the basin depend on the lake for their livelihoods.[25] Subsistence fishing, agriculture and livestock farming are the primary economic activities in the region, which is a hub for trade and commerce.

In the last six decades, the lake has been shrinking, and along with it the quality of life and source of income for the region’s population. The lake presently covers 1,350km2, only one twentieth the area it covered in the 1960s.[26] The United Nations Environmental Programme attributes 50 percent of the lake decrease in size to overuse by humans for irrigation and the rest from changing climate and rainfall patterns.[27] Historic data shows that the lake has recovered from shrinking on multiple occasions, but scientists doubt this will occur due to anthropogenic stressors (i.e. conditions resulting from human influence on the environment) and climate change.[28]

 

Impact of anthropogenic stressors and climate change

Over the last half-century, Lake Chad has been shrinking due to over-exploitation and manipulation by national governments and by local populations (see Figure 1 below). In particular, damming of source rivers like the Chari River and the Komadugu-Yobe for irrigation and fresh water have interrupted the lake’s natural flow regime. Additionally, overgrazing, land-clearing for agriculture and poor farming practices have reduced basin soil quality and water retention, thus putting undue pressure on the basin’s natural resources.[29] An over reliance on extractive practices like fishing has led to biodiversity loss in the lake and decreased system resilience. As the population living around the lake has increased rapidly over the past decade, due to normal population growth as well as forced migration, competition for the lake’s resources has made it nearly impossible for the lake to recover on its own.[30]

Climate variability, shocks and long-term changes compound the human-derived environmental degradation in the Lake Chad basin. Specifically, the Lake Chad Basin is one of the most climate-sensitive regions on the African continent. There is high inter-annual rainfall variability, and the average yearly rainfall has decreased over the last century.[31]  The region alternates between periods of high rainfall and flooding and drought, which makes agriculture and pastoral livelihoods highly vulnerable. Temperatures in the region over the last forty years have increased six degrees Celsius during the dry season.[32] This is compounded by desertification, which is encroaching in the northern part of the lake.[33] Over the course of this century, increasing temperatures and evapotranspiration of the Lake will reduce available water by up to another ten percent.[34] Climate models predict a shift in ecosystem composition around the lake, with arable lands decreasing by up to 135,000 km2 by 2099.[35]

Conflict Over Shrinking Natural Resources

Seasonal fluctuations -- recession and inundation of the floodplains -- make the lake a good habitat for fish as well as fertile ground for farming. Lake recession exposes the lake bed’s fertile soils for farming, and during inundation, nutrients are restored to the soil. Flood recession cropping is a major production system in the Lake Chad Basin, where farmers mostly cultivate sorghum and berbere peppers.[37]

The shrinking of the lake has created tensions among competing resource users - fishermen, farmers, and cattle herders. Farming activities on the exposed lake bed modifies the aquatic community in the lake.[38] Specifically, ploughing close to the lake increases siltation and eutrophication, reducing lake depth and causing fish kills from toxic algae blooms.

The modified aquatic ecosystem alters species biodiversity and the number of fish species that are able to inhabit the lake. Fish evade fishing nets by hiding under marshes and tall grasses growing in the lake due to siltation. Fishing thus becomes difficult and fishermen lose their fishing gear to marsh weeds. In response to this pressure, fishermen start to disregard national boundaries and migrate towards other national territories to fish.[39]  Some fishermen turn to farming to take advantage of the exposed fertile lakebed, which contributes to competition for farmland with traditional farmers. Adding to the mix, pastoral communities clash with farmers as traditional pasture lands have been encroached by farmers and, likewise, cattle often destroy farmland through trampling when they move to drink water from the lake. Farmers, fishers, and cattle herders migrate toward and compete for the shrinking lake’s resources without regard for the imaginary boundary lines.[40] Resource extraction has intensified as a result and crude methods are being used in resource extraction to maximize output.

 

Climate Change as a Threat Multiplier for Violent Extremism in the Region

Boko Haram has taken advantage of porous borders of the Lake Chad basin countries and expanded to Cameroon’s far north, Niger’s southeast, and Chad’s Lac region.[41] Suicide bombing and raids on communities have displaced an estimated 2.3 million people (as of November 2016) in the Lake Chad basin region, of which 1.9 million are in Nigeria’s northeast.[42] By displacing primarily agricultural populations, the insurgency exacerbates climate-driven food shortages -- affecting the fragile food and nutrition security situation of the region, where 6.3 million people were food insecure as of August 2016.[43] Boko Haram interferes with markets and transportation infrastructure, thus further complicating food shortages and access of vulnerable populations.

Communities hosting IDPs face resource competition and land overuse as the influx of people exacerbates food, shelter and livelihood needs.[44]  Forced displacement in this context strips victims of their social and physical capital and networks, undermining their ability to cope with the conflict and the changing climate.[45] 

The impacts of resource scarcity and the humanitarian crises in the region are cyclical, however, as lack of economic opportunity and the prevalence of hunger has led to many Nigerians joining Boko Haram. In fact, some foot soldiers in the Boko Haram sect have been identified as people displaced by severe drought in the regions.[46] While Boko Haram started in urban areas, hunger and natural resource competition may have contributed to young Boudouma men joining the group.[47]  See Figure 2  for a schematic on how Boko Haram contributes to, and is exacerbated by, the humanitarian and climate situation in the Lake Chad Basin.

National and Regional Environmental Policies and Management Bodies

President Buhari has made climate change a national priority for Nigeria - promoting sustainable growth while reducing carbon dioxide emissions on the international, federal, regional and local levels, across sectors. However, there are gaps in climate change adaptation coverage, particularly on the local level and in the security and defense sectors.

On an international level, Nigeria is party to the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCC) and signed the Paris Accords in September of 2016. On a federal level, Nigeria has a national bureaucratic infrastructure for environmental protection, including Nigeria’s Climate Change Policy and the national development plan, Vision 2020.[48]

On a regional level, the government of Nigeria has partnered with nine West African countries to develop sustainable regional climate change policy and research, through the West African Science Service Centre on Climate Change and Adapted Land Use (WASCAL). The Lake Chad Basin Commission (LCBC), founded in 1964, is a regional mechanism to settle natural resource disputes, promote conservation and regional development and peace and security in the region. However, the LCBC has struggled to carry out its mandate due to lack of funding and technical capacity.[49] Additionally, there is little cooperation between the four countries on lake resource management.[50]

On the national level, the National Assembly has various committees that focus on climate change, natural resources and Lake Chad. Specifically, in the Senate there are the following committees:  Climate Change; Poverty Alleviation and Social Welfare; and Water Resources; in the House, there are: Climate Change; Environment and Habitat; IDPs, Refugees and Initiatives on the North East; Lake Chad; and Water Resources. The government has recognized the need to more effectively mainstream climate change into other governmental sectors, particularly in development planning.[51]

On a local level, there are a variety of adaptation projects spearheaded by regional organizations like the Lake Chad Basin Commission, such as the organization’s Development and Climate Action Plan. However, the implementation of consistent Nigerian governmental climate change adaptation projects in conflict zones, particularly in the northeast, have fallen short due to lack of adequate infrastructure, political will and the presence of Boko Haram. While Nigerian policy recognizes the intersectionality between the current conflict with Boko Haram and natural resource clashes due to climate change, it lacks political impetus to approach defense and security issues with a climate change adaptation lens.[52]

 

Climate resilient livelihoods - toolkit

Traditional rain-fed agricultural and pastoral livelihoods are highly vulnerable to a changing climate.[53]  However, there are a variety of tools that farmers and pastoralists can adopt to reduce the impacts of climate change on their livelihoods. A few suggestions are: 

1.     Climate-smart agriculture (CSA) is a common mechanism to improve livelihood resilience to climate change by adapting agriculture to increasing rainfall variability, decreasing water availability for irrigation and higher prevalence of extreme weather events.[54] CSA sustainably increases productivity by utilizing drought-resistant and high yield crops, intercropping and evaporation prevention through cover crops.[55] In the National Agricultural Resilience Framework (NARF), Nigeria’s Federal Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development outlined a path for the country to adopting and implementing climate-smart agricultural innovations and technologies.[56]

 

2.     Index-based livestock insurance is a common tool whereby cash payments are made to cattle herders if their livestock die during drought. The International Livestock Research Institute has successfully implemented pilot projects in Kenya and it is now being done in Ethiopia.[57]

 

3.     Weather Information Services and Agro-advisories on best agronomic practices - best sowing time, soil quality management, fertilizer application, integrated pest management, crop residue management - sent via automated voice messages can help farmers adapt their agricultural livelihoods to changing rainfall patterns and cropping season. [58]  Following the model of India’s  Integrated Meteorological Advisory Service (IAAS), the Nigerian Meteorological Agency (NIMET) can provide weather forecasts and the agricultural universities in northern Nigeria can convert the weather forecast into appropriate farm-level advisories.[59] The farm-level advisories can then be disseminated through local radio stations, and telecom giants like MTN, Globacom and Airtel.


Concrete recommendations:

1.     Create cross-sectoral working groups in the National Assembly to address counter-terrorism with a climate lens. Any counter-terrorism strategy must promote a cross-sectoral approach that considers the wide-ranging impacts of a changing climate. Thus, in the National Assembly it is important to create a cross sectoral committee to tackle counter-terrorism in the northeast. One possibility is to expand the scope of the current committee on IDPs, Refugees and Initiatives on North East in the National Assembly to involve individuals from the Climate Change, Lake Chad and the Environment committees.

 

2.    Support environmental conservation efforts through the Lake Chad Development and Climate Resilience Action Plan. It is important to work with the Lake Chad Basin Commission to implement its Development and Climate Action Plan, to support conservation and livelihoods on the basin/watershed level and to build community resilience through the provision of technical assistance on best farming, fishing and grazing practices.

 

3.    Apply a climate change lens to reintegration programs. The government of Nigeria should push to further incorporate the climate change lens into conflict and peacebuilding policies and actions in northeastern Nigeria, building off of the National Agricultural Resilience Framework. Failing to consider the impact of climate change on livelihoods when demobilizing Boko Haram fighters and providing them with vocational training - as through the current Operation Safe Corridor model - may drive them back into the insurgency. Developing climate resilient livelihoods that utilize tools such as climate-smart agriculture and livestock insurance in the northeast, which is the most impacted region from climate change and desertification, is vital to ensure a stable, sustainable peace.
